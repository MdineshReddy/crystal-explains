Introduction

Geography, as the name suggest is the study of earth ("geo" - greek for earth and "graphy" - greek for study). In geography, we try to find patterns and connections between places and seemingly unrelated processes.

Geography helps us answer bigger questions like "what's the story of the Earth?", "how do humans change their environments?", and "why, the earth's surface is not the same everywhere?"

Traditionally, geography is split into two tightly intertwined parts.

  • Physical geography is concerned with the processes and phenomena of the physical world both above and below the Earth’s surface.
  • Human geography is concerned with the study of people and their communities, cultures, economies, and interactions with the environment.

Physical geography

Our planet is a dynamic place. The earth's landscape isn’t permanent. It’s changed as continents have shifted and humans have interacted with the environment. And it will continue to change.

Some changes happen slowly - we’d have to watch for millions of years to see the continents drift or the Rocky Mountains grow. But a storm or avalanche changes the landscape in the blink of an eye. Both types of change have tremendous influence over our lives, from small decisions like what we’ll wear today to bigger choices like where we choose to live.

All of these changes, above and below the Earth's surface, are part of physical geography. Physical geography is all about recognizing the characteristics of the environment and the processes that create, modify and destroy those environments.

In Physical Geography, we study the earth by dividing it into four distinct systems:

  • Atmosphere is the layers of air surrounding Earth that give us clouds, weather, the ozone layer, and the air we breathe.
  • Hydrosphere is all the water on, below, or above the planet's surface. So all the water in the soils, under the ground, in oceans, lakes, ice caps, and streams, as well as in plants, animals, our bodies, and all the water molecules in the atmosphere.
  • Lithosphere, is the rocky outermost layer of Earth. The rocks of the lithosphere form our continents and line the bottom of Earth’s oceans.
  • Biosphere: is the parts of Earth where all the above three meet in right proportions so that the life can exist.

Earth Systems

Geosphere and its relation with Lithosphere

The term 'Geosphere' has several conflicting definitions:

  • It is used as a collective name for the lithosphere, the hydrosphere, the cryosphere (ice), and the atmosphere.
  • In Aristotelian physics, the term was believed to explain the motions of the four terrestrial elements: Earth, Water, Air, and Fire.
  • In modern Earth system science, geosphere refers to the solid parts of the Earth; it is used along with atmosphere, hydrosphere, and biosphere to describe the systems of the Earth. In that context, sometimes the term lithosphere is used instead of geosphere or solid Earth. The lithosphere, however, only refers to the uppermost layers of the solid Earth (oceanic and continental crustal rocks and uppermost mantle).

All four of the above systems makeup the "Geo-Ecosphere". By looking at the interactions between all the Earth systems we can identify what helps an ecosystem thrive.

Different field of Physical Geography

  • Geomorphology is the study of landforms and processes that shape them. Here we try and understand how the earth formed and evolved overtime.
  • Hydrology is the study of the movement, distribution and quality of water on earth.
  • Climatology is the study of climate which is scientifically defined as the weather conditions averaged over a period of time.
  • Pedology is the study of soil types and how they form.
  • Biogeography is the study of relationships between organisms with their environment.
