Cyclones

Cyclones

A cyclone is a large scale air mass that rotates around a strong center of low atmospheric pressure, counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere as viewed from above (opposite to an anticyclone). Cyclones are of two types:

  • Extratropical cyclones
  • Tropical cyclones

Extratropical cyclones

*To understand Extratropical cyclone, you need to know about fronts. You can read about them Here

The systems developing in the mid and high latitude, beyond the tropics are called the mid-latitude or extra tropical cyclones. The passage of front causes abrupt changes in the weather conditions over the area in the middle and high latitudes.

Extra tropical cyclones form along the polar front. Initially, the front is stationary. In the northern hemisphere, warm air blows from the south and cold air from the north of the front. When the pressure drops along the front, the warm air moves northwards and the cold air move towards south, setting in motion an anticlockwise cyclonic circulation.

The cyclonic circulation leads to a well developed extra tropical cyclone, with a warm front and a cold front. The warm air glides over the cold air and a sequence of clouds appear over the sky ahead of the warm front and cause precipitation. The cold front approaches the warm air from behind and pushes the warm air up. As a result, cumulus clouds develop along the cold front. The cold front moves faster than the warm front ultimately overtaking the warm front. The warm air is completely lifted up and the front is occluded and the cyclone dissipates.

The extra-topical cyclones are intensified because of the presence of polar front jet stream around the 60° latitude which travels up to 450 kilometers per hour, wrapping all around the planet.

Tropical Cyclones

Tropical cyclones are violent storms that originate over warm tropical oceans and move over to the coastal areas bringing about large scale destruction caused by violent winds, very heavy rainfall and storm surges. This is one of the most devastating natural calamities.

They are known as Cyclones in the Indian Ocean, Hurricanes in the Atlantic, Typhoons in the Western Pacific and South China Sea, and Willy-willies in the Western Australia. Tropical cyclones are typically between 100 and 2,000 km in diameter. They move slowly at a speed of about 300 to 500 kilometers per day.

Formation

Since the tropical regions recieve maximum insolation, the air there heats up and rises which forms a low pressure region. The air from surrounding high-pressure region quickly move towards this low pressure region. It too gets heated up, rises upto troposphere, condense and form cumulonimbus clouds.

While this process goes on, another important phenomena comes into picture called Coriolis force. It is the force generated due to variations in rotational speeds of earth at equator and poles. It acts as a deflective force to the wind direction. Because of the Coriolis force, all winds are deflected to the right in the Northern Hemisphere while they are deflected to the left in the southern Hemisphere with respect to the rotating earth while moving from high pressure to low pressure area.

At the center of every cyclone or hurricane lies an area of low pressure (with temperature higher than 27° C) which attracts the cool air from surrounding high pressure area. This central low-pressure region is called the eye of the storm. Around the eye is the eye wall, where there is a strong spiralling ascent of air to greater height reaching the tropopause. The wind reaches maximum velocity in this region, reaching as high as 250 km per hour. Torrential rain occurs here. From the eye wall rain bands may radiate and trains of cumulus and cumulonimbus clouds may drift into the outer region.

Eye of a Cyclone

Diagram of a tropical cyclone in the Northern hemisphere

Due to Coriolis Effect, in the northern hemisphere the cool air rushing towards the center of the storm is deflected slightly to the right. But this cool air keeps getting attracted towards the center because that's where the warm low-pressure zone is. This creates a spin in a counter-clockwise direction. Just the opposite happens in the southern hemisphere, due to the Coriolis effect, cool air gets deflected to the left and as a result the spinning of this cyclone is in a clockwise direction.

Cyclone occurs over the ocean surface and coastal region because there is a continuous supply of moisture from the ocean or sea. When it reaches the land, the moisture supply is cut off and the storm dissipates. The place where tropical cyclone crosses the coast is called the landfall of the cyclone.

Differences between Tropical and Extra Tropical Cyclones

Tropical Cyclone Extra-Tropical Cyclone
The tropical cyclones do not have a clear frontal system. The extra tropical cyclones have a clear frontal system.
Confined to 10-30 degree North and South of equator. Confined to 35-65 degree North and South of equator.
They originate only over the seas and on reaching the land they dissipate. They cover a larger area and can originate over the land and sea.
Affects a smaller area as compared to the extra-tropical cyclone. Affects much larger area as compared to the tropical cyclone.
Wind velocity is much higher and it is more destructive. The Wind velocity is comparatively lower.
They move from east to west. They move from west to east.

Thunderstorms and Tornadoes

They are of short duration, occurring over a small area but are violent. Thunderstorms are caused by intense convection on moist hot days. A thunderstorm is a well-grown cumulonimbus cloud producing thunder and lightening. Sometime they populate the rainbands of tropical cyclones. When the clouds extend to heights where sub-zero temperature prevails, hails are formed and they come down as hailstorm. If there is insufficient moisture, a thunderstorm can generate dust-storms. A thunderstorm is characterised by intense updraft of rising warm air, which causes the clouds to grow bigger and rise to greater height. This causes precipitation. Later, downdraft brings down to earth the cool air and the rain.

From severe thunderstorms sometimes spiralling wind descends with great force, with very low pressure at the centre, causing massive destruction on its way. Such a phenomenon is called a tornado (or twisters). Tornadoes generally occur in middle latitudes. The tornado over the sea is called water spouts.

Anti-Cyclone

As the name suggest, Anti-Cyclones are exactly opposite to the Cyclones. In an anti-cyclone, a large-scale circulation of winds around a central region of high pressure, clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise in the Southern Hemisphere as viewed from above, occurs.

Effects of surface-based anticyclones include clearing skies as well as cooler, drier air. Fog can also form overnight within a region of higher pressure.
