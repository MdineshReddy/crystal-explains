The Universe

Universe is the totality of everything. From the tiniest particles, to the largest galaxies, to the very existence of space, time, and life. The region of universe that can be observed from Earth or its space-based telescopes and exploratory probes is called the Observable universe. The study of the origin and development of the Universe is called Cosmology.

Units of distance in Universe

Unit Description
Light Year It is the distance that light can travel in 1 year. It is equal to almost 9.46 * 1012 km.
Astronomical Unit (AU) It is the average distance between the sun and the earth. 1 AU = 150 million km.
Parsec 1 Parsec = 3.26 light years= 206,000 AU.
* Light Speed = 3 lakh km/s (or) 1.86 lakh miles/s.

The Observable Universe comprises of about 2 trillion galaxies. The galaxies are made up of billions of stars held together by the force of gravity and these stars account for most of the masses of the galaxy.

Our own galaxy is called the Milky Way (Akash Ganga) and it contains more than 400 billion stars. One of these stars happens to be our Sun. Planets and other objects revolve around the sun and makeup the solar system with the sun at the center. Andromeda is our nearest galaxy. Structurally, galaxies are found in four forms:

  • Spiral Galaxies have a flat, spinning disk with a central bulge called nucleus surrounded by spiral arms. Milky Way and Andromeda are the examples.
  • Spiral Barred Galaxies: are the ones that have their arms spiral out from a straight bar of stars instead of coming from a nucleus.
  • Elliptical Galaxies have an elongated oval shape and lack any nucleus or straight bar at the center.
  • Irregular galaxies are the galaxies with no definite shape.

History of the center of the Universe

Starting from ancient greece and egypt to the middle ages, the Earth was believed to be the center of the Universe and the Sun and other heavenly bodies revolved around it. This theory is called Geocentric Theory. Prominent proponents of this theory were two influential Greek philosophers of 4th century BC, Plato and his student Aristotle and later in 2nd century AD, Ptolemy in Egypt.

In 16th century AD, Nicolaus Copernicus propounded the theory that the Sun not the Earth is the center of the Universe. This theory is called Geocentric Theory.

In the early-20th century, the discovery of other galaxies and the development of the Big Bang theory led to theorise that the Universe lacks a central point and is expanding at all points.

Origin of the Universe

The origin of the universe is the origin of everything. Multiple scientific theories and creation myths from around the world have tried to explain its mysterious genesis. However, the most widely accepted explanation is the Big Bang theory based on an idea proposed by an astronomer named Georges Lemaitre.

In 1927, Lemaitre believed that a very long time ago, the universe started as just a single point (often called singularity) and it stretched and expanded to get as big as it is now, and that it could keep on stretching.

Just two years later, an astronomer named Edwin Hubble noticed that other galaxies were moving away from us and the farthest galaxies were moving faster than the ones close to us. This meant that the universe was still expanding, just like Lemaitre thought. If things were moving apart, it meant that long ago, everything had been close together.

The Big Bang Theory

The Big Bang theory states that the universe began as a hot and infinitely dense point (only a few millimeters wide). About 13.7 billion years ago this tiny singularity violently exploded. And it is from this explosion (or bang), that all matter, energy, space, and time were created.

There are two major stages in the evolution of the Universe called the radiation and matter eras. Each era is made of smaller stages call epochs.

Radiation Era (first 20 minutes after big bang)

This era started right after the Big Bang and is dominated by presence of radiation from the explosion. It includes the following epochs:

  • Planck Epoch (started right after the Big Bang) is the earliest epoch where no matter existed in the universe. Only energy and the ancestor to the four forces of nature called the superforce existed.

    • Four forces of nature are:

    • Gravitational Force (Gravity for short)- Weakest force but has long range.
    • Weak Nuclear Force - next weakest and also short range.
    • Electromagnetic Force - Stronger with long range.
    • Strong Nuclear Force - Strongest but short range.
  • Grand Unification Epoch (began 10−43 seconds after big bang): in which the gravitational force separated from the other forces. This epoch is named after the three remaining unified forces of nature.
  • Inflationary epoch (began 10-37 seconds after big bang): during which the universe rapidly expanded.
  • Electroweak epoch (began 10-36 seconds after big bang) in which strong nuclear force separates from the other forces, with only the electromagnetic force and weak nuclear force remaining unified.
  • Quark epoch (began 10-12 seconds after big bang): in which the last two forces, electromagnetic and weak nuclear, finally split off. All of the universe's ingredients were present, however, the universe was still too hot and dense for subatomic particles to form.
  • Hadron epoch (began 10-6 seconds after big bang): in which the universe cooled down enough for quarks to bind together and form hadrons i.e., protons and neutrons (nucleons), the building blocks of atomic nuclei.
  • Lepton and nuclear epochs (between 1 second and 20 minutes after big bang): are the radiation era's last two stages. The protons and neutrons fused and created nuclei. And in doing so, they created the first chemical element in the universe, helium.

Matter Era (spans billions of years and includes the present day)

The universe's new ability to form elements, the building blocks of matter, queued the matter era. It includes the following epochs:

  • Atomic Epoch: during which the universe's temperature cooled down enough for electrons to attach to nuclei for the first time. This process called recombination, helped create the universe's second element, hydrogen.
  • Galactic Epoch: The hydrogen, along with helium atoms, dotted the universe with atomic clouds called nebula. Within the clouds, small pockets of gas may have had enough gravity to cause atoms to collect. These clusters of atoms, formed during the galactic epoch, became the seedlings of galaxies.
  • Stellar Epoch: Nestled inside those galaxies, localised clumps of gas continue to grow into even denser gaseous bodies, giving rise to formation of stars. And in doing so, they queued the latest and current stage of the universe's development, the stellar epoch.

The formation of stars then caused a tremendous ripple effect and helped shape the universe as we know it. Heat within the stars caused the conversion of helium and hydrogen into almost all the remaining elements in the universe. In turn, those elements became the building blocks for planets, moons, life, everything we see today.

Stars

Stars are heavenly bodies made up of hot burning gases (which produces heat and light) held together by their own gravity. The most prominent stars are grouped into patterns called constellations. The most recognizable constellation is the Big Dipper (plough shaped) which is known as Saptarishi (seven sages) Mandala.

Fun Facts

  • Brightest star outside the Solar System is Sirius also called as dog star.
  • Closest star to our solar system is Proxima Centauri (4.2 light years away) followed by Alpha Centauri (4.3 light years away) and Bernard's Star (5.9 light years away).
  • A star's color indicates the temperature of its surface. Blue color denotes maximum temperature. White, Yellow, Orange and Red Stars have sequentially declining range of temperatures.
  • A pole star or polar star is a star, preferably bright, nearly aligned with the axis of the earth and as a result they do not appear to move from east to west but appears fixed. Hence they are very useful for navigational purposes.
    • Polaris (Dhruva nakshatra) is aligned approximately with earth's northern axis, as a result is considered northern polestar.
    • Polaris Australis (Sigma Octantis) is aligned with earth's southern axis but it is barely visible on a clear night, making it unusable for navigational purposes.

Life Cycle of a Star

Giant Gas Cloud: A Star originates from a large cloud of dust and gas called nebuli. The temperature in the cloud is low enough for the synthesis of molecules.

Protostar: Nebuli condenses into a huge globule of gas and dust and contracts under its own gravity. The region of condensing matter will begin to heat up and starts to glow forming Protostars.

T-Tauri Phase: A T-Tauri star begins when materials stop falling into the protostar. The mean temperature of the T-Tauri Star isn't enough to support nuclear fusion at its core.

Main Sequence Star: The main sequence phase is the stage in development where the core temperature reaches the point for the fusion to commence. At this temperature, nuclear reactions in which hydrogen fuses to form helium can start (nuclear fusion). This reaction is exothermic, the star begins to release energy, stopping it from contracting even more and causes it to shine.

What happens to a star during the rest of its life depends on how massive the star is at its birth:

For less massive stars such as the Sun:

A star like the sun is in a delicate balance between gravity which wants to make the star collapse in on itself and the pressure (that comes from the energy produced by the fusion reactions at its core) that pushes outwards.

At some point in the future, the hydrogen runs out. At that point, the core of the star will start to collapse under its own weight. It gets denser and hotter until the point where they start to use the helium atoms themselves as the fuel for the fusion, pushing helium atoms together, making carbon and oxygen, the next heavier elements on the periodic table. As the star begins to fuse helium, it creates more energy and that causes the outer layers of the star to expand.

One day, our sun will grow so large, it will swallow up the inner planets of the solar system out as far as the Earth. It will become a red giant. For the sun, this will be the beginning of the end. What happens is that the outer layers of the star get farther and farther from the middle, the force of gravity that they feel is getting weaker. Eventually, the star loses hold of its outer atmosphere, which then drifts off out into space and expands to become a planetary nebula.

Once the outer layers have drifted away, all that is left of the star is its core. A white dwarf star is the dead, remnant core of a star like the sun at the end of its life. It's something that might weight as much as 1/2 the mass ofthe sun but it's only about the size of the Earth, so it's an incredibly dense object. It's dead, there's no nuclear fusion going on any more, it‘s incredibly hot but then over millions of years, it will gradually cool down to become a black dwarf.

White Dwarf and Planetary Nebula

For stars that are much more massive than the Sun:

Stars that are much more massive than the sun lead very different lives. They are able to fuse heavier and heavier elements inside their core. The star gets bigger and bigger some grow up to 1000 times the size of our sun until it has fused elements all the way up to iron (which is considered nuclear ash).

Once an iron core is formed, there's no more energy that can be got from fusion. The core Collapses, the rest of the star starts to Collapse in after it. And then suddenly, here's a huge shock wave and in just a second: BANG! The outer parts of the star are blasted off in to space in a huge super-nova explosion!

These super-nova explosions are so powerful that when one of these stars explodes it can actually outshine the whole galaxy of which it is a part. For these super giant stars all that is left is a super dense core known as a neutron star, an object that can have a mass greater than our sun but be less than 20 kilometers across.

For the Most Massive Stars:

For the most massive stars of all, when the core collapses the gravity is so strong, it becomes a Black Hole from which even light cannot escape.

Chandrasekhar limit

The Chandrasekhar limit is the maximum mass of a stable white dwarf star. The currently accepted value of the Chandrasekhar limit is about 1.4 times the mass of the sun.

If the mass of a star is below the Chandrasekhar limit, it resist the complete collapse of the star and end up as a white dwarf. If the mass is greater than the limit, then the star is subject to further gravitational collapse, evolving into a different type of stellar remnant, such as a neutron star or black hole.
