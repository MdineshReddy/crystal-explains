Earthquakes and Volcanoes

Earthquakes and Volcanoes are the two frequently occuring natural hazards with tremendous destructive potential. They are both caused by the sudden and Catastropic Endogenic Forces.

Earthquakes

An earthquake is the shaking of the surface of the Earth, resulting from a sudden release of energy in the Earth's lithosphere that creates seismic waves.

Majority of the earthquakes takes place in three regions:

  • The Mid-Atlantic Ridge, an underwater line that runs down the Atlantic Ocean.
  • Alpide belt which stretches from the Mediterranean to Southeast Asia.
  • Circum-Pacific Belt (or Pacific Ring of Fire) which traces along the edges of the Pacific Ocean.

Causes

Earthquakes are the result of pressure caused by extreme stress in the Earth's crust. That stress can be caused by volcanic activity or even man-made activities in certain areas.

However most earthquake inducing stress is caused by the movement of tectonic plates. (read more on plate tectonics). Tectonic Plates are constantly moving either against (convergent), away (divergent), along (transform) or underneath each other (subduction).

But sometimes Plate edges may catch and stick. The plates however continue to move or at least attempt to move. Energy from this attempted movement builds around the edges sticking point, creating immense pressure until the edges are forced to let go and the plates slip. This causes a sudden and powerful release of energy. So powerful, that it breaks the Earth's crust. This fracturing emits shockwaves through the ground and causes intense vibrations or quakes.

The point inside the earth, where the energy is released is called the focus or hypocentre. The energy waves travelling in different directions reach the surface. The point on the surface, nearest i.e., directly above the focus, is called Epicentre.

Richter Scale vs Mercalli Scale vs Moment Magnitude Scale

  • The intensity of an earthquake is measured by Mercalli scale. It is measured by observation of the effects on earth’s surface, humans, objects and man-made structures. Its scale ranges from I (not felt) to XII (total destruction).
  • The magnitude of earthquake is given by Richter scale. It is given by measuring the seismic waves that cause the earthquake using a Seismograph. Its scale range from 2.0 to 10.0+ (never recorded).
  • The Mercalli scale is linear. The Richter scale is logarithmic meaning a magnitude 5 earthquake is ten times as intense as a magnitude 4 earthquake.

Moment Magnitude Scale is similar to Richter Scale, in that it is also logarithmic. But Moment Magnitude Scale has no upper limit and can be applied globally to measure quakes of the highest magnitudes.

The largest recorded earthquake known as Valdivia earthquake, occurred near Bolivia Chile in 1960 nestled within the circum-pacific belt. Measuring 9.4–9.6 on the moment magnitude scale, in addition to causing devastating tremors on land, it also generated a deadly tsunami reaching up to 80 feet high. The tsunami raced across the Pacific Ocean hitting faraway countries like the Philippines and Japan.

Types of Earthquake or Seismic Waves

Earthquake or Seismic waves are the energy waves generated during earthquakes. They travel through the earth in all directions from the focus and are recorded on Seismographs. They are broadly divided into two types: body waves and surface waves.

Body Waves : Traveling through the interior of the earth, body waves arrive before the surface waves. These waves are of a higher frequency than surface waves. They are again classified into following types:

  • P waves or Primary waves are the fastest body waves and the first to 'arrive' at a seismic station. They can move through solid rock and fluids, like water or the liquid layers of the earth. It pushes and pulls the rock as it moves and hence P Waves are also called compressional waves. Subjected to a P wave, particles move in the same direction that the wave is moving in, which is the direction that the energy is traveling in, and is sometimes called the 'direction of wave propagation'.
  • S waves or secondary waves are the second wave you feel in an earthquake. An S wave is slower than a P wave and can only move through solid rock, not through any liquid medium. It is this property of S waves that led seismologists to conclude that the Earth's outer core is a liquid. S waves move rock particles up and down, or side-to-side. Subjected to a S wave, particles move in the perpendicular to the direction that the wave is traveling in (the direction of wave propagation).

Surface Waves: are of a lower frequency than body waves, and are easily distinguished on a seismograph as a result. Though they arrive after body waves, it is surface waves that are almost enitrely responsible for the damage and destruction associated with earthquakes. They are classified as following:

  • Love Waves: It's the fastest surface wave and moves the ground from side-to-side. Confined to the surface of the crust, Love waves produce entirely horizontal motion.
  • Rayleigh Waves: rolls along the ground just like a wave rolls across a lake or an ocean. Because it rolls, it moves the ground up and down, and side-to-side in the same direction that the wave is moving. Most of the shaking felt from an earthquake is due to the Rayleigh wave, which can be much larger than the other waves.

Shadow Zone

Earthquake waves get recorded in seismographs located at far off locations. However, there exist some specific areas where the waves are not reported. Such a zone is called the ‘shadow zone’. The study of different events reveals that for each earthquake, there exists an altogether different shadow zone. It was observed that seismographs,

  • located within 105° from the epicentre, recorded the arrival of both P and S-waves.
  • between 105° and 145° from epicentre both the waves are not recorded.
  • beyond 145° from epicentre, only P-Waves are recorded.

Thus, The shadow zone of S-wave is much larger than that of the P-waves.

Volcanoes

A volcano is an opening on the surface of the Earth that allows hot lava, volcanic ash, and gases to escape from a magma chamber below the surface.

Volcanoes are scattered across the planet. Most volcanoes whether on land or underwater are located where tectonic plates meet. The Pacific Ring of Fire, a path that traces the boundaries between several tectonic plates around the Pacific Ocean contains about 75% of the planets volcanoes.

Volcanoes occur at both diverging or converging plate boundaries. For example, a mid-oceanic ridge, such as the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, has volcanoes caused by divergent tectonic plates whereas the Pacific Ring of Fire has volcanoes caused by convergent tectonic plates. Volcanoes are usually not created where two tectonic plates slide past one another (transform boundary).

Volcanic Eruption

All Active volcanoes emit gas and molten rock. The journey of these emissions begins deep underground in the Earth's core. The core which can burn as hot as the surface of the Sun, transfers its heat to the surrounding rocky mantle.

In doing so, the heat melts some of the rock in the mantle. This molten rock or magma is lighter than the surrounding solid rock layer. So it rises through the mantle. The magma then escapes through vents in the Earth's crust, causing volcanic eruptions.

Now above-ground this magma is referred to as lava and it can reach temperatures of over 1000 degree C. In addition to lava, volcanoes may erupt with searing hot gas formed in the mantle in a phenomenon called pyroclastic flow. This gas combined with hot ash can race down the sides of a volcano as fast as 100 miles per hour burning everything in its path.

Classification Of Volcanoes

Based on their shape and size, volcanoes are mainly classified as:

  • Strato-Volcanoes which often appear as tall steep mountains. Ex: Mount Fuji in Japan, Mayon Volcano in the Philippines, and Mount Vesuvius and Stromboli in Italy.
  • Shield Volcanoes: which are flatter and dome shaped. Ex: Mauna Loa in Hawaii, USA.
  • Calderas: which are large depressions in the ground. Ex: Mount Bromo in Indonesia.
  • Mid Ocean Ridges: which are underwater chains of volcanic mountains.

On the basis of history of their Eruptions, volcanoes are classified as:

  • Acitve Volcanoes that errupt frequently or those that have erupted in recent times. Ex: Kīlauea in Hawaii, Mt. Etna and Stromboli in Italy and volcanoes in Pacific "Ring of Fire".
  • Dormant Volcanoes: that have been known to erupt in past and show signs of possible eruptions in future. Ex: Mt. Kilimanjaro in Tanzania and Narcondam islands in Andaman.
  • Extinct Volcano: that have not erupted in the past 10,000 years. They no longer have lava supply.

While volcanoes are some of the most destructive forces of nature, they are also useful. Volcanic ash provides nutrients to nearby soil making the land fertile and lava when it cools hardens into rock and creates new landforms.
