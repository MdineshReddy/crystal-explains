The Solar System

Our solar system comprises of the Sun, 8 planets, their satellites and other non-stellar objects. It is located in the Milky Way's Orian Spur (or Orian Arm).

The Sun is at the center of the solar system and all the planets revolve around it in an elliptical orbit.

Note: Astronomical Unit (AU) is the average distance between the sun and the earth. 1 AU = 150 million KM.

Components of the Solar System

Solar System

Our Solar System consists of:

  • The Sun, eight planets and their respective satellites (moons).
  • The Dwarf planets which are celestial objects that are too small to be considered a planet but too large to fall under smaller categories. Examples: Pluto, Makemake and Haumea orbits in Kuiper Belt, Ceres orbits in asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, and Eris is the farthest with 68 astronomical units away from the Sun.
  • Interstellar debris such as asteroids, meteoroids, comets:
    • Asteroids/Planetoids are the rocky and airless leftovers from the formation of planets in our solar system. They mostly orbit our sun in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter and range from the size of cars to dwarf planets. Ceres is the only asteroid large enough to be rounded by its own gravity and it is so large that it received a promotion to the status of a dwarf planet in 2006, the same controversial distinction given to Pluto.
    • Comets: are dirty space snowballs of mostly ice and dust that formed during the birth of the solar system 4.6 billion years ago. Most comets have stable orbits in the outer reaches of the solar system past the planet Neptune. They originate from either the Kuiper belt or the Oort Cloud.
    • Meteoroids, Meteors, Meteorites: Meteoroids are tiny asteroids or the broken-off crumbs of comets and sometimes planets. They range in size from a grain of sand to boulders 3 feet (1 meter) wide. When meteoroids collide with a planet's atmosphere, they are called meteors (also known as Shooting stars). If those meteors survive the atmosphere and hit the planet's surface, their remains are called meteorites.
  • Our solar system extends much farther than the eight planets that orbit the Sun. It also includes the Kuiper Belt that lies past Neptune's orbit. This is a sparsely occupied ring of icy bodies, almost all smaller than the most popular Kuiper Belt Object, dwarf planet Pluto.
  • Beyond the fringes of the Kuiper belt is the Oort Cloud. Unlike the orbits of the planets and the Kuiper Belt, which lie mostly in the same flat disk around the Sun, the Oort Cloud is believed to be a giant spherical shell surrounding the rest of the solar system. The Oort Cloud is the boundary of the Sun's gravitational inﬂuence, where orbiting objects can turn around and return closer to our Sun.

Formation and Structure

Formation

Our solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a dense cloud of interstellar gas and dust that collapsed, possibly due to the shockwave of a nearby exploding star (supernova). When this dust cloud collapsed, it formed a solar nebula — a spinning, swirling disk of material.

At the center, gravity pulled more and more material in, Eventually the pressure in the core was so great that hydrogen atoms began to combine and form helium, releasing a tremendous amount of energy with that our Sun was born, and it eventually amassed more than 99.8 percent of the available matter in our solar system.

Matter farther out in disk was also clumping together. These clumps smashed into one another, forming larger and larger objects. Some of them grew big enough for their gravity to shape them into spheres, becoming planets, dwart planets and large moons. In other cases, planets did not form, for example, the asteroid belt is made of bits and pieces of the early solar system that could never quite come together into a planet. Other smaller leftover pieces became asteroids, comets, meteoroids, and small, irregular moons.

Structure

The order and arrangement of the planets and other bodies in our solar system is due to the way the solar system formed. Nearest the Sun, only rocky material could withstand the heat when the solar system was young. For this reason, the first four planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars—are terrestrial planets. They're small with solid, rocky surfaces.

Meanwhile ice, liquid or gas settled in the outer regions of the young solar system. Gravity pulled these materials together, and that is where we find Jovian Planets which comprises of gas giants Jupiter and Saturn and ice giants Uranus and Neptune.

Terrestrial Planets Jovian Planets
They are called Inner Planets as they are nearer to the sun. They are relatively small. They are called Outer Planets as they are far away from the sun. They are immense.
They have very few natural satellites (moons) or no satellites. They don't have ring systems. They have a large number of natural satellites. All of them sport ring systems.
They are primarily made of rocky material. Their surfaces are solid. They have a core of molten rocks. The gas giants are predominantly made of helium and hydrogen, and the ice giants also contain rock, ice, and a liquid mixture of water, methane, and ammonia. They have no solid surface.
They move faster and have a shorter period of revolution. They move rather slowly and have a longer period of revolution.

Moons

There are more than 200 known moons in our solar system and several more awaiting confirmations of discovery. Of the eight planets, Mercury and Venus are the only ones with no moons. The giant planets grab the most moons. Pluto, smaller than our own moon, has ﬁve moons in its orbit, including the Charon, a moon so large it makes Pluto wobble. Even tiny asteroids can have moons. In 2017, scientists found asteroid 3122 Florence had two tiny moons.

The Sun

The Sun is the largest object within our solar system, comprising 99.8% of the system’s mass. It terms of atom count, the sun is about 91% hydrogen (the sun‘s fuel), 8.9 percent helium and 0.1 percent heavier elements such as carhon and nitrogen. Due to the sun's extreme temperatures these elements stay in a gas like phase called plasma.

It takes 225 million years to complete one circle (revolution) around the center of the Milky Way. This is known as a Cosmic year.

Its ﬁery nature along with a tremendous gravitational pull and an extensive magnetic ﬁeld helped it to become the heart of our solar system.

Layers: The sun can be divided into six layers: the corona, the chromosphere, the Photosphere, the Convective Zone, the Radiative Zone and the Core.

Layers of the Sun

It has a surface temperature of about 6000oC. In the core temperatures reaching at least 15 million oC, combined with the sun's powerful gravity fuse together hydrogen molecules to create helium. This thermonuclear fusion, releases an enormous amount of energy in the form of radiation, electricity, solarwind and as we experience on earth, life-giving heat and light.

Gravitation Pull

Due to its immense size, the Sun has a great pull on the fabric of space, creating a gravitational force that causes nearby planetary bodies to be drawn toward it. Without sun, all the celestial bodies in our solar system would drift off into space. The Oort Cloud is regarded as the end of Sun's gravitational pull.

Magnetic Field or the heliosphere

Another critical property of the Sun is its magnetic ﬁeld which encapsulates the entire solarsystem. Called the heliosphere or solar wind, this force field protects the planets from harmful cosmic radiation. It's caused by the sun's plasma pushing electrically charged particles toward the sun's poles. This process turns the Sun into a giant magnet.

Although the sun's magnetic field is invisible to the naked eye, its effects are noticeable. On the sun's surface are dark areas called sunspots, which marked strong pockets of magnetism. On Earth and on some of the other planets the sun's magnetic field interacts with their atmospheres resulting in beautiful Auroras.

The solar winds extends far beyond even the region of Pluto, until it encounters the termination shock, where its motion slows abruptly due to the outside pressure of the interstellar medium. Beyond the shock lies the heliosheath, a broad transitional region between the inner heliosphere and the external environment. The outermost edge of the heliosphere is called the heliopause.

Two NASA spacecraft, launched in 1977, have crossed the termination shock and the heliopause:

  • Voyager 1 crossed termination shock in 2004 and heliopause in 2012 and
  • Voyager 2 crossed termination shock in 2007 and heliopause in 2018.

But it will be many thousands of years before the two Voyagers exit the Oort Cloud.

Heliosphere

Despite its size, the Sun will not last forever. In about 6.5 billion years it will run out of its hydrogen fuel, expand to envelop Mercury, Venus and even earth and then collapse into a small star known as a white dwarf . In The meantime, the Sun will continue to play a critical role in the system that bears its name.

The Planets

There are eight planets in the solar system that revolve around the Sun in an elliptical orbit.

The sequence of planets according to their distance from the Sun is Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune (Useful Mnemonic: My Very Efficient Mother Just Served Us Nuts).

The sequence of planets according to their size (in descending order) is Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Earth, Venus, Mars and Mercury.

Since the planets revolve around the sun in ellipitical orbit, their distance to the sun varies depending on where they are in their orbits. When they get closest to the Sun, it's called perihelion, and when it's farthest away, it's called aphelion.

Mercury

Mercury

  • Named after roman god of speed Mercurius (messenger of the gods), Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and nearest to the Sun, but it’s not the hottest planet (Venus is hotter). Mercury is only slightly larger than Earth's Moon.
  • Like Earth, Mercury is a terrestrial planet with three main layers, a core, a mantle, and a crust. But Mercury's crust has no tectonic plates. Also, its iron core is enormous by comparison, making up 85% of its radius, while Earth's inner and outer core account for just 55%.
  • Due to its proximity to the sun:
    • mercury is the fastest planet, going around the Sun every 88 Earth days.
    • mercury has no atmosphere apart from a very thin exosphere, which is traditionally the outermost layer of a planet's atmosphere.
    • the lack of atmosphere and close proximity to the sun also makes Mercury a planet of extreme temperatures. The surface temperature can can reach highs of 430°C (800°F) during the daytime, and fall to -180°C (-290°F) at night.
    • mercury is difficult to observe because it's fleeting and so close to the sun.
  • It has no satellites and has a cratered surface, much like the moon. It takes 58.65 Earth days, to revolved around itself.
  • So far, it's only been visited by two spacecraft, NASA's Mariner 10 and Messenger. Those missions gave us much of what we know today,

Venus

Venus

(Without clouds)

Venus

(With clouds)

  • Named after the ancient Roman goddess of love and beauty, Venus is the second planet from the Sun and our closest planetary neighbor.
  • It is known as Earth's Twin since its about the same size as Earth, (slightly smaller). It's structure is also nearly identical, with an iron core, a hot mantle, and a rocky crust. The crust of Venus, however, is dotted with thousands of volcanoes. Also, it has no satellites.
  • Venus is unusual because it spins the opposite direction of Earth and most other planets. It is the slowest rotating planet which takes about 243 Earth days to spin around just once. Because it's so close to the sun, a year goes by fast. It takes 225 Earth days for Venus to go all the way around the sun.
  • Its layered atmosphere is so thick, that it creates a surface pressure so high that it would feel like you were 1 mile (1.6 kilometers) deep underwater.
  • The atmosphere is made of greenhouse gases, primarily carbon dioxide. Which create an extreme case of global warming. They trap the sun's heat, causing surface temperatures to rise over 470 degrees Celsius (880 degrees Fahrenheit), making it the hottest planet in our solar system.
  • It reflects 70% of all the sunlight (albedo) that reaches the planet, which is why Venus shines more brightly than any other planet or star in the night sky.

Earth

Earth

  • While all of the planets, except for Earth, were named after Greek and Roman gods and goddesses, the name Earth is a Germanic word, which simply means “the ground.”
  • It is the third planet from the Sun, orbiting the star from an average distance 1 Astronomical Unit i.e., 150 million km (149,597,870 km to be exact). It's close enough to the sun to be warm.
  • It has a radius of 6,371 kilometers, Earth is the biggest of the terrestrial planets, and the fifth largest planet overall. It is the densest planet of all in the Solar System.
  • Earth's unique position in the solar system allows it to house phenomena yet to be found anywhere else in the universe, particularly liquid surface water and life. Because of Earth's unique distance from the sun, the planet is able to contain water in all of its forms, liquid, ice and gas.
  • It has one natural satellite i.e., earth's moon. Since 71% of its surface is made up of water, it is called 'Blue Planet'.

Mars

Mars

  • Named after the ancient Romans god of war (because its reddish color was reminiscent of blood), Mars is the second smallest planet in the solar system and fourth from the sun.
  • Mars is dense and has a core made of iron, nickel and sulphur. Enveloping the core is a rocky mantle made of silicate minerals and a crust rich in iron. These iron minerals react with the small traces of oxygen in Mars's atmosphere and rusts, giving the planet its signature reddish hue. It is thus called 'Red Planet'.
  • It completes one rotation every 24.6 hours. Martian days are called sols—short for "solar day." A year on Mars lasts 669.6 sols, which is the same as 687 Earth days.
  • It has the largest volcano in the solar system called Olympus Mons, that is three times the height of Mount Everest. Once geologically active but not anymore.
  • It is most intensely studied planet beyond earth. Water exists today on Mars, but mostly in the form of polar ice caps. Because of factors, such as the presence of water, some scientists believe life may have existed on the Red Planet and may exist in the future.

Jupiter

Jupiter

  • Being the biggest planet of the solar system, Jupiter is aptly named after the king of the ancient Roman gods.
  • Jupiter is the fifth planet from the sun and the first planet to be formed in our solar system. It has twice the amount of mass as the other planets put together. Its incredible gravitational force serves as a shield by attracting comets and asteroids onto Jupiter's own surface, preventing them from crashing onto inner planets like Earth.
  • Despite its size, Jupiter has the shortest day of any other planet; it only takes about 10 hours for a complete rotation. But takes 11.8 years to revolve around the sun.
  • Unlike previoulsy seen terrestrial planets, Jupiter has no solid surface. It may not even have a traditionally solid core. Rather, this giant planet may have a dense, liquid center surround by 44 miles thick atmosphere of hydrogen and helium gases. Deep in the atmosphere, pressure and temperature increase, compressing the hydrogen gas into a liquid. This gives Jupiter the largest ocean in the solar system—an ocean made of hydrogen instead of water.
  • The most iconic feature of Jupiter, is a crimson brown storm that's been raging for over 300 years, the Great Red Spot. Even the largest and most powerful hurricanes ever recorded on Earth would be dwarfed by the Great Red Spot with winds peaking at about 400 mph.
  • It has 79 moons, the most of the eight known planets. The four largest moons were discovered by Galileo in 1610 and hence called the Galilean Satellites, they include:
    • Io the most volcanically active celestial body in the solar system;
    • Ganymede, the solar system's largest moon, even larger than the planet Mercury;
    • Callisto and Europa, which along with Ganymede, may contain oceans of liquid water underneath their crusts.
  • Jupiter also has several rings, but unlike the famous rings of Saturn, Jupiter’s rings are very faint and made of dust, not ice.

Saturn

Saturn

  • Named after the Roman god of agriculture and wealth (who is also the father of Jupiter), Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest planet in our solar system.
  • Despite its size, Saturn is actually the lightest planet. It could float in a bathtub if such a colossal thing existed.
  • Like fellow gas giant Jupiter, Saturn is a massive ball made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Saturn's gaseous makeup also means that it has no true surface. At its center, the planet has a dense core of water, ice, and rocky material, but it has no actual landmass.
  • It takes about 10 hours to rotate around its axis and 29.4 years to revolve around the sun.
  • Saturn's ring system is the largest and most complex in the entire solar system. The rings are made of icy and rocky remnants from comets, asteroids, and moons. How the rings are able to stay on track and intact has to do with Saturn's smallest moons. Called Shepherding moons, these tiny satellites orbit between the rings, and they seem to use their gravity to shape the ring material into circular paths.
  • Saturn has more than 60 known moons, and each varies in size and terrain.
    • Enceladus, one of Saturn's smallest moons, is covered in ice.
    • Titan is the Saturn's largest moon and also the only moon in the solar system with clouds and a dense atmosphere. Both Titan and Enceladus have underground oceans that would make them potentially capable of sustaining life.

Uranus

Uranus

  • Named after Greek god of the sky, Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun with the third largest diameter in our solar system.
  • Uranus takes 17 hours to rotate and 84 Earth years to orbit the sun. Partially due to its distance from the Sun (20 AU), Uranus boasts the coldest temperatures in the solar system with temperatures, dropping as low as -224.2 degrees Celsius (-370 degrees Fahrenheit).
  • Uranus has an Earth-sized core made of iron and magnesium silicate. The remainder, approximately 80% of Uranus, is a worldwide ocean of ices made of water, ammonia, and methane. This chemical composition makes planet appear in cool blue color.
  • In addition to its extreme temperatures and orbit, Uranus has a dramatic orientation. While the other seven planets spin on their axes like tops, Uranus appears to roll along its equator. The planet is tilted at a near right angle, in which polar regions point toward and away from the Sun, rather than upward and downward.
  • This tilt, thought to be the result of Uranus' collision with at least one celestial body, has also affected the orientation of Uranus' 13 rings and 27 known moons. Unlike the rings and moons of other worlds, which orbit their home planets horizontally, those of Uranus orbit in a vertical orientation along the planet's tilted equator.

Neptune

Neptune

  • Named after the Roman god of the sea, Neptune is the eigth and most distant planet in our solar system. Being so far away from the heat and light of the Sun, Neptune is cold, dark, and icy.
  • With a distance of 30 Astronomical Units from the sun, Neptune is the only planet in our solar system not visible to the naked eye. This distance creates the longest orbit of the eight worlds, about 165 years. Its rotational time is 16 hours.
  • At its heart is a solid core about 1.5 times the size of Earth. Making up about 45% of the planet's mass, the core is made of water, ice and silicate rock. The rest of the planet is believed to be a hot pressurized ocean of water, methane, and ammonia ices surrounded by a layer of cloud made of hydrogen and helium, include traces of methane, which give this ocean world its rich blue color.
  • Neptune is our solar system's windiest world. These winds whip clouds of frozen methane across the planet at speeds of more than 2,000 kilometers per hour (1,200 mph) . Even Earth's most powerful winds hit only about 400 kilometers per hour (250 mph). In fact, the winds are so powerful that they break the sound barrier.
  • Drifting high above this windy ice giant is a quiet ecosystem of Six rings and 14 known moons. The largest moon, Triton has ice volcanoes and may even contain a subsurface ocean.

Earth's Moon

Moon

  • Earth's only natural satellite is simply called "the Moon" because people didn't know other moons existed until Galileo Galilei discovered four moons orbiting Jupiter in 1610.
  • Moon is the brightest and largest object in our night sky that makes Earth a more livable planet by moderating earths wobble on its axis, leading to a relatively stable climate. It also causes tides, creating a rhythm that has guided humans for thousands of years.
  • The Moon was likely formed after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth several billion years ago. Debris from this collision began to orbit Earth, and accumulated, forming today's moon.
  • It is fifth largest moon in the solar system with size nearly 1/4th the size of earth. It is composed of an iron rich core, plus a mantle and crust containing minerals made of magnesium, oxygen and silicon. The moon's surface was once geologically active and covered in an ocean of magma. But today, apart from traces of water ice, the surface is completely covered in dust and rocky debris.
  • Countless craters dot the moon's surface. Each formed by objects such as meteroids, comets and asteroids crashing onto the moon. The largest crater, the South Pole Aitkin basin, spans across a quarter of the moon's surface, and is nearly deep enough to fit Mount Everest inside.
  • The moon is about 3,84,400 km away from earth. It takes 27 days 7 hours (27.32 days) to rotate around its axis. It rotates at the same rate that it revolves (called synchronous rotation). So as it revolves around our planet, the same side of the moon faces the Earth at all times .
  • In Latin, the Moon is called Luna, which is the main adjective for all things Moon-related: lunar.
  • Similar to earth's orbit around the sun, moon's orbit around the earth is elliptical. The point of the orbit at which the moon is closest to Earth is called perigee (360,000 km), while the point at which the moon is farthest from Earth is known as apogee (405,000 km).
  • From the Earth's surface, we can observe eight distinct traditionally recognized stages of the moon's illumination, called lunar phases. They have been observed for thousands of years, and even provided the basis for the earliest calendars.

Lunar Phases

The Moon's phase and the date of its approach to its perigee or apogee are not synced. When a Full Moon or New Moon occurs close to the Moon's perigee, it is known as a Supermoon. On the other hand, when a Full Moon or New Moon occurs close to the Moon's apogee, it is known as a Micromoon.
