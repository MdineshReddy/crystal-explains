Rocks and Minerals

Minerals

A Mineral is a naturally occurring organic or an inorganic substance, having an orderly atomic structure and a definite chemical composition and physical properties. A mineral is composed of two or more elements. But, sometimes single element minerals like sulphur, copper, silver, gold, graphite etc. are found.

The basic source of all minerals is the hot magma in the interior of the earth. When magma cools, crystals of minerals appear and a systematic series of minerals are formed in sequence to solidify so as to form rocks.

Types of Mineral Resources

On the basis of chemical and physical properties, minerals may be grouped under two main categories of metallics and non-metallics which may further be classified as follows:

Metallic minerals are the sources of metals. Iron ore, copper, gold produce metal and are included in this category. Metallic minerals are further divided into ferrous and non-ferrous metallic minerals. Ferrous refers to iron. All those minerals which have iron content are ferrous such as iron ore itself and those which do not have iron content are non-ferrous such as copper, bauxite, etc.

Non-metallic minerals are either organic in origin such as fossil fuels also known as mineral fuels which are derived from the buried animal and plant life such as coal and petroleum. Other type of non-metallic minerals are inorganic in origin such as mica, limestone and graphite, etc.

Minerals have certain characteristics:

  • These are unevenly distributed over space.
  • There is inverse relationship in quality and quantity of minerals i.e. good quality minerals are less in quantity as compared to low quality minerals.
  • The third main characteristic is that all minerals are exhaustible over time. These take long to develop geologically and they cannot be replenished immediately at the time of need. Thus, they have to be conserved and not misused.

Some of the major minerals include Feldspar, Quartz, Pyroxene, Amphibole, Mica and Olivine.

Rocks

A rock is an aggregate of one or more minerals. Rocks do not have definite composition of mineral constituents. Feldspar and quartz are the most common minerals found in rocks.

Rocks are mainly of three types: igneous, sedimentary and metamorphic rocks. Each rock type is altered when it is forced out of its equilibrium conditions by physical changes—such as melting, cooling, eroding, compacting, or deforming.

Igneous Rocks

The igneous rocks are formed when magma cools and solidifies. They are known as the Primary Rocks as all other rocks are formed directly or indirectly from the igneous rocks.

On the basis of mode of occurence, igneous rocks are classified as:

  • Intrusive Igneous Rocks: are formed due to the solidification of rising magma below the surface of the earth. They have huge mineral grains (or crystals) as the cooling takes place slowly. Ex: Granite.
  • Extrusive Igneous Rocks: are formed due to cooling and solidification of hot and molten magma at the surface of the earth. They have small mineral grains (or crystals) and are relatively smooth as the cooling takes place rapidly. Ex: Basalt.

Sedimentary Rock

Sedimentary rocks are formed due to the deposition, aggregation and subsequent compaction of sediments derived from the older rocks or dead remains of plants and animals.

On the basis of formation, they are of three types:

  • Clastic sedimentary rocks: These rocks are formed by mechanical compression of smaller grains of rocks broken off other rocks by physical weathering. Ex: Sandstone, limestone, shale etc.
  • Chemical sedimentary rocks: These rocks are formed from dissolved chemicals that hardens over time. Ex: Chert, limestone etc.
  • Organic sedimentary rocks: These rocks are formed from the accumulation of plant and animal debris. These rocks often contain fossils. Ex: Coal.

Metamorphic Rocks

Metamorphic rocks are formed by subjecting any rock type—sedimentary rock, igneous rock or another older metamorphic rock— to different temperature and pressure conditions than those in which the original rock was formed.

Metomorphism of rocks takes place with changes in pressure, volume and temperature (PVT).

This process is called metamorphism, meaning to "change in form". By Metamorphism, already formed rocks undergo recrystallisation and reorganisation of materials within original rocks. The result is a profound change in physical properties and chemistry of the stone.

They are of two types:

  • Foliated metamorphic rocks have a layered appearance that is produced by exposure to heat and directed pressure. Examples: gneiss, schist, and slate.
  • Non-foliated metamorphic rocks do not have a layered appearance. Examples of nonfoliated rocks include: marble, quartzite etc.

Rock Cycle

One type of rock changes to another type under certain conditions in a cyclic manner. This process of transformation of the rock from one to another is known as the rock cycle .

Rock Cycle Process:

  • Molten magma cools and solidifies to become igneous rocks.
  • These igneous rocks are broken down into small particles that are transported, deposited and compacted to form sedimentary rocks.
  • When the igneous and sedimentary rocks are subjected to heat and pressure they change into metamorphic rocks.
  • The metamorphic rocks which are still under great heat and pressure melt down to form molten magma. This molten magma again can cool down and solidify into igneous rocks and the process repeats.
