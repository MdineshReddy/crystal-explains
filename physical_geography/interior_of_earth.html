Interior of the Earth

Earth radius ranges from 6,378 km at the equator to 6,357 km at a pole. The interior of the Earth is divided into three parts namely, crust, mantle and core.

Sources of Information about the Earth

Direct Sources

The most easily available solid earth material is surface rock or the rocks obtained from mining areas. Gold mines in South Africa are as deep as 3 - 4 km. Mining beyond this depth is not possible as it is very hot at this depth.

Besides mining, scientists have taken up a number of projects to penetrate deeper depths to explore the conditions in the crustal portions. Scientists world over are working on many major projects. Kola Superdeep Borehole of the erstwhile Soviet Union near the present-day Russia and Norway border is the deepest drill on earth with depth of 12.2 km and often regarded as entrance to hell. This and many deep drilling projects have provided large volume of information through the analysis of materials collected at different depths.

Volcanic eruption forms another source of obtaining direct information. As and when the molten material (magma) is thrown onto the surface of the earth, during volcanic eruption, it becomes available for laboratory analysis. However, it is difficult to ascertain the depth of the source of such magma.

Indirect Sources

Analysis of properties of matter indirectly provides information about the interior. We know through the mining activity that temperature and pressure increase as we go deep. Moreover, it is also known that the density of the material also increases with depth. It is possible to find the rate of change of these characteristics. Knowing the total thickness of the earth, scientists have estimated the values of temperature, pressure and the density of materials at different depths.

Another source of information are the meteors that at times reach the earth. The material and the structure observed in the meteors are similar to that of the earth. They are solid bodies developed out of materials same as, or similar to, our planet. Hence, this becomes yet another source of information about the interior of the earth.

The other indirect sources include gravitation, magnetic field, and seismic activity.

Gravitation

  • The gravitation force (g) is not the same at different latitudes on the surface. It is greater near the poles and less at the equator. This is because of the shape of earth. Its not a perfect sphere. There is a small flattening at the poles and bulging around the equator due to Earth's rotation, thereby distance from the centre at the equator being greater than that at the poles. [Gravity decreases as distance Increases].
  • The gravity values also differ according to the mass of material. The uneven distribution of mass of material within the earth influences this value. The reading of the gravity at different places is influenced by many other factors. These readings differ from the expected values. Such a difference is called gravity anomaly. Gravity anomalies give us information about the distribution of mass of the material in the crust of the earth.

Magnetic Surveys: also provide information about the distribution of magnetic materials in the crustal portion, and thus, provide information about the distribution of materials in this part.

Layers of the Earth

Cross Section of the Earth

Crust

The Crust is the Outermost and the thinnest layer of the Earth. Its thickness ranges from 5–70 kilometres.

  • The thin parts are the oceanic crust, which underlie the ocean basins. Their thickness ranges from 5-10 km. The main mineral constituents of the Oceanic Crust are silica and magnesium and therefore called sima. They are composed of much denser rocks (like basalt) compared to those of Continental Crustal rocks (like Granite).
  • The thicker crust is continental crust with mean thickness of 30 km. It is much thicker in the areas of major mountain systems for example, it is as much as 70 km thick in the Himalayan region. The main mineral constituents of the Continental Crust are silica and alumina and therefore called sial. They are composed of less denser rocks (like Granite) compared to those of Oceaninc Crustal rocks (like basalt).

About 98 per cent of the total crust of the earth is composed of eight elements like oxygen (46%), silicon (28%), aluminium (8%), iron (5%), calcium (4%), sodium (3%), potassium (2%) and magnesium(2%). The average density of the crust is about 2.7 g/cm³.

(average density of the earth is 5.51 g/cm³). The boundary between the Crust and Mantle is called Mohorovicic Discontinuity, or the Moho. It is defined by the distinct change in velocity of seismological waves as they pass through changing densities of rock.

Mantle

The portion of the interior beyond the crust is called the mantle. The mantle extends from Moho’s discontinuity to a depth of 2,900 km. It is composed of silicate rocks richer in iron and magnesium than the overlying crust. The average density is about 4.5 g/cm³.

During the early stages of earth's formation, the mantle was molten. Over millions of years, the mantle cooled. Water trapped inside minerals erupted with lava, a process called “outgassing.” As more water was outgassed, the mantle solidified.

Convection (transfer of heat in fluids by the actual motion of matter) of the mantle propels the motion of the tectonic plates in the crust. The source of heat that drives this motion is the primordial heat left over from the planet's formation, renewed by the radioactive decay of uranium, thorium, and potassium in Earth's crust and mantle.

The mantle is divided into upper and lower mantle separated by a transition zone, as a result of sudden seismic-velocity discontinuities at depths of 410 and 660 km.

Upper Mantle: begins just beneath the crust and ends at the top of the lower mantle at 660 km.

  • The uppermost solid part of the mantle along with the crust is called Lithosphere. It extends upto a depth of 200km.
  • Below the Lithosphere is the asthenosphere, a highly viscous, mechanically weak and ductile part of upper mantle. Astheno means "Weak". It is considered to be extending upto 400 km. It is the main source of magma that finds its way to the surface during volcanic eruptions. The weakness of the asthenosphere allows the tectonic plates to move easily towards a subduction zone.

Transition Zone: is located between the lower mantle and the upper mantle, between a depth of 410 and 660 km. It is also referred to as Repetti discontinuity.

Lower Mantle: extends from 660 to 2900 km below Earth's surface; between the transition zone and the outer core.

The lowest part of the mantle next to the core-mantle boundary is known as the D″ (D-double-prime) layer. Core-Mantle boundary, also referred to as Gutenberg discontinuity is at the depth of 2900 km..

Core

The core is the innermost layer of the Earth and occupies its centre. It is about 3500 km in radius. Information about Earth's core mostly comes from analysis of seismic waves and Earth's magnetic field.

The Core is believed to be mostly composed of an iron–nickel alloy; therefore it is referred to as nife. The average density is about 11.0 g/cm³.

The core is further divided into outer core and inner core. The outer core is in liquid state while the inner core is in solid state.

Outer Core:

  • It extends from 2900 km and 5100 km below the earth’s surface and surrounds the inner core. Density ranges from 9.9 g/cm³ to 12.2 g/cm³.
  • The temperature of the outer core ranges from 4400 °C in the outer regions to 6000 °C near the inner core.
  • It is composed of iron mixed with nickel (nife) and trace amounts of lighter elements.
  • The outer core is not under enough pressure to be solid, so it is liquid even though it has a composition similar to the inner core.
  • Dynamo theory suggests that convection in the outer core, combined with the Coriolis effect, gives rise to Earth's magnetic field.

Coriolis effect: is an effect in which a rotating object experiences a force (the Coriolis force) perpendicular to the object's axis of rotation. On the earth, the effect tends to deflect moving objects to the right in the northern hemisphere and to the left in the southern and is important in the formation of cyclonic weather systems.

Inner Core

  • The inner core extends from 5100 km below the earth’s surface to the centre of the earth. Density ranges from 12.6 g/cm³ to 13 g/cm³.
  • The solid inner core is too hot to hold a permanent magnetic field but probably acts to stabilize the magnetic field generated by the liquid outer core.
  • It has a temperature reaching 6000°C. At 6000°C, this iron core is as hot as the Sun’s surface, but the crushing pressure caused by gravity prevents it from becoming liquid.

Note: Although the core and mantle are about equal in thickness, the core actually forms only 15 percent of the Earth's volume, whereas the mantle occupies 84 percent. The crust makes up the remaining 1 percent.
