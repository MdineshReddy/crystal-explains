Salient Features of the Indian Constitution

Written, Lengthy and Detailed

  • There are two types of Constitutions:
    • written, like the American Constitution
    • non-written, like the British Constitution.
  • The Constitution of India is the lengthiest of all the written Constitutions of the world.
  • Currently (2021) it consists of a Preamble, about 470 Articles (divided into 25 Parts) and 12 Schedules. (originally i.e, in 1949, it had 395 articles divided into 22 parts and 8 schedules.)
  • The following factors contributed to the elephantine size of Indian Constitution:
    • The Geographical factors such as the vastness of the country and its diversity.
    • The Historical factors such as the influence of the Government of India Act of 1935, which itself was bulky.
    • Single Constitution for the whole country i.e., both the Centre and the states.

Inspired From Various Sources

  • The Constitutions of various countries all over the world were referred while drafting the Indian Constitution. The Constitution makers picked up the best of the provisions from different constitutions and molded them to suit the Indian Scenario.
  • In this context, Dr. B.R. Ambedkar proudly acclaimed that the Constitution of India has been framed after ‘ransacking all the known Constitutions of the World.

Mixture of Rigidity and Flexibility

  • On the basis of amendability (add,change or repeal any provision), Constitutions are also classified into two types:
    • A rigid Constitution in which, a special procedure is established for its amendment. Example: the American Constitution.
    • A flexible constitution, that can be amended in the same manner as the ordinary laws are made. Example: the British Constitution.
  • The Constitution of India is neither completely rigid nor completely flexible, but a synthesis of both. Article 368 provides for two types of amendments:
    • Some provisions can be amended by a special majority of the Parliament. Special majority means two-third majority of the members of each House present and voting, and a majority of the total membership of each House.
    • Some other provisions can be amended by a special majority of the Parliament and with the ratification by half of the total states.

Note: Some provisions of the Constitution can be amended by a simple majority of the Parliament in the manner of ordinary legislative process. These amendments do not come under Article 368. Read More about Various Majorities.

federal in form but, unitary in spirit

  • The Constitution of India establishes a federal system of Government. It contains all the usual features of a federation:
    • Federal Features: 1) Dual Government 2) Division of powers 3) Written Constitution 4) Supremacy of Constitution 5) Rigidity of Constitution 6) Independent judiciary 7) Bicameralism.
  • But, the Indian Constitution also contains a large number of unitary or non-federal features.
    • Unitary Features: 1) A strong Centre 2) Single Constitution 3) Single citizenship 4) Flexibility of Constitution 5) Integrated judiciary 6) Appointment of state governor by Centre 7) All-India services 8) Emergency provisions
  • Hence the Indian Constitution has been described as:
    • ‘federal in form but, unitary in spirit’, ‘quasi-federal’ by K.C. Wheare
    • ‘bargaining federalism’ by Morris Jones
    • ‘co-operative federalism’ by Granville Austin
    • ‘federation with a centralizing tendency’ by Ivor Jennings.

Parliamentary System of Government

  • The Constitution establishes parliamentary type of government both at the centre and the state under which the executive is responsible to the legislature for all its policies and actions.
  • It follows the British Cabinet System with the Prime Minister as the head of the executive of the union. The real powers are vested and exercised by the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.
  • The President and the Governor are merely constitutional heads.
  • The Parliamentary System of Government as in the Great Britain has been adopted primarily because of the political experience of the Indian people gathered from the british rule in India.

Integrated and Independent Judiciary

  • The Supreme Court stands at the top of the integrated judicial system in our country. Below it, there are high courts at the state level. Under a high court, there is a hierarchy of subordinate courts, that is, district courts and other lower courts. This single system of courts enforces both the central laws as well as the state laws, unlike in USA, where the federal laws are enforced by the federal judiciary and the state laws are enforced by the state judiciary.
  • Our Constitution established an independent Judiciary, absolutely free from the influence of the executive and legislature. The Constitution has made various provisions to ensure its independence–security of tenure of the judges, fixed service conditions for the judges, all the expenses of the Supreme Court charged on the Consolidated Fund of India, prohibition on discussion on the conduct of judges in the legislatures, ban on practice after retirement, power to punish for its contempt vested in the Supreme Court, separation of the judiciary from the executive, and so on.

Balance Between Judicial Supremacy and Parliamentary Sovereignty

  • The Constitution of India is a refined blending of judicial supremacy and parliamentary sovereignty.
  • The principle of judicial supremacy with that of the American Supreme Court, which can invalidate any act of the American Congress on the simple plea of its being contrary to the spirit of the Constitution.
  • The British Constitution on the other hand embodies the principle of parliamentary sovereignty. The British Parliament can pass, amend, repeal any type of legislation, good or bad, natural or unnatural and the courts in England cannot declare null and void any Act of Parliament on any ground whatsoever.
  • The Indian Constitution attempts to strike a perfect balance by avoiding Judicial Supremacy of the American type and parliamentary sovereignty of the British type. The Supreme Court have power of judicial review and can stand in judgement over the sovereign will of Parliament in ensuring justice for the people. Parliament is absolutely supreme in the sphere of legislation within the bounds of basic structure of the constitution.

Three-tier Government

  • Originally, the Indian Constitution, like any other federal Constitution, provided for a dual polity and contained provisions with regard to the organisation and powers of the Centre and the states.
  • Later, the 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendment Acts (1992) have added a third-tier of Government (i.e., local government).

Fundamental Rights

  • Similar to the Constitution of the USA, the Constitution of India also includes a separate chapter guaranteeing Fundamental Rights to all the citizens. These rights are justiciable and inviolable.
  • They are binding on the legislature as well as on the executive. If any of the rights is violated, a citizen has the right to seek the protection of the judiciary.
  • Any Act of the legislature or order of the executive can be declared null and void if it violates any of the Fundamental Rights guaranteed to the citizens by the Constitution.
  • However, the Fundamental Rights are not absolute and subject to reasonable restrictions.

Directive Principles of State Policy

  • Taken from the constitution of Ireland, Directive Principles of State Policy are the ideals which the state shall strive to achieve as far as possible.
  • These principles are non-justiciable. No legal action can be taken against the state in courts of law if it fails to follow or realise any of these principles. They do not confer any constitutional rights upon citizens and these cannot be enforced through courts of law as in case of Fundamental Rights.
  • Yet, the Constitution itself declares that ‘these principles are fundamental in the governance of the country and it shall be the duty of the state to apply these principles in making laws’. Hence, they impose a moral obligation on the state authorities for their application.

Fundamental Duties

  • The original constitution did not provide for the Fundamental Duties of the citizens.
  • These were added during the operation of internal emergency (1975–77) by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment Act of 1976 on the recommendation of the Swaran Singh Committee.
  • The fundamental duties serve as a reminder to citizens that while enjoying their rights, they have also to be quite conscious of duties they owe to their country, their society and to their fellow citizens.
  • However, like the Directive Principles, the duties are also non-justiciable in nature.

A Secular State

  • The Constitution of India establishes a Secular State. Hence, it does not uphold any particular religion as the official religion of the Indian State.
  • The Indian concept of secularism is different from that the western concept of secularism preaches a complete separation between the religion (the church) and the state (the politics), Indian Constitution embodies the 'positive' concept of secularism, i.e., giving equal respect to all religions or protecting all religions equally.

Universal Adult Franchise

  • Every Indian citizen above 18 years has a right to vote in the elections without any discrimination of caste, sex, religion etc. The voting age was reduced to 18 years from 21 years in 1989 by the 61st Constitutional Amendment Act of 1988.

Single Citizenship

  • All the Indians irrespective of their state, enjoy a single citizenship of India.
  • In countries like USA, each person is not only a citizen of USA, but also a citizen of the particular state to which he belongs. In both these capacities, they enjoy different rights and owe different obligations.
  • The principle of single citizenship was provided for in the Indian Constitution in order to foster strong bond of social and political unity among the people of India who were divided on account of race, ethnicity, languages and multiplicity of religious and cultural background.

Independent Bodies

  • The Indian Constitution stablishes certain independent bodies that are envisaged to be the bulkworks of the democratic system of Government in India. These are:
    • Election Commission to conduct free and fair elections to the Parliament, the state legislatures, the office of President of India and the office of Vice-president of India.
    • Comptroller and Auditor-General of India to audit the accounts of the Central and state governments.
    • Union Public Service Commission to conduct examinations for recruitment to all-India services and higher Central services and to advise the President on disciplinary matters.
    • State Public Service Commission in every state to conduct examinations for recruitment to state services and to advice the governor on disciplinary matters.
  • The Constitution ensures the independence of these bodies through various provisions like security of tenure, fixed service conditions, expenses being charged on the Consolidated Fund of India, and so on.

Emergency Provisions

  • The Indian Constitution contains elaborate emergency provisions to enable the President to meet any extraordinary situation effectively. The rationality behind the incorporation of these provisions is to safeguard the sovereignty, unity, integrity and security of the country, the democratic political system and the Constitution.
  • During emergency our federal system becmones unitary without any amendment of the constitution.
