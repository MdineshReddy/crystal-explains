Types of Majorities

There are four types of Majorities used in Parliament and State Legislatures. They are:

  1. Simple Majority
  2. Absolute Majority
  3. Effective Majority
  4. Special Majority

Simple Majority

  • Simple Majority means a majority of more than 50% of the members present and voting.
  • It is also known as functional majority or ordinary majority or working majority.
  • This is the default majority, meaning when the constitution does not specify the type of majority needed, the simple majority is considered for voting.

Example: Consider the current strength of Lok Sabha as 545 and suppose if 45 of them are absent and 100 of them abstained from voting on an issue. i.e., The number of members present and voting will be 400. Then, the simple majority will be (50% of 400) plus 1 i.e., 201.

Cases where the simple majority is used:

  • Under Articles 2 and 3 Parliament can make laws to admit new states or reorganise the States. Such laws can be passed by a simple majority.
  • To pass money bill (Article 110)/financial (Article 117)/ordinary bills
  • In Join Sessions of Parliament (Article 108).
  • To pass Adjournment Motion/No-Confidence Motion/Censure Motion/Confidence Motion.
  • Vote of Thanks on President's address.
  • To declare President’s Rule (Article 356)
  • To declare a financial emergency (Article 360)
  • To elect the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Lok Sabha ans State Legislatures
  • To elect Deputy chairman of Rajya Sabha.
  • To elect Chairman and Deputy Chairman of Legislative Councils.
  • Constitution Amendment Bill under Article 368 which needs to be ratified by the states needs only a simple majority at the State Legislatures.

Absolute Majority

  • Absolute Majority means a majority of more than 50% of the total strength/membership of the house.

Example: Consider the current strength of Lok Sabha as 545. Then the absolute majority is given by (50% of 545) = 272.5 i.e., 273.

Cases where the Absolute majority is used:

  • Used to prove the stability of a government.
  • Not used in any bills.

Effective Majority

  • This refers to a majority of more than 50% of the effective strength of the House.
  • Effective Strength = total Strength - Vacant Seats
  • Vacant Seats may be due to either resignations or other reasons like death etc.
  • In the Constitution, an effective majority is mentioned as “all the then members”.

Example: Consider the Current total strength of Lok Sabha be 545. Of them, there are 45 vacancies, then the effective strength of the house is 500. Then the effective majority is (50% of 500) plus 1, ie 251.

Cases where the Effective Majority is Used:

  • Removal of Speaker/Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha & Legislative Assembly.
  • Removal of Vice-President in Rajya Sabha.
  • Removal of Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha.
  • Removal of chairman and Deputy Chairman of Legislative Council.

Special Majority

There are four types of special majorities. They are as follows:

  1. Special Majority according to Article 61
  2. Special Majority according to Article 249 and 312
  3. Special Majority according to Article 368
  4. Special Majority according to Article 368 + 50 percent state ratification by a simple majority

Special Majority according to Article 61

  • It refers to a majority of 2/3rd of the total membership of the House.
  • It is used for the impeachment of the President of India.
  • It is the toughest majority to achieve.

Special Majority according to Article 249 and 312

  • This refers to a majority of 2/3rd members present and voting.
  • Cases where it is used:
    • This is used to pass a Rajya Sabha resolution to empower the Parliament to make laws in the State List (Article 249).
    • Rajya Sabha can authorize the Parliament to create new All-India Services common to both the Centre and states (Article 312).

Special Majority according to Article 368

  • This refers to a majority of 2/3rd members present and voting supported by over 50% of the total strength (i.e., absolute majority) of the House. This is chiefly used for most of the Constitution Amendment Bills.
  • Cases where this type is used:
    • Passing a constitutional amendment bill which does not affect federalism.
    • Removing judges of the Supreme Court or High Court.
    • Removing the Comptroller and Auditor General of India (CAG) or the Chief Election Commissioner (CEC).
    • Approval of National emergency (Article 352)
    • Resolution by the State Legislature for the abolition or creation of the Legislative Council (Article 169).
Note: For the abolition or creation of the Legislative Council, a special majority is required at state legislature but a simple majority is enough at the Parliament for approval.

Special Majority according to Article 368 + 50 percent state ratification by a simple majority.

  • This type of majority is needed when a constitutional amendment tries to change the federal structure.
  • E.g.: A good example would be the bill which introduced the GST. It required the support of at least 15 state legislatures out of then 29 states.
