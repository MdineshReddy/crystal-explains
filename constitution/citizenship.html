Citizenship

Part II

(Articles 5-11)

The Constitution of India provides a Single Citizenship for all the citizens of India. It is dealt in Articles 5 to 11 under Part-II of the constitution.

Articles related to Citizenship

Article No. Deals with
5 Citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution
6 Rights of citizenship of certain persons who have migrated to India from Pakistan
7 Rights of citizenship of certain migrants to Pakistan
8 Rights of citizenship of certain persons of Indian origin residing outside India
9 Persons voluntarily acquiring citizenship of a foreign State not to be citizens
10 Continuance of the rights of citizenship
11 Parliament to regulate the right of citizenship by law

Articles 5 to 8 deal with how a person became citizen of India, right after Comencement of Constitution.

Article 11 empowers the Parliament of India to enact a law related to citizenship. It led to enactment of Citizenship Act, 1955 which was amended many times subsequently.

Acquisition of Citizenship

The Citizenship Act of 1955 provides for 5 ways of acquiring citizenship as described below:

By Birth

  • Every person born in India between 26th January, 1950 and 1st July, 1987 shall be a citizen of India by birth irrespective of the nationality of his parents.
  • A person born in India on or after 1st July, 1987 is considered as citizen of India only if either of his parents is citizen of India at the time of his birth.
  • Those who born in India after 3rd December, 2004 are considered as citizen of India only if both of his parents are citizens of India or one of whose parents is a citizen of India and the other is not an illegal migrant at the time of their birth.
  • The children of foreign diplomats posted in India and enemy aliens cannot acquire Indian citizenship by birth.

By Descent

  • A person born outside India betwen January 26, 1950 and December 10, 1992 is a citizen of India by descent, if his father was a citizen of India at the time of his birth.
  • A person born outside India on or after December 10, 1992 is considered as a citizen of India if either of his parents is a citizen of India at the time of his birth.
  • From December 3, 2004 onwards, a person born outside India shall not be a citizen of India by descent, unless his birth is registered at an Indian consulate within one year of the date of his birth.

By Registration

A person may get citizenship of India by an application to Central Government, if he falls under any of the following categories:

  • A person of Indian origin, residing in India for 7 years before making an application.
  • A person of Indian origin, who is ordinarily resident in any country or place outside undivided India.
  • A person, who is married to citizen of India and resident of India for 7 years before making an application.
  • Minor children of persons, who are citizen of India.
  • A person of full age and capacity, whose parents are registered as citizen of India.
  • A person of full age and capacity who, or either of his parents, was earlier citizen of independent India, and has been residing in India for 12 months immediately before making an application for registration;
  • A person of full age and capacity who has been registered as an overseas citizen of India cardholder for 5 years, and has been residing in India for 12 months before making an application for registration.

By Naturalisation

The citizenship of India can be acquired through the process of Naturalisation on the following grounds:

  • He is not a citizen of any country where citizens of India are prevented from becoming citizens.
  • If he is a citizen of any country, he undertakes to renounce the citizenship of that country in the event of his application for Indian citizenship being accepted.
  • He has either resided in India or been in the service of a Government in India throughout the period of 12 months immediately preceding the date of the application;
  • During the 14 years immediately preceding the said period of 12 months, he has either resided in India or been in the service of a Government in India for periods amounting in the total to not less than 11 years
  • He is of good character.
  • He has an adequate knowledge of a language specified in the 8th Schedule to the Constitution
  • In the event of a certificate of naturalisation being granted to him, they intend to reside in India.

Note: The Government of India may waive all or any of the above conditions for naturalisation in the case of a person who has rendered distinguished service to the science, philosophy, art, literature, world peace or human progress.

By Incorporation of Territory

  • If any new territory becomes a part of India, the Government of India specifies who among the people of that territory shall be the citizens of India.

Loss of Citizenship

According to the Citizenship Act, 1955, the citizenship can be lost in following three ways:

By Renunciation

  • If any citizen of India of full age and capacity can make a declaration renouncing his Indian citizenship. Upon the registration of that declaration, that person ceases to be a citizen of India.
  • However, if such a declaration is made during a war in which India is engaged, its registration shall be withheld by the Central Government.

By Termination

  • When an Indian citizen voluntarily acquires the citizenship of another country, his Indian citizenship automatically terminates.
  • This provision, however, does not apply during a war in which India is engaged.

By Deprivation

  • Central government can terminate a person's Indian citizenship, if:
    • the citizen has obtained the citizenship by fraud.
    • the citizen has shown disloyalty to the Constitution of India.
    • the citizen has unlawfully traded or communicated with the enemy during a war.
    • the citizen has, within five years after registration or naturalisation, been imprisoned in any country for two years; and
    • the citizen has been ordinarily resident out of India for seven years continuously.

Person of Indian Origin (PIO) and Overseas Citizens of India (OCI)

Person of Indian Origin (PIO)

The person registered as PIO are governed under the Ministry of Home Affairs Scheme. Following persons are eligible for PIO card:

  • Anyone who held Indian passport anytime.
  • He or either of his parents or grandparents or great grandparents was born in and was permanently resident in India.
  • He is spouse of a citizen of India or a person of Indian origin as mentioned above.

Overseas Citizens of India (OCI)

OCIs are governed as per the Citizenship Act, 1955. Following persons are eligible for becoming OCI:

  • A foreign national who was eligible to become a citizen of India on 26/01/1950.
  • A person who was a citizen of India on or at anytime after 26/1/1950.
  • He who belonged to a territory that became part of India after 15/8/1947.
  • His children of such person.
  • Minor children of such person.

These schemes are not available to the persons belonging to Pakistan and Bangladesh. These schemes allows multiple entries into India without VISA. The voting rights are still not granted to them.

Merger of PIO and OCI

In January, 2015, the government announced merger of these two scheme. It means that PIO category has been withdrawn and new applicants may apply for OCI scheme. Now, the following changes have been carried out:

  • All individuals holding PIO card would automatically be granted benefits of OCI card.
  • The government of India has stopped issuing the PIO cards and asked the new applicants to apply for OCI card Scheme.
  • The OCI cardholders can apply for Indian citizenship if they stay in India for atleast one year within the five years period of obtaining OCI status.
