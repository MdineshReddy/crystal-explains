Historical Background

The Constitution of India is heavily influenced by various acts passed by the erstwhile British Government in India. The important of such acts are listed below:

Acts Passed During the Company Rule (1773-1858)

Regulating Act of 1773

  • This was the first step taken by the British Parliament to control and regulate the affairs of the East India Company in India.
  • The Governor of Bengal was made the Governor-General of Bengal. Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General of Bengal.
  • An Executive Council of Four members was established to assist the Governor-General. There was no separate legislative council.
  • The Presidencies of Madras and Bombay were made subordinate to the Bengal Presidency.
  • The Supreme Court was established at Fort William (Calcutta) in 1774. The Court claimed jurisdiction over any person residing in Bengal, Bihar or Orissa.
  • It prohibited servants of the company from engaging in any private trade or accepting bribes from the natives.
  • The governing body of the company called the Court of Directors, were ordered to report on the companies revenue, civil, and military affairs in India.

Amending Act of 1781

  • This act is also known as the Act of Settlement.
  • It exempted the Governor-General and his Council and also the servants of the company from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for the acts done by them in their official capacity.
  • It excluded the revenue matters from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court.

Pitt’s India Act of 1784

  • This act separated commercial and political functions of the company.
  • It allowed the Court of Directors to manage the commercial affairs, but created a new body called Board of Control to manage the political affairs.
  • The company territories in India were called ‘British possession in India’ for the first time. This indicated the ownership of British Crown over the territory of East India Company in India.
  • Governor’s Councils were also formed at Madras and Bombay.

Charter Act of 1793

  • It extended the overriding power given to Lord Cornwallis (in 1786) over his council, to all future Governor-Generals and Governors of Presidencies.
  • It extended the trade monopoly of the Company in India for another period of twenty years.

Charter Act of 1813

  • This act ended the East India Company’s monopoly over trade with India. Trade with India was open to all British subjects. However, it continued the monopoly of the company over trade in tea and trade with China.

Charter Act of 1833

  • Governor-General of Bengal became the Governor-General of India . First Governor-General of India was Lord William Bentick.
  • The legislative powers of the Bombay and Madras Presidencies were removed. The Governor-General of India was given exclusive legislative powers for the entire British India.
  • This was the final step towards centralization in British India.
  • The Act ended the activities of the East India Company as a commercial body and it became a purely administrative body.

Charter Act of 1853

  • The legislative and executive functions of the Governor-General’s Council were separated.
  • A Central Legislative Council was created, consisting of 6 members out of which 4 were to be appointed by the provisional governments of Madras, Bombay, Agra and Bengal.
  • It introduced an open competition system of selection and recruitment of civil servants.

Acts Passed During the Crown Rule (1858-1947)

Government of India Act, 1858

  • After the 1857 revolt, the rule of the company was replaced by the rule of the Crown in India.
  • The office of the Secretary of State for India was created. The Indian administration was under his authority. He was assisted by a 15-member Council of India. He was a member of the British Cabinet and was responsible ultimately to the British Parliament.
  • The Governor-General of India was designated the Viceroy. He was the direct representative of the British Crown in India. Lord Canning was the first Viceroy of India. Viceroy was answerable to the Secretary of State for India.
  • The Court of Directors and the Board of Control were abolished, thereby ending the system of double government.

Indian Councils Act, 1861

  • It introduced for the first time Indian representation in law-making (legislative) process. 3 Indians entered the Legislative council.
  • It also provided that the Viceroy’s Executive Council should have some Indians as the non-official members.
  • It initiated the process of decentralisation by restoring the legislative powers to the Bombay and the Madras Provinces.
  • It recognised the portfolio system, introduced by Lord Canning. Under this, a member of the Viceroy’s council was made in-charge of one or more departments of the Government and was authorised to issue final orders on behalf of the council on matters of his department(s).
  • Viceroy was empowered to issue ordinances, without the concurrence of the legislative council, during an emergency. The life of such an ordinance was six months.

Indian Councils Act, 1892

  • Legislative Councils were expanded and were given more functions such as the discussion of budget and addressing questions to the executive.
  • Indirect elections (nominations) were introduced in central and provincial legislative councils.

Indian Councils Act, 1909

  • This Act is also known as the Morley-Minto Reforms as Lord Morley was the Secretary of State for India and Lord Minto was the Viceroy of India at that time.
  • The number of members of the central legislative council was increased from 16 to 60. It changed the name of the Central Legislative Council to the Imperial Legislative Council.
  • Direct elections to the legislative councils were introduced for the first time.
  • Legislatures could pass resolutions , ask questions and supplementaries and vote for separate items on the budget.
  • Introduced a system of communal representation for Muslims by accepting the concept of ‘separate electorate’. Lord Minto came to be known as the Father of Communal Electorate.
  • For the first time, Indians were appointed to the Viceroy's executive council. Satyendra Prasanna Sinha was appointed as the Law Member.

Government of India Act, 1919

  • This Act is also known as the Montague-Chelmsford Reforms as Montagu was the Secretary of State for India and Lord Chelmsford was the Viceroy of India at that time.
  • The Central subjects were demarcated and separated from those of the Provincial subjects.
    • The central legislature was authorised to make laws on the Central list of subjects.
    • The provincial legislatures were authorised to make laws on the provincial list of subjects.
  • Provincial subjects were further divided into two parts as transferred and reserved. This scheme of dual governance was called ‘Dyarchy’. It was introduced only in the Provincial subjects.
    • The transferred subjects were to be administered by the Governor with the aid of Ministers and was responsible to the legislative council.
    • The reserved subjects, on the other hand, were to be administered by the Governor and his executive council without being responsible to the legislative council.
  • A bicameral legislature was introduced for the first time at the centre. Thus, the Indian legislative council was replaced by a bicameral legislature consisting of an Upper House (Council of State, which later became Rajya Sabha) and a Lower House (Legislative Assembly, which later became Lok Sabha).
  • The Act also mandated that the three of the six members of the Viceroy’s Executive Council (other than Commander-in-Chief) were to be Indians.
  • It extended the principle of communal representation by providing separate electorates for Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians and Europeans.
  • It provided for the establishment of a public service commission. Hence, a Central Public Service Commission was set up in 1926 for recruiting civil servants.
  • This act had a provision that a commission would be appointed ten years from date (1919) to study the progress of the governance scheme and suggest new steps.

Simon Commission (1927): An all-white, seven-member Indian Statutory Commission, popularly known as the Simon Commission (after the name of its chairman, Sir John Simon), was set up by the British government. All parties boycotted the commission. The commission recommended the abolition of dyarchy, extension of responsible Government in the provinces, establishment of a federation of British India and princely states, continuation of communal electorate and so on. The recommendations of this committee were incorporated (with certain changes) in the next Government of India Act of 1935.

Provinces and Princely States: Before 1947, there were two types of states in India — the provinces and the Princely states. Provinces were ruled directly by British officials, who were appointed by the Viceroy. Princely states were ruled by local, hereditary rulers, who acknowledged sovereignity in return for local autonomy.

Government of India Act, 1935

  • An all-India Federation was proposed which would consist of British India and the princely states. This federation never came into being as the princely states did not join it.
  • The Act divided the powers between the Centre and the units into three lists, namely the Federal List (59 items), the Provincial List (54 items) and the Concurrent List (36 items). Residuary (remaining) powers were given to the Viceroy.
  • The Act abolished the Dyarchy in the Provinces and introduced ‘Provincial Autonomy’.
  • The act tried introducing Dyarchy at the Centre but it never came into effect.
  • Introduced bicameralism in 6 out of 11 Provinces. These six provinces were Assam, Bengal, Bombay, Bihar, Madras and the United Province.
  • It abolished the Council of India, established by the Government of India Act of 1858.
  • It provided for the establishment of a Reserve Bank of India as the Central Bank of India.
  • It provided for the establishment of Federal Court(1937) which was replaced by the Supreme Court of India on 28 January 1950.
  • This Act continued until it was replaced by the new Indian Constitution.

Indian Independence Act of 1947

  • India was declared independent and sovereign. India was partitioned into two independent dominions of India and Pakistan.
  • It granted freedom to the Indian princely states either to join the Dominion of India or Dominion of Pakistan or to remain independent.
  • The office of the Secretary of State for India was abolished.
  • The Viceroy and the Governors were made constitutional (nominal) heads. They were made to act on the advice of the respective council of ministers in all matters.
  • It empowered the Constituent Assemblies of the two dominions to frame and adopt any constitution for their respective nations.
  • It also assigned both legislative and executive powers to the Constituent Assembly until new elections were conducted.
