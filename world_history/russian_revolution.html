Russian Revolution

The Russian Revolution of 1917 was one of the most significant political and social revolution commencing with the abolition of the monarchy in 1917 and concluding in 1923 with the Bolshevik establishment of the Soviet Union at the end of the Civil War.

The Russian Revolution of 1905 was a major factor contributing to the cause of the Revolutions of 1917. Lenin, as later head of the USSR, called it "The Great Dress Rehearsal", without which the "victory of the October Revolution in 1917 would have been impossible".

Background

Russia, in the 19th century was underdeveloped and a backwards society compared to the rest of the Europe. Besides the territory around Moscow, the Russian empire included current-day Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, Moldova, Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan and Central Asian states (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan).

The Russian Empire practiced serfdom—a form of feudalism (which was by then ended in other european nations) in which landless peasants were forced to serve the land-owning nobility. While the serfs were breaking their backs in the fields, the Tsars held all the power.

In 1861, the Russian Emperor Tsar Alexander II of the Romanov dynasty also known as Alexander the Liberator finally abolished serfdom. But the life of a peasant did not improve much. They resented paying redemption payments to the state, and demanded communal tender of the land they worked.

Alexander II was followed by his son Alexander III as the new Tsar in 1881. He was highly reactionary and reversed some of the liberal reforms of his father. If Alexander II was the great reformer, Alexander III was the great repressor.

Alexander III was followed by his son Nicholas II as the new Tsar in 1894. He was neither a reformer nor a repressor. He was a timid and easily swayed ruler.

Russian Revolution of 1905

At the beginning of the twentieth century, the vast majority of Russia’s population were agriculturists. Sergei Witte, One of the Tsar Nicholas II's most influential advisors and first 'Prime Minister' of Russian Empire, knew that Russia needed to catch up with the rest of Europe. Based on his Suggestions, the Nicholas borrowed some money and started industialization by setting up factories at St Petersburg (then capital) and Moscow.

But the workers were subjected to poor working conditions - Long hours, low wages, filthy and disease-ridden factories, sleep in over crowded dormitories. So the workers started to protest and strike.

During this period Russia went to war with Japan (1904-1905) over rival imperial ambitions in Manchuria and Korea. Seeing Russia as a rival, Japan offered to recognize Russian dominance in Manchuria in exchange for recognition of Korea being within the Japanese sphere of influence. Russia refused and demanded the establishment of a neutral buffer zone between Russia and Japan in Korea. Negotiations broke down and the war started in 1904. The war ended with a humiliating defeat to the Russia in 1905.

The people of Russia were outraged with russia's defeat, Workers continued their strikes for better working conditions, Peasants were uprising for land rights, liberals were asking for reforms in administration and the military mutinies continued. The tension was rising rapidly and Russia was on the verge of revolution. All it needed was one disaster to push it over the edge.

And that disaster would come on 22nd January, 1905. An Orthodox priest named Father Gapon was leading workers and their families to the winter palace (at St. Petersburg) in a peaceful protest. They wanted to deliver a petition to Nicholas which simply asked for more freedom and better working conditions. But the Imperial Soldiers opened fire on the crowd. Nicholas was not at the winter palace at that time.

Hundreds died and Hundreds wounded. The event became known as Bloody Sunday and Nicholas's reputation plummeted. Strikes erupted across the empire, workers demands increased, liberals demanded political power, peasants demanded land. The country was out of control and the 1905 revolution had begun.

But there was no unity among different parts of the society and people weren't working together. Meaning there was a weakness to exploit. Sergei wrote a manifesto that would give the liberals an elected assembly called the Duma. Nicholas agreed to share power and have his laws approved by an elected assembly.

With the liberal satisfied and war with Japan ended, Nicholas ordered thousands of troops back home who then dismantled the remaining protestors, arrested their leaders and crushed the peasant uprisings in the countryside and the revolution of 1905 died out.

In 1906, Nicholas issued the Fundamental Laws, which gave him the title of "supreme autocrat". Although no law could be made without the Duma's assent, neither could the Duma pass laws without the approval of the noble-dominated State Council (half of which was to be appointed directly by the Tsar), and the Tsar himself retained a veto. Furthermore, the Tsar had the power to dismiss the Duma and announce new elections whenever he wished. All these powers and prerogatives assured that, in practice, the Government of Russia continued to be a non-official absolute monarchy.

Vladimir Lenin (1870-1924)

The rule of the Tsars was quickly becoming outdated and more Russians began wondering if there was a better way. For many, the solution was simple. Just look to the West, republics, democracies and constitutional monarchies galore.

But a small growing group rejected that for an even better idea, communism. Prominent one among them was Vladimir Lenin, an intelligent member of Russia's middle-class.

Lenin embraced revolutionary socialist politics from a young age. His older brother was executed for plotting to kill the Tsar and Lenin himself was expelled from university for participating in a student protest against the Tsarist government.

His interest in Marxism was sparked by Karl Marx's writings, particularly, The Communist Manifesto and Das Capital . Lenin developed a variant of the communist ideology known as Leninism .

Marxism is a social, political, and economic philosophy that examines the effect of capitalism on labor, productivity, and economic development and argues for a worker revolution to overturn capitalism in favor of communism. Marxism points that the struggle between social classes—specifically between the bourgeoisie, or capitalists, and the proletariat, or workers—defines economic relations in a capitalist economy and will inevitably lead to revolutionary communism.

Leninism: is a political theory that proposes the establishment of the dictatorship of the proletariat (working-class people) led by a revolutionary vanguard party, as the political prelude to the establishment of communism.

He moved to Saint Petersburg in 1893 and became a senior Marxist activist. In 1897, he was arrested for sedition and exiled to Siberia for three years, where he got married. After his exile, he moved to Western Europe, where he became a prominent theorist in the Marxist Russian Social Democratic Labour Party (RSDLP).

In 1903, he took a key role in the RSDLP ideological split, leading the Bolshevik (russian for Majority) faction against Mensheviks (russian for Minority). The Mensheviks were less radical and were worried that Lenin's attitude could lead to a one-man dictatorship.

With the outbreak of Russian Revolution of 1905, Lenin tried desperately to radicalize the uprising. Still in exile and lacking funds, the Bolsheviks simply weren't in a position to do anything. Around this time, Lenin met an Georgian revolutionary Joseph Stalin at a communist convention in Finland. Stalin raised funds for Vladimir Lenin's Bolshevik faction via robberies, kidnappings and protection rackets. Stalin was frequently exiled to Siberia.

Till 1917, Lenin remained in Europe, writing his socialist newspaper and awaiting an opportunity to overthrow the Tsar and bring communist utopia to Russia.

Period between 1905 and 1917

After the 1905 revolution had failed, the Tsars new top man was Pyotr Stolypin who replaced Sergei Witte as the new Prime Minister. In order to prevent more chaos, he started agricultural reforms to please the Peasants and also cracked down hard on the Tsar's opponents.

Many positive reforms were also being made and the Russian economy even began to improve. This is a problem for Lenin. If the people weren't suffering, then they wouldn't support a revolution.

The Russian economy was making a recovery. For the Tsar, things were looking up. But the assosciation of the Tsar with a controversial and scandal ridden Mystic Grigori Rasputin helped discredit the tsarist government and became an important factor in the revolution of 1917.

Grigori Rasputin (1869 - 1916)

Grigori Yefimovich Rasputin born in 1869 in Siberia to a peasant family. He might have lived a life of obscurity in his small village, if not for his conversion to the Russian Orthodox Church in the 1890s.

Inspired by the humbled monks that wandered endlessly from holy site to holy site, he spent years on pilgrimages across Russia. On his travels, strangers were captivated by Rasputin’s magnetic presence. Some even believed he had mystical gifts of prediction and healing. Despite Rasputin’s heavy drinking, petty theft, and promiscuity, his reputation as a monk quickly spread beyond Siberia and attracted both laypeople and powerful Orthodox clergymen.

When he finally reached the capital, St. Petersburg, Rasputin used his charisma and connections to win favor as the imperial family’s spiritual advisor.

In November 1905, Rasputin was finally introduced to Russian Tsar Nicholas II. Nicholas and his wife Alexandra devoutly believed in the Orthodox Church, as well as in mysticism and supernatural powers, and this Siberian holy man had them transfixed. It was a particularly tumultuous period for Russia and their family. The monarchy was barely clinging to control after the Revolution of 1905. Their political struggles were only intensified by personal turmoil: Alexei, the heir to the throne, had a life-threatening blood disease called hemophilia. When Alexei suffered a severe medical crisis in 1912, Rasputin advised his parents to reject treatment from doctors. Alexei’s health improved, cementing the royal family’s belief that Rasputin had magical healing powers, and guaranteeing his privileged place on the royal court.

Today, we know that the doctors had prescribed aspirin, a drug that worsens hemophilia. After this incident, Rasputin made a prophecy: if he died, or the royal family deserted him, both their son and their crown would soon be gone.

Outside the royal family, people had mixed views on Rasputin. On one hand, peasants regarded him as one of their own, amplifying their often-unheard voice to the monarchy. But nobles and clergymen came to despise his presence. Rasputin never ceased his scandalous behavior, and they were skeptical of his so-called powers and thought he was corrupting the royal family. They were convinced the only way to maintain order was to eliminate him.

He was assassinated in december 1916. His death, like his life was shrouded in mystery. But far from stabilizing the monarchy’s authority, Rasputin’s death enraged the peasantry. Just as Rasputin prophesied, his murder was swiftly followed by that of the royal family. Whether the downfall of the Russian monarchy was a product of the monk’s curse, or the result of political tensions decades in the making, well, we may never know.

World War I and Russia

The outbreak of war in August 1914 initially served to quiet the prevalent social and political protests, focusing hostilities against a common external enemy, but this patriotic unity did not last long. As the war dragged on inconclusively, war-weariness gradually took its toll. Around this time the capital St. Petersburg was renamed to Petrograd.

Nicholas had taken direct command of the army and left his ambitious but incapable wife Alexandra (who was a German) in charge of the government. Reports of corruption and incompetence in the Imperial government began to emerge, and the growing influence of Grigori Rasputin in the Imperial family was widely resented.

An inefficient Tsarist government, meant there were shortages of just about everything you need to fight a war. Soldiers were deserting. The economy was imploding and in no time, Russia was starving. The peasants and the workers protests continued.

The February Revolution of 917

By 1917, Russia had been fighting a war it couldn't afford for three years. They were running out of many things, most worryingly, food. On International Women's Day, (March 8, 1917) , thousands of hungry women in Petrograd were so sick of being hungry that they took to the streets. Soon men joined too. The crowd demanded an end to the war, an end to food rationing and even an end to the Tsar autocracy. Even the soldiers joined the protest.

Liberal politicians (members of Duma) had long been dissatisfied with the Tsar since he would shut their parliament down anytime they did something he didn't like. They convinced the generals (military chiefs) that if Nicholas abdicated, the people would calm down and the generals were onboard. And with the military no longer on his side, Nicholas had no choice but to step down. And so, 300 years of Romanov rule in Russia came to an end in March 1917. Nicholas was reunited with his family at the Alexander Palace at Tsarskoye Selo. He was placed under house arrest with his family by the Provisional Government.

The immediate effect of the February Revolution was a widespread atmosphere of elation and excitement in Petrograd.

  1. On 16 March, a provisional government was announced which comprised the Liberals i.e., the members of Duma.
  2. The socialists had formed their rival body, the Petrograd Soviet (or workers' council) largely controlled by Social Revolutionaries and the Mensheviks.

The Petrograd Soviet and the Provisional Government competed for power over Russia, creating what in Soviet historiography is known as the Dvoyevlastiye (Dual power).

Lenin's Arrival

The provisional government did not drag Russia out of the war, the people were still hungry and the peasants were still hoping to get land rights. Meanwhile, the Soviets hadn't done much to change things either.

With Germany's help Lenin returned to Petrograd in April and gave a speech to Bolshevik supporters condemning the Provisional Government and again calling for a continent-wide European proletarian revolution.

He publicly condemned both the Mensheviks and the Social Revolutionaries, who dominated the influential Petrograd Soviet, for supporting the Provisional Government, denouncing them as traitors to socialism. He advocated immediate peace with Germany and Austria-Hungary, getting rid of the provisional government and the rule by soviets under his leadership.

Lenin and Bolsheviks promised three things "Peace, Food and Land". Bit by bit, the Bolsheviks became more popular. Some of the Mensheviks even sided with Bolsheviks, prominent among them is Leon Trotsky.

The Minister of War of the Provisional Government, Alexander Kerensky, thought that if people saw more Russian victories, they'd have to support the new government. But the German troops won battle after battle against the Russians. These heavy defeats worsened the Russian economy and made the hungry people hungrier. Tens of thousands of armed workers took to the streets during some of the worst violence Petrograd had seen yet. And in response Kerensky called in the troops who opened fire on the demonstrators.

For now, Lenin and other Bolshevik leaders wanted to distance themselves from the violence but the crowds marched under Bolshevik slogans and as a result, Kerensky now the prime minister took the opportunity to crack down hard on the Bolsheviks. Lenin fled to Finland under threat of arrest while Trotsky, among other prominent Bolsheviks, was arrested.

In August, General Kornilov was appointed Supreme Commander of Russian military forces. Unfortunately for Kerensky, Kornilov hated the liberal and socialist reforms of the new government. Fearing a Bolshevik takeover was imminent, Kornilov ordered his men towards Petrograd to oust the soviet and take over.

Desperate, Kerensky released Trotsky and other Bolshevik leaders and sought their help. He also sought help from the Petrograd Soviet, which called upon armed Red Guards to "defend the revolution". The Kornilov Affair failed largely due to the efforts of the Bolsheviks, whose influence over railroad and telegraph workers proved vital in stopping the movement of troops. With his coup failing, Kornilov surrendered and was relieved of his position. This whole affair was a huge propaganda win for the Bolsheviks. They had defended the revolution and their popularity skyrocketed.

In early September, the Petrograd Soviet freed all jailed Bolsheviks and Trotsky became chairman of the Petrograd Soviet. They were now in a very powerful position, almost powerful enough for Lenin to return home from Finland and finally the stage was ready for the communist revolution.

October Revolution 1917

The Bolsheviks began planning their takeover of the Russian government. Lenin proposed an armed revolution while some members argued for a more peaceful approach. Kerensky began arresting Bolsheviks and as a result, they felt that they had no choice but to commence the revolution right away.

Lenin was back in Petrograd but was still in hiding. So Trotsky using his position as Soviet Chairman organized the Bolshevik militias. In just one day, they took control of the city without much resistance. Kerensky managed to escape.

Lenin came out of hiding to play a bigger role in the revolution. With him back at the helm, they stormed the Winter Palace and arrested the provisional government.

Lenin was finally in charge of Russia. New elections were held (on 25 November 1917) to replace the newly dissolved provisional government. In the elections the Bolshevik party received 25% of the votes and the Socialist-Revolutionaries as much as 58%. When it became clear that the Bolsheviks had little support outside of the industrialized areas of Saint Petersburg and Moscow, they simply barred non-Bolsheviks from membership in the Soviets and dissolved the Constituent Assembly in January 1918.

For Lenin, setting up a communist utopia was looking suspiciously like setting up a dictatorship. While he was implementing many of the communists policies, he was also refusing to work with other political parties and cracking down on opposition.

Outside russia, they were still at war with the Germans and they had promised to give the people peace. So Lenin made Trotsky Commissar for Foreign Affairs and sent him off to negotiate a peace deal with the Germans. The Germans offered Trotsky really harsh terms like giving up huge chunks of land. Trotsky proposed a 'No-War and No-Peace' plan, where instead of appealing for peace, they simply do not take part in it.

This plan proved disastrous, when the Germans saw the Russians had stopped fighting they slammed 700,000 troops deep into Russian territory with no resistance. Now the new peace treaty offered by Germans was way worse with Russia losing a huge amount of territory, population and resources.

The Bolsheviks had no choice but to accept and Russia was humiliated and signed Treaty of Brest-Litovsk on 3 March 1918. The treaty ceded vast territories, including Finland, the Baltic provinces, parts of Poland and Ukraine to the Central Powers. With Petrograd now in an exposed position, Lenin moved the capital to Moscow.

Civil War (1917-1923)

Things really weren't going well for Lenin and many, many people were extremely unhappy with the Bolshevik government and its actions. A variety of forces had united together to topple Lenin's government and Russia descended into a full blown civil war.

The war was fought mainly between the Red Army ("Reds"), consisting of the Bolsheviks, and the "Whites"– socialists, liberals and supporters of autocracy who opposed the drastic restructuring championed by the Bolsheviks following the collapse of the Provisional Government, to the Soviets (under clear Bolshevik dominance). The whites were supported by the allied powers (Britain, France..).

The Whites gained control of vast underdeveloped areas while the Bolshevik Reds controlled the industrial heartland using this to their advantage, along with the military genius of Trotsky and the disorganization of the White Army, the fortified Red Army gradually came out on top. It was an absolutely brutal conflict with both sides committing horrendous atrocities.

To maintain order at home, the Bolsheviks began the Red Terror and the secret police would execute thousands of suspected traitors. No one was safe from the violence not even Nicholas himself.

Execution of the Romanov family

After his abdication, Nicholas and his family were placed under house arrest at the Alexander Palace at Tsarskoye Selo. At first, they were allowed to live in their usual luxury but after Lenin took over, their conditions worsened.

The Bolsheviks feared that the White armies would free Nicholas and re-instate him as the Tsar. To stop this from happening, Nicholas's Bolshevik guards decided to act.

It's not entirely certain whether Lenin ordered it or if the guards were acting on their own volition but on July 17th, 1918 with White Armies approaching, they took Nicholas, his family and their servants down into the basement. There a squad of Bolsheviks murdered the entire family and their servants. Nicholas, the last Tsar, once one of the most powerful men alive had met a brutal end.

Last days of Lenin

After years of fighting and millions of deaths, Trotsky and his Red Army came out victorious in 1923. Shortly after Lenin suffered two separate strokes.

As the Soviet union (Union of Soviet Socialist Republics) was officially declared under a strict one party-system , Lenin's health continued to decline and his ability to lead the communist party went with it.

Everybody assumed Trotsky would succeed him as he was a great speaker and he'd won the civil war. The last person anyone expected to take over was Stalin.

Stalin's Rise to Power

After the revolution, a new government was formed comprising mainly Bolsheviks. Lenin nominated Stalin as the party's new General Secretary .

As secretary, he had the power to give people jobs within the party. So he started giving jobs to all of his friends and loyalists who in turn would give him their support. Lenin took note of Stalin abusing his position. But by this stage he was just too sick to fight it.

Vladimir Lenin, died on 21 January 1924. Petrograd (St. Petersburg) was renamed Leningrad. This name remained until 1991, after which it was changed back to Saint Petersburg.

By the time Lenin died, Stalin was too powerful to remove. He had his remaining opponents arrested or killed. Trotsky was banished and fled to Europe (eventually he would be assassinated by Soviet spies in 1940).

Stalin's Collectivization

After 1917, land had been given over to peasants. But the food shortages continued. Stalin believed that shortages were partly due to the small size of holdings. These small-sized peasant farms could not be modernised. He also believed that in order to develop modern farms, and run them along industrial lines with machinery, it was necessary to take away land from peasants, and establish state-controlled large farms.

In 1929 Stalin proposed the first five-year plan in which he started collectivization of agriculture. As a part of the plan, the government took control of agriculture and collective farms (kolkhozes) were established. Individual peasants (kulaks) were forced off their land.

Peasants were forced to work on the collective land, and the kolkhoz profit was to be shared. Those who resisted collectivisation were severely punished. Many were deported and exiled.

In spite of collectivisation, production did not increase immediately. In fact, the bad harvests of 1930-1933 led to one of most devastating famines in Soviet history when over 4 million died.

Penalties were introduced, and many citizens were prosecuted for criticizing the government. The labor provided by convicts working in the labor camps of the Gulag system became an important component of the industrialization effort. An estimated 18 million people passed through the Gulag system, and perhaps another 15 million had experience of some other form of forced labor.

Aside from Agriculture, Russia, in many measures the poorest nation in Europe at the time of the Bolshevik Revolution, now industrialized at a phenomenal rate.

Lenin vs Stalin: ideological differences

Lenin had waited so long to take control in Russia but he never got to see his communist utopia. His short time in charge was spent dealing with the destroyed Russian economy, World War One and the civil war. He was cruel and merciless, but he really did seem to believe communism would make Russia a better place.

Stalin on the other hand, would take the Soviet Union down a different path. A secret police state, a rapidly militarizing superpower led by a man who deeply distrusted the West and would see the world come to the brink of nuclear annihilation. (read more on The Cold War. )
