World War 1

World War I, also known as the Great War or the first total war or the war to end all wars, began in 1914 with the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria and lasted until 1918.

During the conflict, the great powers of Europe were divided into two coalitions:

  • Great Britain, France, Russia, Belgium, Italy, Serbia, Romania, Japan and the United States (from 1917) etc., which were called the Allied Powers and
  • Germany, Austria-Hungary, Bulgaria and the Ottoman Empire etc., which were called the Central Powers .

Background

For much of the 19th century, the major European powers had tried to maintain a tenuous balance of power among themselves, resulting in a complex network of political and military alliances.

The Spark: Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand

On 28 June 1914, Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian Empire, visited the Sarajevo, the then part of Austria-Hungary with Serbian and Bosnian population who were not happy with Austria-Hungary annexing the Southern provinces.

A group of bosnian-serb assassins gathered on the street where the Archduke's motorcade was to pass, with the intention of assassinating him. They threw a grenade at the car but missed. Some nearby were injured by the blast, but Ferdinand's convoy carried on.

About an hour later, when Ferdinand was returning from a visit at the Sarajevo Hospital with those wounded in the assassination attempt, the convoy took a wrong turn into a street where, by coincidence, Gavrilo Princip, one of the assassin stood. With a pistol, Princip shot and killed Ferdinand and his wife Sophie.

The assassination led Austria to issue July Ultimatum, a series of ten demands that were made intentionally unacceptable, in an effort to provoke a war with Serbia. Serbia accepted all the terms of the ultimatum except for articles five and six, which demanded that Austrian-Hungarian representatives be allowed to assist in suppressing subversive elements inside Serbia's borders and to participate in the investigation and trial of Serbians linked to the assassination.

With Serbia not accepting all the demands, Austria broke off diplomatic relations with Serbia and on 28 July 1914, a month after the assassination, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia.

The War Begins

Russia under Tsar Nicolas II, due to its alliance with the Serbs, mobilized its army against Austria-Hungary.

Kaiser Wilhelm II the emperor of Germany asked his cousin, Tsar Nicolas II, to suspend the Russian mobilisation, which Russia failed to do, so then Germany mobilized its own army and declared war on Russia.

The German government issued demands to France that it remain neutral. the French did not respond but sent a mixed message by ordering their troops to withdraw 10 km from the border to avoid any incidents, and at the same time ordered the mobilisation of their reserves. Germany declared war on France.

Germany’s War plan, the Schlieffen Plan, required that it invade France via neutral Belgium, And Great Britain was a friend of Belgium and so they declared war on Germany.

Within a week, Russia, Belgium, France, Great Britain and Serbia had lined up against Austria-Hungary and Germany, and World War I had begun.

The War

According to Schlieffen Plan, Germany was to quickly knock France and Britain out of the war on the western front, then redeploy to the Eastern front and do the same to Russia.

The initial German advance in the West was very successful: by the end of August the left Allied forces (France & Britain) were in full retreat by the Battle of the Frontiers .

But Russia is ready for war, way earlier than expected. Germany had to send some troops back to the east to defend against the Russians, contributing to the German loss in the Battle of the Marne.

Austria-Hungary was fighting both Serbia and Russia at the same time.

First Battle of the Marne: A gap opened up between the German armies as they closed in on Paris. If a gap opens up, the opponents can use it to flank from the sides and behind. This made German forces retreat back some 50 km. The allies launch a counter attack. The Germans dig into defensive position. The allies did the same. This started the trench warfare.

trench warfare

The trench warfare on the Western Front is most famous for its brutal futility. Great Britain and France on one side, Germany on the other, with no man’s land (a muddy land of shell craters and barbed wire) between.

One side would pummel the other with endless barrage of artillery shells. This had a huge psychological effect on the soldier, leaving many shell shocked.

Sometimes, the attackers would leave their trenches and rush across no man's land. The defending side would then unleash machine gun fire on the attackers inflicting thousands of casualties. The attacking side would send wave after wave, until either they gave up or the defending trench was finally overrun.

There would be months of fighting and the deaths of thousands in order to gain a few meters or kilometers of land.

Living in the trenches was hard too. Corpses, mud that could swallow you whole, pools of poisonous water, rats, disease, the smell of decomposing bodies. World War I was a writer’s war, and there’s a lot of metaphorical resonance in living men digging holes where they would in time die.

And for most soldiers, especially the British and French, the pay for their efforts was pitiful. So why did they even keep fighting? Duty, nationalism, loyalty to comrades, and fear of being shot for desertion all played a role.

New Frontiers and Participants

When the war first broke out Australia was quick to take German New Guinea. The Allies also quickly invaded Germany's colonies in Africa, and particularly in German East Africa, locals were enlisted as soldiers and carriers by both sides. Leading to a tragic loss of life for the Native Africans.

Italy, Romania and Japan joined the war on the side of Allied Powers. Japan captured German Islands and colonies in East Asia. Italy was promised a part of Austria-Hungary's land by the Allies. Romania also joined the Allied Powers in 1916, only to be defeated in 1917.

The Ottomans joined the Central Powers in the middle East. First, the Ottomans tried to attack Russia in the Caucasus Mountains. But they weren't prepared for the cold and many of them froze to death. Then they crossed miles of desert to take the Suez Canal from the British. But that failed, too. Then the Allies tried to take the Dardanelles (the strait linking the Sea of Marmara with the Aegean Sea) at Gallipoli in a long and hard trench warfare campaign but that also failed. The Ottomans blamed their initial losses on the ethnic Armenians living within Ottoman territory. And the resulting Armenian genocide led to the deaths of one and a half million people.

Bulgaria joined the Central Powers as the Central Powers promised land to it.

India and the World War 1

British feared that Indian nationalist would start instigating uprisings in India in the backdrop of world war 1. However, contrary to British fears of a revolt in India, the outbreak of the war saw an unprecedented outpouring of loyalty and goodwill towards Britain. Indian political leaders were eager to support the British war effort since they believed that strong support for the war effort would further the cause of Indian Home Rule. India supplied men, food, clothes, and ammunition. The suffering caused by the war, as well as the failure of the British government to grant self-government to India after the end of the war, fuelled the campaign for full independence that would be led by Gandhi and others.

All these new Frontiers hadn't done much to change the war. Aware that the Allies had more men and supplies than them, the Germans knew they had to do something to break the stalemate.

The Germans (violating the Hague Convention that banned chemical warfare and attacks on civilians) used chlorine gas for the first time in the Second Battle of Ypres (2 Apr 1915) on the Western Front. Several types of gas soon became widely used by both sides and became one of the most-feared and best-remembered horrors of the war. The Germans also started Zeppelin air raids over British cities and they used submarines to sink civilian ships. One such civilian ship was the Lusitania which had a Americans on board when it was sunk. Further swaying US opinion against the Germans.

Battles of Verdun (1916) and Somme (1916): The longest and one of the bloodiest battles of the war started when the Germans launched an attack around the French city of Verdun. The French defended it desperately, leading to hundreds of thousands of casualties. Under pressure, the French called on its allies to draw the German's attention away. So, the British started their own long and brutal fight, the Battle of the Somne, with 60,000 British casualties on just the first day. Tanks were developed by Britain and France and were first used in combat by the British.

In the Middle East, Russia was pushing into Ottoman territory from the North. A new offensive under one famous British officer, better known as Lawrence of Arabia was launched from the south with all-out desert warfare. He helped to lead the Arab tribes in a revolt that wreaked Havoc on the Ottoman supply lines.

Russia Drops, America Joins

By the time 1917 rolled around everyone was exhausted. There are mutinies in the French army, the German populace was starving, and the war had drained all of Russia's supplies. There is no clear winner, and it was still anyone's war.

The Russia was first to break. Tired of food shortages, there was an uprising in Petrograd complete with riots and strikes. The riots turned into a full-scale revolution (read more on Russian Revolution) and a new government overthrew the Tsar. Then a few months later the Bolsheviks overthrew the new government. And they pulled Russia out of the war by signing Treaty of Brest-Litovsk on 3 March 1918. The treaty ceded vast territories, including Finland, the Baltic provinces, parts of Poland and Ukraine to the Central Powers

At the outbreak of the war, the United States pursued a policy of non-intervention: avoiding conflict while trying to broker peace. When the German U-boat sank the British liner RMS Lusitania on 7 May 1915 with 128 Americans among the dead, President Woodrow Wilson demanded an end to attacks on passenger ships. Germany complied. Wilson unsuccessfully tried to mediate a settlement. In January 1917, Germany invited Mexico to join the war as its ally against the US. In return, the Germans would finance Mexico's war and help it recover the territories of Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. But the British intercepted the message, showed it to the Americans, and that was the final straw and Wilson declared war on Germany. American troops began shipping out to Europe.

This was terrible news for Germany. And they knew their only hope now is to force France and the UK to surrender before the fresh American troops arrived. It was now or never. So they started one final attack. They converged their troops and hit hard at the Somme and pushed the Allies back. They hit a second time for the north, then again, and again.

With each hit the germans were spending more and more resources while the allies were getting better and better at repelling their attacks. With American troops now arriving in larger numbers the Allies launched a counter-attack, and that was it.

The central powers were being pushed back on all fronts. Bulgaria collapsed first, followed by the Ottoman Empire, then Austria-Hungary and finally on November 11th, 1918 at 11 o'clock, Germany surrendered.

Treaty of Versailles: ended World War I, fixed the blame for the war on Germany, which proved ruinous to the German economy and destructive to its political institutions.

To prevent a future world war, the League of Nations was created during the 1919 Paris Peace Conference. The organisation's primary goals were to prevent armed conflict through collective security, military and naval disarmament, and settling international disputes through peaceful negotiations and arbitration.

Conclusion

Most people think of World War I as a tragedy because it didn’t need to happen and didn’t really accomplish much, except for creating social and economic conditions that made World War II possible.

The war itself was incredibly destructive. Over 15 million people were killed and over 20 million wounded. A lot of civilians died, especially in the Ottoman Empire where more than 2 million of the 3 million people killed were non-combatants. Armenian Genocide by Ottomans which killed 1.5 million Armenians took place in 1915.

Another major outcome of the war was the end of the Ottoman Empire and the emergence of the nation-state of Turkey. The rest of the world saw some change too, but not much for the better. In Africa, Britain took Germany’s colonies, and even though Indians fought and died in a higher percentage, India didn’t gain any real autonomy.

All these terrible outcomes led to a general sense of disappointment in literary circles, and this feeling of pointlessness and cynicism was expressed by the writers of the “lost generation.” It was a war full of loss: Millions of people were lost. Traditional ideals of war’s nobility and heroism were lost as well. What is heroism when you’re just sitting in a trench, waiting to be blown up?

One silver lining in all of this tragedy can be the experiences of the soldiers from India, Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and Canada that helped build nationalist movements when survivors returned home after the war.
