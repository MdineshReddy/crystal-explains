World War 2 - Background

World War I had radically altered the political European map, with the defeat of the Central Powers—including Austria-Hungary, Germany, Bulgaria and the Ottoman Empire and the 1917 Bolshevik seizure of power in Russia, which led to the founding of the Soviet Union.

Meanwhile, the victorious Allies of World War I, such as France, Belgium, Italy, Romania, and Greece, gained territory, and new nation-states were created out of the collapse of Austria-Hungary and the Ottoman and Russian Empires.

To prevent a future world war, the League of Nations was created during the 1919 Paris Peace Conference. Despite strong pacifist sentiment after World War I, irredentist and revanchist nationalism emerged in several European states in the same period.

Germany under Hitler

Early Life

Adolf Hitler had a troubled upbringing. Right from his childhood, he got big into German nationalism. He dropped out of school but he was an Incredible Speaker.

By his 20s, both his parents were dead. He went to Vienna (Austria) hoping to find a career in the arts. Instead without parental support, hitler ended up on the streets. He spent a few rough years living in and out of homeless shelters, making what little he could from selling postcards he painted.

During his time in Austria, he became a firm believer in the idea that there are many Races, the purest of which were the German aryan people and the worst of which he believed were jews. His anti-semitic beliefs originated here.

Since Austria-Hungary was a multi-ethnic Empire full of lesser races according to hitler, he moved to Munich in Germany at 24 years of age and for one more year he was a drifter on the streets.

World War I and Aftermath

When the World war 1 started, Hitler volunteered for the German Army. He was reportedly a brave soldier and was awarded the iron cross. He had many close encounters with death. He was blinded by a british gas attack in 1918. A month later still recovering in hospital, he learned of Germany's defeat and Surrender.

The conditions of Treaty of Versailles wreaked havoc in Germany. They lost territory, had to demilitarise the Rhineland (western Germany), had to reduce their army to just 100,000 men, couldn't have an airforce, had to pay the Allies a huge amount of money that it didn't have.

Hitler, seeing his country humiliated grew hatred in him for those responsible, who according to him were the communists and jews. He believed that they had stabbed Germany in the back by spreading dissent and Anti War Propaganda.

The German Empire was dissolved in the German Revolution of 1918–1919, and a democratic government, later known as the Weimar Republic, was created which lasted till 1933.

Entry into politics

Since Germany's military had to be reduced, Hitler could no longer remain a soldier after the war, but he kept working for the army as an informant. After the war, communists in Germany had attempted a revolution, and the government was worried about communism in general, so Hitler was tasked with infiltrating and reporting on any new political parties that could pose a communist threat.

One time he infiltrated into the German Workers' Party but found out that they weren't communists at all - they were extreme right, and shared many of his extreme beliefs, so he left the army, and signed up to join the party. His fantastic speaking abilities impressed the party's leadership and supporters, and he very quickly rose to the top. He renamed the party to the National Socialist German Workers' Party, or Nazi for short, and adopted the Swastika as its symbol.

Hitler gave a new direction to the party by promising to return Germany to it's former glory by undoing the Treaty of Versailles, and reuniting all ethic Germans into one nation. He also said that only pure Aryan people should be allowed to be citizens and that all Jews would lose their citizenship.

Many of the political parties in Germany at the time had para-military wings, and the Nazis were no different. Hitler set up the "Storm Detachment" (Sturmabteilung), or SA for short. Their job was to defend Nazi party meetings and intimidate political opponents, and they were frequently engaged in battles with communists on the streets.

Rise to power

The new democratic government (Weimar Republic) was pretty weak and ineffective. In order to pay reparations to the Allies, it started printing more money. The problem is that printing money doesn't actually give a country more money - it just makes money less valuable (Inflation). So as the country printed more and more money, it became worth less and less and the currency crashed. Banknotes became worthless.

In such an economic crisis, Germany struggled to pay the allies. France occupied the Ruhr, an area full of factories, and took the economic output from the area as payment. They treated the German civilians badly and many Germans were killed during the occupation.

Germans were furious and Hitler and the Nazis thought that now would be a great time to lead a revolution. He made an unsuccessful attempt to overthrow the German government in 1923. Even though the attempt failed, his popularity rose.

To help Germany pay its reparations, America agreed to give it loans. In October 1929, the Wall Street crash happened that started the Great Depression. America wanted its money back. The economic strain that stood on an already struggling Germany was severe. Unemployment skyrocketed, Poverty was widespread .

It was clear that the newly formed democracy wasn't working. In the face of crisis, Germans began moving to the political extremes i.e., either the communists or the Nazis. The Nazi party used propaganda to make Hitler seem like a great and powerful man and they became more and more popular until in 1932, they became the biggest party in the German parliament.

Appointment as chancellor and then the Dictator

Hitler ran for president but lost to the extremely popular World War I general, Paul von Hindenburg. Since he was now the leader of the biggest party, though, he demanded President Hindenburg make him chancellor, to which he agreed reluctantly.

Hitler became chancellor of Germany in January 1933, but he was not yet a dictator. In February, the German Parliament building was set on fire. Historians still aren't sure who did it and many suspect the Nazis did it themselves, but Hitler blamed the communists, and he convinced president Hindenburg to sign an emergency decree allowing him to imprison all communists and other political opponents.

Dictatorship

Communists and others were sent off to the first concentration camp in Dachau. At this time, the elderly president Hindenburg passed away, giving Hitler the perfect opportunity. He introduced a law to parliament that would allow him to make all future laws and decisions entirely on his own. Just two months after becoming chancellor, Hitler was now a dictator.

He still had one problem. The leader of the SA, Rohm wanted the SA to take over the job of the regular German Army and the German Army didn't like that idea. Hitler needed to maintain the support of his professionally trained German army, more so than his rough and rowdy SA.

So one night in June 1934, he had Rohm and many other of his own SA officers rounded up and murdered. While he was at it, he took the opportunity to brutally settle some personal scores as well. Politicians who had disagreed with him in the past, reporters who had printed negative articles about him and in some cases, even their families were murdered. In total, up to 200 people were killed in what became known as the Night of the Long Knives.

The army, now satisfied that they wouldn't be replaced, pledged total allegiance to their new fuhrer and Hitler's control was now absolute.

Life in Nazi Germany

Life in Germany changed violently. Freedom of the press, expression, and public assembly were suspended. Jews were initially branded and their businesses boycotted, and eventually, Hitler would go on to have six million Jewish men, women, and children killed in concentration camps. Hundreds of thousands of people were forced into sterilization for physical and mental imperfections.

Hitler Youth an youth organisation of the Nazi Party was formed to brainwash the young. Boys were trained to fight and returned home from camp, violent. Girls were told their purpose was to have many pure Aryan children and they would sometimes return from camp, pregnant. When their parents were understandably horrified, their children would threaten to turn them over to the Gestapo (secret police) for standing in the way of Germany's greatness.

Anyone who dared to pose Hitler or speak out against him in any way, would be sent to a concentration camp.

Why Hitler Went to War?

Hitler had a number of reasons for wanting to expand Germany’s territory, but he often talked about Lebensraum or living space for the German people. German agriculture was inefficiently organized into lots of small farms, and that meant that Germany needed a lot of land in order to be self-sufficient in food production.

His plan was to take Poland, the Ukraine, and Eastern Russia, and then resettle that land with lots of Germans, so that it could feed German people. This was called the Hunger Plan because the plan called for 20 million people to starve to death. Many would be the Poles, Ukrainians, and Russians who’d previously lived on the land. The rest would be Europe’s Jews, who would be worked to death.

In Conclusion,

German Nationalism captivated the young Adolf. Extreme ideology and anti-semitism vested in him as a young man living a hard life on the streets. Germany's defeat in the First World War filled him with hatred and a thirst for vengeance. A political movement that treated him like a god and hundreds of thousands looking up to him as their savior made him a megalomaniac, and soon, his aggressive foreign policies would drag the world into a second tragic global conflict, otherwise known as World War II.

Italy and Benito Mussolini

Mussolini was originally a socialist politician and a journalist. He later denounced socialism for extreme nationalism also called Fascism.

Fascism is a form of far-right, authoritarian ultra-nationalism characterized by dictatorial power, forcible suppression of opposition, and strong regimentation of society and of the economy.

Mussolini's aim was to restore the ancient glory of the Roman Empire by expanding Italian colonial possessions and reunite all Italian peoples.

World War 1 and Italy

Italy joined on the side of Allied Powers during world war 1, since it was promised a part of Austria-Hungary's land by the Allies. After the war, the refusal of the Allies to grant these promised territories caused widespread indignation among Italian nationalists.

On top of that, a bad economy, high unemployment and weak governments meant that the Italian people were unhappy. Post-War period was characterized by mass strikes and unrests.

Rise of Mussolini

Tired of Instable governments, Mussolini's fascist movement gained a lot of support. Mussolini was appointed prime minister in 1922 by King Victor Emmanuel III. After removing all political opposition through his secret police and outlawing labor strikes, Mussolini and his followers consolidated power through a series of laws that transformed the nation into a one-party dictatorship.

Within five years, Mussolini had established dictatorial authority by both legal and illegal means and aspired to create a totalitarian state.

Hitler and Mussolini had a lot of the same ideas and beliefs, both were radical nationalists, anti-democratic, anti-socialist, both envisioned a totalitarian regime with them at center, both had superiority complex and most importantly both had same enemies.

With the Outbreak of World War 2, Italy alienated itself from Britain and France by aligning with Germany and Japan.

Japan

Japan at the start of 20th century, was suffering from an acute fear of food shortage because its agricultural sector was having trouble keeping up with population growth. And the Japanese too, sought to expand their agricultural holdings by, for instance, resettling farmers in Korea. So, they fought with Russia in 1904-1905 and surprisingly won. Then they fully annexed Korea.

Japan joined the World War 1 on the side of Allied Powers primarily to capture German Islands and colonies in East Asia.

Then there was an incident (that maybe staged by the Japanese army) known as the Mukden Incident (1931) where a bomb blew up a Japanese train in Manchuria (in China), giving them an excuse to launch an invasion and take over Manchuria.
