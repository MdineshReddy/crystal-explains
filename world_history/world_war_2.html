World War 2

World War II or the Second World War lasted from 1939 to 1945 and involved almost all the countries of the world. The Participants were grouped into two alliances.

  • The Axis Powers: Germany, Japan and Italy were the Major Axis Powers.
  • The Allies: UK, Soviet Union (USSR) and US were major among the Allies.

World War II is generally considered to have begun on 1 September 1939, when Nazi Germany, under Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland.

Background

Note: We will continue from where we left off in World War 2 - Background.

The definite cause of the war is unbridled military expansion by Germany, Japan, and, to a small extent, Italy.

Italy

Italy under Mussolini seized Abyssinia (in Africa) from Ethiopia. Italy also wanted to control the entrance to the Adriatic Sea. So they occupied Albania.

Japan

China appealed to the League of Nations to stop the Japanese invasion of Manchuria (1931). Japan withdrew from the League of Nations after being condemned for its incursion into Manchuria. The two nations then fought several battles until a Truce was signed in 1933. Thereafter, Chinese volunteer forces continued the resistance to Japanese aggression.

In 1937, there was another incident (that may have been planned by the Japanese Army) called Marco Polo Bridge Incident (7 July 1937) where gunfire was exchanged by Japanese and Chinese troops at the Marco Polo Bridge. This incident is generally reckoned as the start of the Second Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945) in which Japanese launched yet another invasion against China. They swept through Peking (Beijing) and Shanghai, and then advanced through the Yangtze Valley to China's then-capital, Nanking. It was here that saw the worst of Japan's brutal atrocities committed against the Chinese people and is still so controversial today that it affects relations between Japan & China.

This full-scale war between the Chinese and the Empire of Japan is often regarded as the beginning of World War II in Asia.

Germany

Hitler started undoing the Treaty of Versailles, he set up a fighter-wing unit called Luftwaffe to carry on aerial warfare. Then followed a massive re-armament program and complusory military service i.e., conscription to expand the army. He remilitarised the Rhineland (western Germany) in March 1936.

The allies followed a policy of Appeasement, a diplomatic policy of making political or material concessions to an aggressive power in order to avoid conflict. Hitler wanted to create more Lebensraum (living space) for Germany, so, he seized Austria and Czechoslovakia with little resistance from other European powers.

Italy and Germany signed Pact of Steel (22 May 1939) to continuing trust and co-operation between Germany and Italy.

Hitler hated the Polish Corridor that split Germany into two. The Allies warned him that an invasion of Poland would mean war. Hitler had planned to continue his advance eastward, but he didn't want to end up fighting a war on two fronts. So, he signed Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact, a non-aggression pact (23 Aug 1939) with Soviet Union, led by Joseph Stalin that included a secret agreement to partition Poland between the two countries.

The War

On the 1 September 1939, German troops entered Poland, and Britain and France declared war on Germany. The Poles fought hard, but they were no match for the German Army.

The Phoney War

The eight-month period at the start of World War II was referred to as the Period of Phoney War where there was no major hostilities on the Western Front.

The French had launched a small invasion into the Saar Land (western German State), but they maintained mostly defensive positions, that too for a short period. After the World War 1, France did not do much to improve its army and technology. They built a line of defenses along their German border (Maginot Line) but did not extend all the way to the English Channel. Meaning, it could be bipassed by going through Belgium, Netherlands, and Luxembourg, which Hitler exactly did.

And at first, the UK wasn't much better. UK Prime Minister Chamberlain still naively hoped that the war could be ended diplomatically. Instead of bombing raids, the Royal Air Force (RAF) dropped propaganda leaflets over German cities which had little to no effect. They also only sent 200,000 men to France, while the French had mobilised millions.

Both Britain and France wanted to avoid a repeat of the First World War, and so they wanted to keep the war as far from home as possible. So they turned their eyes north, towards Norway.

Sweden was neutral but it was exporting iron ore to Germany, through Norway which was also neutral. So the Allies asked them to stop exporting iron ore to Germany which they refused. Britain and France started naval blockade and mined the waters around Norway to force any transport ships into international waters.

Hitler realised what the Allies were up to, and he quickly moved to secure his supply of iron ore. He launched an invasion through Denmark into Norway. The Allies rushed to land troops along the coast, but Germany had taken control of Norway's airfields, and with their air superiority, decided to fight.

The Allies had to retreat. After the embarrassing failure of Norwegian campaign , Chamberlain resigned. And was replaced with Winston Churchill as Prime Minister on 10 May 1940.

In November 1939, Soviet Union attacked Finland. Despite overwhelming numerical superiority, it became a humiliating struggle for Soviet Union and their military ineptitude was put on full display. In the end they did force the Finns to sue for peace (March 1940) with minimal finnish concessions. Then they continued their campaign of pushing around much smaller countries by annexing the baltic states of Estonia, Lativa, Lithuania, and part of northern Romania (which later became Moldova). Which were setup as Soviet Socialist Republics.

France's Invasion

Hitler's overall strategy was similar to Germany's WW1 strategy. Attack and defeat France, knocking out the UK in the process on the Western Front, then turn on the Soviet Union on the Eastern front and win the war.

The period of Phony War with the Allies had given Hitler enough time to prepare his forces. Hitler launched an invasion on neutral Belgium, Netherlands and Luxembourg to get around France's defenses. The Allies charged into Belgium at full speed, to meet the German invasion head-on, and it looked like a repeat of the First World War was coming.

But Germany followed a new Strategy called Blitzkrieg. It was characterized by a swift movement of troops, tanks, and massive use of air power to support infantry movements. In the very early years of the war, it was extremely effective.

The Germans carried out a flanking manoeuvre through the Ardennes (forest) region, which was mistakenly perceived by Allies as an impenetrable natural barrier against armoured vehicles. But the Germans squeezed in from all sides, taking out France's best armies, and nearly wiping out the British, too. But they managed to make a desperate last-minute escape at Dunkirk ( 26 May to 4 June 1940), with British civilian ships even making the perilous journey to bring their young men home.

On 10 June, Italy invaded France, declaring war on both France and the United Kingdom. With most of the French forces depleted, the Germans breezed through, taking Paris, and France fell on 14 June 1940. Hitler occupied only the coastal areas of France for defense, but allowed it to continue its existence as a German puppet state.

With the fall of france, Hitler hoped now all alone UK would also lose hope and sue for peace. But it didn't.

Air Battle with Britain (The Blitz)

The United Kingdom rejected Hitler's peace offer, and the German air superiority campaign started in August. Waves of German bombers flew over Britain, while the completely outnumbered RAF (Royal Air Force) worked bravely around the clock in an attempt to quell the German attacks.

At first, the Luftwaffe targeted British ports and coastal facilities, then it attacked RAF bases, crippling the RAF's ability to defend the nation. Churchill ordered a small, pretty insignificant bombing raid over Berlin.

Furious, Hitler immediately ordered the Luftwaffe to refocus its attacks on civilian targets in London. Children were sent off to the countryside, away from their parents, to avoid danger. And frequent trips to air-raid shelters became a daily occurrence.

This refocusing on London also gave the RAF time to reorganise. So when the Luftwaffe sent one massive all-out attack on London, the RAF successfully repelled it, destroying many of the German aircraft, and placing air superiority firmly in British hands. Hitler's invasion had to be postponed, but the bombing of British cities continued for some time.

New Participants

On the Allies side, many Commonwealth nations and other allied colonies had joined the war in Britain support. They would play a key role throughout the war particularly in the African and Italian campaigns. France's colonies in Equatorial Africa also pledged their allegiance to free France. Churchill requested Franklin D. Roosevelt (of USA) to join on Allies side. Roosevelt refused, but helped the allies by supplying food, weapons and ammunitions

At the end of September 1940, the Tripartite Pact formally united Japan, Italy, and Germany as the Axis powers. Later Hungary, Bulgaria and Romania also joined. Romania wanted to recapture territory ceded to the Soviet Union.

New Technology

German u-boats were sinking thousands of Allied supply ships in the Atlantic including American ones in the hopes that if the Germans could sever Britain's supply line then UK would starve.

Throughout the war the Allies had to come up with better technology to fight the u-boats. Improved radar aircraft with longer range better, anti-aircraft weaponry and convoy tactics. In the end, Alan Turing and his team of code breakers cracked Germany's Enigma code to intercept and decrypt german orders and messages. The u-boats gradually became less and less of a threat.

Axis attack on the Soviet Union (1941)

Hitler believed that the UK's refusal to end the war was based on the hope that the US and the Soviet Union would enter the war against Germany sooner or later. He therefore, decided to try to strengthen Germany's relations with the Soviets or failing that to attack and eliminate them as a factor. In November 1940, negotiations took place to determine if the Soviet Union would join the Tripartite Pact. The Soviets showed some interest but asked for concessions from Finland, Bulgaria, Turkey, and Japan that Germany considered unacceptable.

On 22 June 1941, Germany, supported by Italy, Romania, Finland and Hungary, invaded the Soviet Union in Operation Barbarossa. Stalin was far from ready, the Soviets (red army) didn't stand a chance and Germany made staggering progress with huge encircling movements capturing mind-boggling numbers of Russian troops at major cities. Leningrad was put under siege.

Germany's ultimate goal included the eventual extermination, enslavement, Germanization and mass deportation to Siberia of the Slavic peoples, and to create more Lebensraum (living space) for Germany.

The Eastern Front of the Second World War was brutal for all that endured it. A major offensive against Moscow was launched; after two months of fierce battles in increasingly harsh weather, the German army almost reached the outer suburbs of Moscow, where the exhausted troops were forced to suspend their offensive because of extreme cold. Stalin called in troops from the Siberian front, specially trained to fight in the extreme cold and the Germans were no match. They were now being pushed back and had no choice but to dig in and wait for winter to end.

Japan's Expansion and America's Entry

Japan's war in China had come to a standstill and they wanted to keep expending the sphere of influence. Southeast Asia and Pacific Islands ware heavily colonized by America and Great Britain. It was also full of ocean. Japanese Navy could stand up to that of the US and the UK.

So Japan planned to destroy their navies before starting the conquest. On December 7 1941, Japanese launched a surprise air raid on the US Pacific Fleet at Pearl Harbor (Hawaii) and inflicted a huge amount of damage. They also attacked British colonies in Southeast Asia.

Roosevelt and Churchill declared war on Japan. Hitler then declared war on America even though he didn't have to.

Meanwhile Japan took Guam, the Gilbert Islands, Wake Island, Hong Kong, Philippines, and forced Thailand to join them so they can March their troops through to Malaya. They swept through Singapore, North Borneo, the East Indies, New Guinea, the Solomons and they were now threatening northern Australia and conquered Burma and reached the borders of India.

Japan's victory had been as staggering as the Germans. But as with the Germans, their victories had been based on speed not power and power would eventually catch up.

Eastern Front (1942–43)

With winter over, Germans launched their main summer offensive against southern Russia in June 1942, to seize the oil fields of the Caucasus.

Germans split its army into two groups:

  • Army Group A advanced to the lower Don River and struck south-east to the Caucasus and
  • Army Group B headed towards the Volga River to capture Stalingrad (now known as Volgograd), a key city.

The German attack on Stalingrad was one of the bloodiest battles in the history of war, with more than two million dead. The Germans began by dropping more than 1,000 tons of bombs on Stalingrad, and then the Russians responded by “hugging” the Germans, staying as close to their front lines as possible so that German air support would kill Germans and Russians alike.

The Germans had nearly taken Stalingrad, but the Soviets were now better prepared and in an attack that resembled Hitler's own encirclement tactics, sixth army of the Nazis were completely cut off. After that, due partly to Hitler’s overreaching megalomania and partly to soldiers being scared of him, the sixth army slowly froze and starved to death before finally surrendering. And of the 91,000 Axis POWs from Stalingrad, only about 6,000 ever returned home.

With complete air superiority, the Soviets started pushing westward. For Stalin, it was a resounding victory, for Hitler an absolute catastrophe.

Allies gain momentum (1943–44)

When Japan tried to capture the Island of Midway, US Navy was ready for the attack and they sank Japan's carriers from which the Japanese Navy would never recover. American followed a strategy of "island hopping or Leapfrogging" in the Pacific, where in they went from island to island, liberating them and getting closer to mainland Japan.

In March 1944, the Japanese launched the first of two invasions, an operation against British positions in Assam, India, and soon besieged Imphal and Kohima. In May 1944, British-Indian forces mounted a counter-offensive that drove Japanese troops back to Burma by July.

American and British troops squeezed German and Italian troops out of Africa. With the axis Out of Africa, the Allies had to decide their next move: Churchill wanted to attack from the south starting with Italy. Roosevelt preferred full-on sea invasion in northern France. They went with both.

  • From South: Allies' invasion of Sicily (in Italy) launched on 9 July 1943, which, combined with previous Italian failures, resulted in the ousting and arrest of Mussolini later that month.
  • From North: On 6 June 1944 (called D-Day), the Ally troops of America, Britain and Canada landed at Normandy in France, under the careful planning of General Dwight Eisenhower. It was a tremendous struggle with a great loss of life. Finally Paris was liberated and the Germans were pushed out of France, as the Allies entered Belgium. D-Day invasion of Normandy was the beginning of the end for the Nazis.

Axis collapse, Allied victory (1944–45)

The allies suffered some setbacks trying to liberate the Netherlands but they were making progress and were now threatening the industrial heartland of Germany.

On 16 December 1944, Germany made a last attempt on the Western Front by using most of its remaining reserves to launch a massive counter-offensive in the Ardennes region again. By January 1945, the offensive had been repulsed with no strategic objectives fulfilled.

Hitler's last-ditch attempt had failed and what followed was a total collapse of the German forces. The Allies pushed into Germany from both sides. Soviet and Polish forces stormed and captured Berlin in late April. In Italy, German forces surrendered on 29 April.

Several changes in leadership occurred during this period. On 12 April, President Roosevelt died and was succeeded by Harry S. Truman. Clement Attlee replaced Churchill as Prime Minister. Benito Mussolini was killed by Italian partisans on 28 April. Two days later, Hitler committed suicide in besieged Berlin. Now only Japan remains who continued to fight rejecting the call for unconditional surrender.

On August 6th and on August 9th, the United States dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki respectively. The cities were reduced to rubble and for the people living there, it was a terrible fate. But for the Allies it achieved their main aim, in September the Emperor announced Japan's surrender.

After six years, the war was finally over. The Allies occupied Japan for eight years, the Emperor was allowed to keep his position. Germany was divided between America, the UK, France and the Soviet Union. In 1949 the Allied sectors were united into West Germany (also called Federal Republic of Germany). The East Germany (also called German Democratic Republic) was under Soviet Influence.

Conclusion

The Second World War had been more terrible and destructive than the first. Some 60 million people died in the war, including about 20 million military personnel and 40 million civilians. Many of the civilians died because of deliberate genocide, massacres, mass bombings, disease, and starvation. World War II proved the latin proverb "homo homini lupus": Man is a wolf to man.

Six million Jews were killed by the Nazis during the Holocaust, many by starvation and many through a chillingly planned effort of extermination in death camps. These death camps can be distinguished from concentration camps or labor camps in that their primary purpose was extermination of Jews, communists, homosexuals, disabled people, and others that the Nazis deemed unfit.

More than a million British-Indians died, mainly due to famine (Bengal famine of 1943) that could have been avoided if the British had redistributed food. A famine in Vietnam (1944–45) who were at the time under Japanese control, killed a million or more.

World War II saw modern industrial nations, which are supposed to represent the best of the Enlightenment and the Scientific Revolution, descend into unimaginable cruelty. While it’s tempting to say that World War II was about the Allies fighting for democratic ideals against the totalitarian, militaristic, imperialism of the fascist Axis powers, it just doesn’t hold up to scrutiny. For instance, a hugely important Allied power, Stalin’s Soviet Union, was the least democratic place, ever. And, by far, the biggest imperialists of the war were the British.

Aftermath

When war broke out in 1939, the League of Nations closed down and its headquarters in Geneva, which remained empty throughout the war. United Nations (UN) was established on 24 October 1945 which succeeded the ineffective League of Nations.

A denazification programme in Germany led to the prosecution of Nazi war criminals and the removal of ex-Nazis from power.

In the aftermath of World War 2, two superpowers with two very different ideologies had come out victorious and the tension between the two of them would create a new kind of war, a very cold one. ( read more on cold war. )
