French Revolution (1789-1799)

The French Revolution was a watershed event in modern European history that began in 1789 and ended in the late 1790s with the ascent of Napoleon Bonaparte. The Revolution was caused by widespread discontent with the French monarchy and the poor economic policies of King Louis XVI.

Background

France in the start of the 18th century was a rich and populous country, but it had a systemic problem collecting taxes because of the way its society was structured.

French society in the eighteenth century followed a political and social system called 'Ancien Regime' (Old Order) according to which the society was divided into three estates with everyone being a subject to the King.

  1. The First Estate included the Clergy, i.e., the Church and its members.
  2. The Second Estate included the Nobility, i.e., the rulers and other feudal lords.
  3. The third Estate included everone else, for example; businessmen, merchants, lawyers, artisans, servants, peasants etc. which constituted more than ninety per cent of the entire population.

Only the members of the third estate were required to pay taxes. This was bad because the people with the money -the nobles and the clergy - never paid taxes.

By 1789, France was deeply in debt thanks to their funding the American Revolution that helped thirteen American colonies gain their independence from Britain. This added more than a billion livres to a debt that had already risen to more than 2 billion livres. Lenders who gave the state credit, now began to charge 10 per cent interest on loans. King Louis XVI was spending half of his national budget to repay the federal debt.

To meet its regular expenses, such as the cost of maintaining an army, the court, running government offices or universities, the state had only one solution: tax the third estate. The state levied two types of taxes:

  1. a direct tax, called taille and
  2. a number of indirect taxes which were levied on articles of everyday consumption like salt or tobacco.

There's also labour tax where peasants were required to work a certain number of days for their local lord without pay. The Church too extracted its share of taxes called tithes from the peasants.

This unfortunately coincided with hailstorms that ruined a year’s harvest, thereby raising food prices and causing widespread hunger and a subsistence crisis. Meanwhile, the King and his wife Marie Antoinette (an Austrian princess) led an extravagant lifestyle in the palace of Versailles. The upper classes had massive stocks of grain, wheat etc. So they were virtually untouched by this crisis.

The Emergence of an Intellectual Middle Class

The eighteenth century france witnessed the emergence of social groups, termed the middle class, which included rich businessmen, manufacturers, lawyers and administrative officials. Most of them were educated and believed that no group in society should be privileged by birth. Rather, a person’s social position must depend on his merit.

These ideas envisaging a society based on freedom and equal laws and opportunities for all, were put forward by philosophers such as John Locke and Jean Jacques Rousseau .

  • John Locke in his Two Treatises of Government, sought to refute the doctrine of the divine and absolute right
  • Jean Jacques Rousseau proposed a form of government based on a social contract between people and their representatives.
  • Montesquieu in his The Spirit of Laws, proposed a division of power within the government between the legislative, the executive and the judiciary, which was received well in both Great Britain and the American colonies.

The Outbreak of the Revolution

Basically, the peasants were hungry and started rioting, the intellectuals were questioning the unjust and unequal treatment, and the nobility were failing to make any meaningful financial reform.

In response to the crisis, Louis XVI called a meeting of the Estates General which hadn’t met since 1614. The Estates General was like a parliament (but merely an advisory body) made up of representatives from the First Estate, the clergy, the Second Estate, the nobility, and the Third Estate, everyone else.

On 5 May 1789, the assembly of the Estates General took place to find an amicable solution to the ongoing crisis. A resplendent hall in Versailles was prepared to host the delegates. The Third Estate showed up with about 600 representatives, the First and Second Estates both had about 300. Voting in the Estates General was based on the principle that "each estate had one vote".

The third estate knew that any reform proposed by them would be out-voted by the first and second estates. So, they demanded that voting now be conducted by the assembly as a whole, where each member would have one vote. When the king rejected this proposal, members of the third estate walked out of the assembly in protest and decided to form their own National Assembly.

The Third Estate representatives assembled in an indoor tennis court where they swore the famous Tennis Court Oath (on 20th June 1789) pledging to not to give up until a French constitution was established.

Composition of the National Assembly

The members of the National Assembly were of two type:

  1. The Moderates who wanted to create a constitutional monarchy with equal rights for everyone. They believed that the king was necessary for a functioning state.
  2. The radicals who called for the creation of a republic i.e., the removal of the King altogether. The most radical wing was called the Jacobins under the leadership of Maximilian Robespierre. They are particularly inspired by the writings of Jean-Paul-Marat and his newspaper the 'Friend of the People'.

While the National Assembly was busy at Versailles drafting a constitution, the rest of France was in turmoil. A severe winter caused a bad harvest; the price of bread rose, often bakers exploited the situation and hoarded supplies. After spending hours in long queues at the bakery, crowds of angry men and women stormed into the shops.

Louis XVI responded by sending troops to Paris primarily to quell uprisings over food shortages, but the revolutionaries saw this as a provocation, so they responded by seizing the Bastille Prison on July 14th 1789, which was later called the Bastille Day. The Bastille was stormed to free seven prisoners and to get gunpowder.

The wave of revolutionary fervor and widespread hysteria quickly swept the countryside. Revolting against years of exploitation, peasants looted and burned the homes of tax collectors, landlords and the elite.

Known as the Great Fear, the agrarian insurrection fuelled the exodus of nobles from the country and inspired the National Constituent Assembly to abolish feudalism on August 4, 1789, signing what the historians call the “death certificate of the old order.”

Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen

In August of 1789, the Assembly adopted the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, , a statement of democratic principles grounded in the philosophical and political ideas of Enlightenment thinkers like Jean-Jacques Rousseau.

Article Content
Article I Men are born and remain free and equal in rights.
Article II The goal of any political association is the conservation of the natural and imprescriptible rights of man. These rights are liberty, property, safety and resistance against oppression.
Article III The source of all sovereignty resides in the nation; no group or individual may exercise authority that does not come from the people.
Article IV Liberty consists of the power to do whatever is not injurious to others
Article V The law has the right to forbid only actions harmful to society.
Article VI Law is the expression of the general will. Every citizen has a right to participate personally, or through his representative, in its foundation. It must be the same for all, whether it protects or punishes.
Article VII No man can be accused, arrested or detained, except in cases determined by the law.
Article VIII The law should establish only penalties that are strictly and evidently necessary, and no one can be punished but under a law established and promulgated before the offense and legally applied.
Article IX All persons are held innocent until they have been declared guilty, if the arrest is deemed indispensable (necessary), the law prohibits any unnecessary harshness in arresting that person.
Article X Freedom of speech and expression including profession of religious beliefs, provided that their manifestation does not trouble the public order established by the law.
Article XI Every citizen may speak, write and print freely; he must take responsibility for the abuse of such liberty in cases determined by the law
Article XII The guarantee of the rights of man and of the citizen requires a public force. These forces are established for the good of all and not for the personal advantage of those to whom they shall be intrusted.
Article XIII For the maintenance of the public force and for the expenses of administration, a common tax is indispensable; it must be assessed equally on all citizens in proportion to their means.
Article XIV All the citizens have a right to decide, either personally or by their representatives, as to the necessity of the public contribution; to grant this freely; to know to what uses it is put; and to fix the proportion, the mode of assessment and of collection and the duration of the taxes.
Article XV The society has the right of requesting an account from any public agent of its administration.
Article XVI Any society in which the guarantee of rights is not assured, nor the separation of powers determined, has no Constitution at all.
Article XVII Since property is an inviolable and sacred right, no one shall be deprived thereof except where public necessity, legally determined, shall clearly demand it, and then only on condition that the owner shall have been previously and equitably indemnified.

On October of 1789, a rumor started that Marie Antoinette was hoarding grain somewhere inside the palace of Versailles. In what became known as the Women's March, a bunch of armed peasant women stormed the palace and demanded that Louis and Marie Antoinette move from Versailles to Paris, which they did. The King also agreed to share his power with the revolutionary government (i.e., the National Assembly).

France Becomes a Constitutional Monarchy

The National Assembly completed the draft of the constitution in 1791. Its main object was to limit the powers of the King who was made a constitutional head. These powers instead of being concentrated in the hands of one person, were now separated and assigned to different institutions – the legislature, executive and judiciary. This made France a constitutional monarchy.

Ancient Régime was abolished - feudal rights, tithes, privileges for nobles, unequal taxation were eliminated.

The Constitution of 1791 vested the power to make laws in the National Assembly, which was indirectly elected. That is, citizens voted for a group of electors, who in turn chose the Assembly. Not all citizens, however, had the right to vote. Only men above 25 years of age who belonged to the highest bracket of taxpayers were entitled to vote.

The Constitution began with a Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. Rights such as the right to life, freedom of speech, freedom of opinion, equality before law, were established as ‘natural and inalienable’ rights, that is, they belonged to each human being by birth and could not be taken away. It was the duty of the state to protect each citizen’s natural rights.

The radicals such as those in the Jacobian club, were outraged that the new constitution did not remove the King entirely.

France Abolishes Monarchy and Becomes a Republic

France’s monarchical neighbors were getting nervous about france becoming a republic fearing those revolutionary ideas might spread to their nations. Leopold II, the emperor of Austria who was also Marie Antoinette’s brother and King William Frederick II of Prussia (Germany) decided to restore the French monarchy.

Before that could happen, the National Assembly voted in April 1792 to declare war against Prussia and Austria. Thousands of volunteers thronged from the provinces to join the army. They saw this as a war of the people against kings and aristocracies all over Europe. Among the patriotic songs they sang was the Marseillaise (composed by the poet Claude Joseph Rouget de Lisle) which is now the national anthem of France.

In the summer of 1792 the Jacobins planned an insurrection of a large number of Parisians who were angered by the war time miseries of short supplies and high prices of food. On the morning of August 10 they stormed the Palace of the Tuileries in Paris, massacred the king’s guards. The King and his family take refuge in the Legislative Assembly.

The Assembly voted to suspend the monarchy, have new elections in which everyone could vote (as long as they were men), and create a new republican constitution. The newly elected assembly was called the National Convention. It abolished the monarchy and declared France a republic.

Soon, the assembly had a trial for Louis XVI, who was found guilty and was sentenced to die via guillotine on 21 January 1793. The queen Marie Antoinette met with the same fate shortly after.

The Reign of Terror

The death of Louis XVI marks the beginning of the period called The Terror (1793 to 1794). The radical Jacobians under Robespierre followed a policy of severe control and punishment. All those whom he saw as being ‘enemies’ of the republic – ex-nobles and clergy, members of other political parties, even members of his own party who did not agree with his methods – were arrested, imprisoned and then tried by a revolutionary tribunal. If the court found them ‘guilty’ they were guillotined.

Robespierre pursued his policies so relentlessly that even his supporters began to demand moderation. Finally, he was convicted by a court in July 1794, arrested and sent to the guillotine.

His death marked the beginning of the Thermidorian Reaction, a moderate phase in which the French people revolted against the Reign of Terror’s excesses.

A Directory Rules France

Some historians believe that the french revolution ended with the Robespierre's execution in 1794. While some believe that it lasted till 1799 when Napoleonic era began.

After the Terror, the revolution pulled back a bit and another new constitution was put into place, this one giving a lot more power to wealthy people. At this point, France was still at war with Austria and Britain, wars that France ended up winning, largely thanks to a corporal named Napoleon Bonaparte.

In November 1799, Napoleon Bonaparte engineered a coup and established himself as the First Consul of France which granted him almost unlimited executive power under yet another constitution.

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769 – 1821)

In 1804, Napoleon Bonaparte crowned himself Emperor of France. He set out to conquer neighbouring European countries, dispossessing dynasties and creating kingdoms where he placed members of his family. Napoleon saw his role as a moderniser of Europe. He introduced many laws such as the protection of private property and a uniform system of weights and measures provided by the decimal system.

Initially, many saw Napoleon as a liberator who would bring freedom for the people. But soon the Napoleonic armies came to be viewed everywhere as an invading force.

He was finally defeated at Waterloo in 1815 and was exiled to island of Saint Helena in the Atlantic Ocean. Many of his measures that carried the revolutionary ideas of liberty and modern laws to other parts of Europe had an impact on people long after Napoleon had left.

Conclusion: Was French Revolution, a success?

It's not easy to conclude that the French Revolution was a success since Napoleon was basically a dictator and, in some ways, he was even more of an absolute monarch than Louis XVI had been. Gradually the nobles came back to France, although they had mostly lost their special privileges. The Catholic Church returned, too, although much weaker because it had lost land and the ability to collect tithes.

And when Napoleon himself fell in 1815, France restored the monarchy. Granted they were no longer absolute monarchs who claimed that their right to rule came from God; they were constitutional monarchs of the kind that the revolutionaries of 1789 had originally envisioned. But the fact remains that France had a king again, and a nobility, and an established religion and it was definitely not a democracy or a republic.

But it's definitely not a Failure either, the revolution succeeded in spreading enlightenment ideals and insisted on the universality of these ideals. Look at Article 6 of the Declaration of Rights of Man and Citizen: “Law is the expression of the general will. Every citizen has a right to participate personally, or through his representative, in its foundation. It must be the same for all, whether it protects or punishes.” For that period in time, those were radical ideas, that the laws come from citizens, not from kings or gods, and that those laws should apply to everyone equally.

So one might conclude that even though, the short-term achievements of the revolution are not that impressive, the long-term effect it had on the rest of world is truly remarkable.
