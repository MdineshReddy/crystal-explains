Cold War

The Cold War was an ideological conflict between the USSR which stood for communism and the USA which stood for capitalism, that played out globally. It is believed to be started on 12 March 1947 with Truman Doctrine and ended on 26 December 1991 with the Dissolution of the Soviet Union.

All the Countries were grouped into three groups:

  • First World (or) Western/Free/Capitalist/American Bloc: led by the USA.
  • Second World (or) Eastern/Communist/Socialist/Soviet Bloc: led by the Soviet Union (USSR).
  • Third World (or) Non-Aligned (or) Neutral Countries

Background

United States and Soviet Russia fought on the same side during the World War 2. Towards the end of the war, The alliance between the Western Allies and the Soviet Union had begun to deteriorate.

Stalin’s distrust of the U.S. and Britain kept growing as they refused to invade Europe and open up a second front against the Nazis. And some even say that the decision to drop the first Atomic Bombs on Japan was motivated in part by a desire to intimidate the Soviets. Soviets were not intimidated but motivated to develop atomic bombs of their own which they successfully tested in 1949.

U.S. feared that the Soviets wanted to destroy democratic and capitalist institutions. And the Soviets feared that the US wanted to use its money and power to dominate Europe and eventually destroy the Soviet system.

After World War 2, Germany, along with its capital Berlin was divided into two parts. The East Germany came under the Soviet Influence.

Immediately after World War 2, the Soviets created a sphere of influence in eastern european nations of East Germany, Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, and Albania, which is why Winston Churchill famously said in 1946 that an “Iron Curtain” had descended across Europe. The relationship between the old Allies was deteriorating fast.

Cold War

Truman's Doctrine

Truman Doctrine announced on 12 March 1947 by the U.S President Harry S. Truman. It called for a policy of containment which basically involved stopping the spread of communism by standing up to the Soviets wherever they seemed to want to expand.

In Europe this meant spending a lot of money. First the Marshall Plan spent $13 billion on re-building western Europe with grants and credits that Europeans would spend on post-war re-construction. This plan led to the establishment of Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) in 1961.

Truman doctrine also lead to military containment by the creation of North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) in 1949, whereby its independent member states agree to mutual defence in response to an attack by any external party.

Marshall Plan and Comecon

Under Marshall plan, the US government gave to Western European countries over $13 billion to rebuild their economy. The plan's aim was to rebuild the democratic and economic systems of Europe and to counter perceived threats to Europe's balance of power, such as communist parties seizing control through revolutions or elections.

One month later, Truman signed the National Security Act of 1947, creating a unified Department of Defense, the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA), and the National Security Council (NSC). These would become the main bureaucracies for US defense policy in the Cold War.

If the Western nations developed faster and better than the East, that would be a defeat for Stalin. So he set up his own rival economic recovery plan in 1949 called Molotov Plan which was eventually expanded to become the Comecon (Council for Mutual Economic Assistance).

This was a full-on economic battle raging between capitalism and communism in Europe. But nowhere did this economic battle rage harder than in the city of Berlin. The city had been divided up between the Allies. And the western segments were still under Western control. East Berliners could travel freely to West Berlin and envy the economic prosperity of the West.

Espionage

The two sides were spying on each other a whole lot throughout the Cold War. The most famous and active organizations were the American CIA, the Soviet KGB, and the British MI6. Espionage took place all over the world, but Berlin was the most important battleground for spying activity.

The KGB had spies and informants in nearly every aspect of Western life and government. Members of the Manhattan Project (that developed atom bomb) aided the Soviet Union in building its own atom bomb.

The CIA was also involved in European politics and elections where communists had a chance, as in Italy. US had an edge over the Soviet Union in its espionage technology. In particular, U-2 spy planes flew across Russia carrying out surveillance from the skies.

Berlin Blockade and Airlift

In 1947, US, UK and later France (1949) decided to unite their part of occupation zones in the western half of Germany into Trizonia or West Germany. Shortly thereafter, Stalin instituted the Berlin Blockade (24 June 1948 – 12 May 1949), one of the first major crises of the Cold War, preventing food, materials and supplies from arriving in West Berlin.

In response, the US, Britain, France, Canada, Australia, New Zealand and several other countries began the massive "Berlin airlift", supplying West Berlin with food and other provisions. The Berlin Airlift was an incredible undertaking and a major success for the Western Allies. Stalin ended his blockade of West Berlin (12 May 1949).

soviet atomic bomb project

On 29 August 1949, the Soviet Union secretly conducted its first successful weapon test in a test site in Kazakh SSR. The USA no longer had a nuclear monopoly. The world now knew that if a major war broke out between the two superpowers, it would be more destructive than anyone could imagine.

China Becomes Communist

After China lost the Opium War against British in 19th Century, resulting in European domination, all of which was deeply embarrassing to the Qing dynasty and led to calls for reform.

One strand of reform that called for China to adopt European military technology and education systems was called self-strengthening, But it didn't happen. Instead, China experienced an anti-Western Boxer Rebellion of 1900, which helped spur some young liberals, including one named Sun Yat Sen to plot the overthrow of the dynasty.

Sun Yat Sen is called the "Father of the Nation" in the Republic of China (now Taiwan), and the "Forerunner of the Revolution" in the People's Republic of China for his instrumental role in the overthrow of the Qing dynasty during the Xinhai Revolution.

The 1911 revolution (Xinhai Revolution) led to the end of the Qing dynasty. The Qing emperor abdicated and Sun Yat Sen was declared president of a provisional government of the Republic of China in 1912. After the first elections, Sun Yat Sen’s party, the Kuomintang (KMT) (Nationalist Party) were the largest, but they weren’t the majority. So, Sun Yat Sen turned power over to to Yuan Shikai (a General), which turned out to be a huge mistake because he abolished the national and provincial assemblies and declared himself as the emperor of China in late 1915.

But when Yuan Shikai died in 1916, creating a power vacuum; This opened the way for the Warlord Era, during which much of China was ruled by shifting coalitions of competing provincial military leaders.

The leaders of Kuomintang and of Chinese Communist Party (CCP) formed an alliance to regain power from those big landlords and reunify China. But then Sun Yat Sen died in 1925, and one of his proteges, Chiang Kai-shek, seized control of the Nationalist Party. The alliance between the nationalists and communists fell apart in 1927 which started a civil war which lasted till 1949. In the end, The CCP, under the leadership of Mao Zedong, successfully defeated the Kuomintang, who retreated to Taiwan.

The CCP was left in control of mainland China. On 1 October 1949, Mao Zedong proclaimed the People's Republic of China. Soviet Union promptly created an alliance with the newly formed People's Republic of China.

Korean War

After the Second World War, Korea was divided into two parts. In the north, the Soviets set up a communist regime. In the south, America set up an anti-communist regime. North was under Kim Il-sung and South was under Rhee Syng-Man. Both were dictators and both dreamed of reuniting Korea under their own regime.

The North launched a surprise invasion of the South on June 25th, 1950. With Soviet aid, the North Koreans steamrolled through, taking Seoul in just three days. The UN created an emergency force made up of troops from 16 countries to defend the south. They pushed the North Koreans out of Seoul and continued all the way up the Korean Peninsula at which point, China joined on the Side of North Korea. They pushed the West right back almost to the exact same spot they had all started from, and ended up in a stalemate.

In the north, Kim Il Sung created a highly centralized, totalitarian dictatorship that accorded his family unlimited power. In the South, the American-backed dictator Syngman Rhee ran a violently anticommunist and authoritarian regime. While Rhee was overthrown in 1960, South Korea continued to be ruled by a military government of former Japanese collaborators until the re-establishment of a multi-party system in the late 1980s.

Khrushchev, Eisenhower, De-Stalinization, Warsaw Pact and Sino-Soviet split

Famed World War II general, Dwight D. Eisenhower, was elected as new U.S President in January, 1953. Two Months later Stalin died. The new Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev went on a campaign of de-Stalinization from 1956. Statues of Stalin were taken down, Stalingrad was renamed Volgograd, and Khrushchev announced that he wanted the Soviet people to be happy and would allow greater freedom in the Soviet Union. He permitted more cultural expression, but then began banning stuff based on his own personal taste.

West Germany was allowed to join NATO in 1955. Soviets set up the defensive Warsaw Pact as a balance of power or counterweight to NATO, by strengthening the military ties between the Soviet Union and its satellite states. There was no direct military confrontation between them; instead, the conflict was fought on an ideological basis and in proxy wars.

After 1956, the Sino-Soviet alliance began to break down. Mao had defended Stalin when Khrushchev criticized him in 1956. A bitter rivalry started between the Soviets and Mao's China for leadership of the global communist movement.

Space Race

Much to America's concern, the Soviet Union appeared to be ahead in the space race. In August 1957, the Soviets successfully launched the world's first intercontinental ballistic missile (ICBM), and in October they launched the first Earth satellite, Sputnik 1. The launch of Sputnik inaugurated the Space Race. Then in 1961, Yuri Gagarin, a soviet pilot and cosmonaut (astronaut) became the first human to journey into outer space, achieving a major milestone in the Space Race.

Kennedy and Berlin Crisis of 1961

John F. Kennedy became president of the United States in 1961.

The contrast between the economically prosperous West and the struggling East was clearer day-by-day and East Berliners were still able to freely travel to the West. Many of them were deciding to stay there. Millions defected to West Germany via West Berlin, causing Eastern factories to lose workers, and taking a heavy toll on the economy.

Khrushchev came up with a new idea to build a wall. On August 13th, 1961, Berliners woke up to find their city divided into two; with barbed wire and guards blocking the border between East and West. Over time, a wall (Berlin Wall) was constructed in its place across the city.

Families were torn apart. Thousands would risk their lives escaping over the wall and hundreds would die trying. To the despair of Berliners, the West were unable to do anything about it. But the wall did put on full display the failure of the communist system. As Kennedy said (June 26, 1963): "Freedom has many difficulties and Democracy is not perfect, but we have never had to put a wall up to keep our people in."

Cuban Missile Crisis (October Crisis of 1962), Kennedy's Assassination and Khrushchev's ousting

In Cuba two young socialist revolutionaries Fidel Castro and Che Guevara seized power toppling the elected President in 1959. Cuba began negotiating for arms purchases from the Eastern Bloc in March 1960. In January 1961, just prior to leaving office, Eisenhower formally severed relations with the Cuban government and gave approval to CIA plans and funding to overthrow Castro.

Kennedy wanted to conceal any US involvement in the overthrowing plan. So he massively scaled back American air support, and as a result the Bay of Pigs Invasion was a humiliating defeat for America. Kennedy administration continued seeking ways to oust Castro experimenting with various ways of covertly facilitating the overthrow of the Cuban government.

Khrushchev made preparations to install Soviet nuclear missiles in Cuba, to which Castro agreed. Alarmed, Kennedy responded with a naval blockade. The US navy announced it would stop and search any Soviet ships heading to Cuba. And would sink any that did not comply. In response, the Soviet put its military into full combat readiness. The US did the same, and began drawing up plans for an attack on Cuba. Things were escalating fast, and both superpowers were getting ready for nuclear annihilation.

A Soviet nuclear submarine in the Caribbean mistakenly believed war had already broken out and two of the senior officers gave the go-ahead to fire its nuclear torpedo. Thankfully the third senior officer, Vasili Arkhipov, refused to authorise the decision.

After much deliberation between the Soviet Union and Kennedy's cabinet, Kennedy pledged not to invade Cuba again and secretly agreed to remove all missiles set in Turkey on the border of the Soviet Union, in exchange for Khrushchev removing all missiles in Cuba.

On November 22, 1963, President Kennedy was assassinated by Lee Harvey Oswald. In 1964, Khrushchev's Kremlin colleagues managed to oust him and replaced him with Leonid Brezhnev.

Lyndon B. Johnson, Richard Nixon and Vietnam War (1955-1975)

After Kennedy's assassination, Vice President Lyndon B. Johnson took over. And he inherited a developing crisis in Vietnam. Instead of direct conflict, the US and Soviet Union involved in a lot of Proxy wars, famous of which is the Vietnam war.

After the French were ousted from Vietnam in 1950s, it was divided into two. In the North, a communist regime and in the South, an anti-communist regime. North Vietnam was under Ho Chi Minh and South was under Ngo Dinh Diem. They both dreamed of reuniting Vietnam under their own regime.

The North established the National Liberation Front, also known as the Vietcong, to carry out a campaign of guerrilla warfare in the South with Soviet support. The US sent advisors to help train the South Vietnamese to deal with the threat, but President Diem's brutal policies pushed more and more South Vietnamese to support the Vietcong.

And over the next decade, the situation escalated to a breaking point. America feared the Domino Effect, that if South Vietnam fell to communism, the neighbouring nations would also move towards communism. So, the US got bogged down in one of its most brutal and longest wars while the Soviets assisted the North Vietnamese army in the Viet Cong.

Richard Nixam became president in 1968. As the Vietnam War appeared to be increasingly unwinnable, and public opinion turning increasingly sour, Nixon made the decision to begin bringing the troops home, and ended US involvement in Vietnam by 1973. Two years later, the war ended with the capture of Saigon, the capital of South Vietnam by the People's Army of Vietnam (PAVN) and the Viet Cong on 30 April 1975.

Detente

The Cold War was now taking its toll on both superpowers. In Russia, a huge percentage of the budget was still going to the military and not towards the people. Both sides needed to reduce spending in order to rescue their economies.

Both Nixon and Brezhnev proclaimed a new era of "peaceful coexistence" and established the groundbreaking new policy of détente (or cooperation) between the two superpowers.

To improve relations, Nixon became the first US President to visit Moscow in 1972. And Brezhnev returned the favour a year later. A number of treaties were signed, including the 1972 SALT I (Strategic Arms Limitation Talks) agreement, that limited anti-ballistic missiles and nuclear missiles.

Relations with China were also improving via Ping-Pong Diplomacy, when the US table tennis team went on a tour of the People's Republic. Nixon even visited China in 1972 and met Mao Zedong.

Watergate scandal

Period between 1972 to 1974 saw a major Political scandal involving the administration of U.S. President Richard Nixon. Nixon was accused of abusing his power and ordering men to break into the Democratic National Committee headquarters to wiretap the offices and dig up dirt on opponents for the reelection campaign.

During the impeachment process, Nixon resigned from office on August 9, 1974. He remains the only U.S. president to have resigned from office. On September 8, 1974, Nixon's successor, Gerald Ford, pardoned him.

New Cold War (1979–1985)

The term new Cold War refers to the period of intensive reawakening of Cold War tensions and conflicts in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Tensions greatly increased between the major powers with both sides becoming more militant.

Gerald Ford was followed by Jimmy Carter who was followed by Ronald Reagan as the new US President (1981). Both Reagan and new British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher denounced the Soviet Union and its ideology. Reagan labeled the Soviet Union an "evil empire" and predicted that Communism would be left on the "ash heap of history," while Thatcher inculpated the Soviets as "bent on world dominance."

Reagan wanted to renew the arms race using technological advances in computing. The Soviet Union did not respond by further building its military, because the enormous military expenses, along with inefficient planned manufacturing and collectivized agriculture, were already a heavy burden for the Soviet economy.

On 1 September 1983, the Soviets shot down a Korean airliner that had strayed into their airspace. And it looked like the world was going right back to mid-20th-century Cold War tension. NATO coordinated exercise to simulate a nuclear conflict. It was perhaps the most dangerous moment since the Cuban Missile Crisis, as the Soviet leadership feared that a nuclear attack might be imminent.

Brezhnev died in 1982, and was ultimately replaced by Mikhail Gorbachev in 1985.

Gorbachev's reforms

Gorbachev's philosophy differed a lot from previous Soviet leaders. He felt that the reason the Soviet system and economy was struggling was that it didn't allow the Soviet people to find satisfaction in their work. Because they weren't allowed to speak freely, and lived in fear. Gorbachev wanted the Soviet people to be happy. But unlike previous Soviet leaders, he actually made the change happen.

He announced an agenda of economic reform called perestroika, or restructuring. Perestroika allowed private ownership of businesses and paved the way for foreign investment. He simultaneously introduced glasnost, or openness, which increased freedom of the press and the transparency of state institutions. Glasnost led to more information from the west and less censorship led to a flood of criticism as people realized how much poorer the second world was than the first

He also knew that the arms race needed to end in order to rescue the Soviet economy, and a positive relationship with the West must be established.

Reagan's tone towards the Soviet Union began to soften, and he agreed to renew talks on economic issues and the scaling-back of the arms race. Constructive dialogue reopened and resulted in the INF Treaty, which saw all intermediate-range missiles eliminated.

Eastern Europe breaks away

For decades, the Soviets had been brutally repressing any attempt at change in their satellite states in Eastern Europe. But now Gorbachev was prepared to let the Eastern Bloc choose its own future.

And one by one, often quite suddenly, former communist states collapsed. The Iron Curtain was unravelling. In a historic movement, the Berlin Wall came down in November 1989, which symbolized the collapse of European communist governments. After decades of family separation and travel restriction, the people were allowed to pass through. East and West Berliners couldn't believe it, and celebrated together throughout the night. Some even climbed the wall, and began to topple it. The Iron Curtain had fallen and in 1990, Germany was reunited.

In Poland, the anti-Soviet party, Solidarity, won 99 out of 100 seats in the 1989 election. Hungary held multiparty elections in 1990. Elections in Bulgaria, a peaceful revolution in Czechoslovakia, and a violent one in Romania, brought to an end communist authority in the Eastern Bloc.

Soviet dissolution

In the USSR itself, glasnost weakened the ideological bonds that held the Soviet Union together. People wanted an end to the communist single-party rule and Gorbachev had to give in.

For the first time in history, elections were held in which candidates not officially endorsed by the party were allowed to run. Ambitious rival of Gorbachev, Boris Yeltsin led a growing democratic movement and won the democratic presidential elections for the Russian republic.

The Hard-line Communist opponents of Gorbachev's reform program, angry at the loss of control over East European satellites, and fearful of the new union treaty that was about to be signed which would decentralize much of the central government's power to the 15 republics. So they planned a coup (Augusty Coup 1991).

The hard-liners were very poorly organized. They met defeat by a short but effective campaign of civil resistance mainly in Moscow. It was led by Russian president Boris Yeltsin. The coup collapsed in only two days and Gorbachev returned to office, while all the plotters lost office.

Yeltsin became the dominant leader and Gorbachev lost much of his influence. The failed coup led to both the immediate collapse of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union and the dissolution of the USSR on 26 December 1991.
