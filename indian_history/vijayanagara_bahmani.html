VIJAYANAGARA AND BAHMANI

The Vijayanagara Kingdom (1336-1650 AD)

  • The Vijayanagara Kingdom (also known as Karnata Rajya) constituted four dynasties —
    • Sangama (1336–1485 AD),
    • Saluva (1485–1503 AD),
    • Tuluva (1503–1570 AD) and
    • Aravidu (till the end of the 17th century).
  • It included people from different cultural regions (the Tamil, Telugu and Kannada regions) who all spoke different languages and belonged to different cultures.
  • In the south, Vijayanagara’s main rivals were the Sultans of Madurai and the struggle between them continued for almost four decades. By c.1377 CE, the Sultanate of Madurai was wiped out.
  • In the north, they had continuous hostility with the Bahmani Sultanate.

The Sangamas (1336-1485 AD)

Harihara I and Bukka Raya I (1336–1377 AD)

  • Founders of the Sangama dynasty of Vijayanagar kingdom.
  • Originally feudatories of the Kakatiyas of Warangal, Muhammad bin Tughlaq imprisoned the two brothers and were converted to Islam.
  • Later, they returned to the Hindu fold at the initiative of the saint Vidyaranya. They also proclaimed their independence and to commemorate the memory of their guru, the brothers founded a new city of Vidyanagar or Vijayanagara (meaning city of victory) on the south bank of river Tungabhadra.
  • The decline of the Hoysala kingdom enabled Harihara and Bukka raya to expand their newly founded kingdom.
  • Bukka raya succeeded his brother in c.1356 and ruled till 1377 CE. During his reign, the Sultanate of Madurai was eliminated. He also defeated the chiefdom of Arcot (Tamil Nadu), the Reddys of Kondavidu (AP), and gained control over Goa.

Harihara II (1377–1406 AD)

  • Harihara II, the second son of Bukka Raya I, further consolidated the kingdom beyond the Krishna River.

Deva Raya I (1406–1422 AD)

  • The next ruler, Deva Raya I, emerged successful against the Gajapatis of Odisha and undertook important works of fortification and irrigation.
  • He constructed a dam across river Tungabhadra to bring canals into the city to relieve the shortage of water.
  • Early in his career, he was defeated by the Bahmani ruler Firoz Shah and had to not only pay a huge indemnity but also had to marry his daughter to the Sultan. In c. 1420 CE, Firoz Shah invaded Pangal, which had been taken by Vijayanagar, but this time Deva Raya inflicted a shattering defeat on Firoz Shah. The Sultan had to hand over the southern and eastern districts of his kingdom to Deva Raya I. Consequently, by c. 1422, Deva Raya I came to control territory up to the Krishna– Tungabhadra doab including Pangal.
  • Srinatha was his court poet, who wrote Haravilasam, a poetic composition in Telugu. He was widely regarded as the Kavi Sarvabhowma (The emperor among poets).
  • Italian traveller, Nicolo de Conti (in c.1420), and Russian merchant Nikitin (author of Voyage to India), visited during his reign and wrote memoirs about him.
  • The noted Hazara Rama temple , an excellent example of Deccan architecture, was constructed during his rule.

Hazara Rama Temple, Hampi, Karnataka

Deva Raya II (1425–46 AD)

  • He was the greatest Sangama ruler. He wrote Mahanataka Suddhanidhi.
  • He took the tittle ‘Gaja Betekara’ i.e the elephant hunter.
  • Persian Ambassador Abdul Razzaq, the envoy of Shahrukh (of Timurid Empire, Persia), visited his court.

Later rulers (Mallikarjuna Raya, Virupaksha Raya II ) of the Sangamas were weak and much of Karnataka was last to the Bahamani Sultan.

Saluva Dynasty (c.1486–1503 CE)

  • The next dynasty, Saluva founded by Saluva Narasimha, the governor of Virupaksha Raya II.
  • Vasco da Gama came to India (Calicut) during this period (1498 AD).
  • Saluva Narasimha left his two adolescent sons under the care of general Tuluva Narasa Nayaka who ably defended the kingdom from their traditional enemies, the Gajapati king and the Bahamani Sultan.

Tuluva Dynasty (1503–1570 AD)

Vir Narasimha (1503-1509): In 1503, Narasa Nayaka's son Vira Narasimha had prince Immadi Narasimha of the Saluva dynasty assassinated and took over the rule and thus become the first of the Tuluva dynasty rulers.

Krishna Deva Raya (1509–29 AD)

  • The empire came under the rule of Krishna Deva Raya in 1509, another son of Tuluva Narasa Nayaka.
  • The greatest of the Vijayanagar rulers, was also known as ‘Abhinava Bhoja’, ‘Andhra Pitamah’, ‘Andhra Bhoja’ (as he was great patron of literature and art).
  • The empire reached its peak during the rule of Krishna Deva Raya when Vijayanagara armies were consistently victorious.
  • He was a great commander and an efficient administrator. He fought a series of war with the independent kingdoms (Deccan Sultanates) that came up on the ruins of the Bahmani kingdom.
  • He maintained friendly relations with the Portuguese. King Albuquerque sent his ambassadors to the court of Krishna Deva Raya. The Portuguese travellers Domingo Paes and Barbosa came to India during his reign.
  • Though a Vaishnavaite, he respected all religions. Eight eminent scholars of Telugu known as the ‘Ashta diggajas’ adorned his court, famous of which are:
    • Allasani Peddanna (also called Andhrakavita Pitamaha); his important works include Manucharitam (or Manu Sambhavam) and Harikathasaram.
    • Tenali Ramakrishna, author of Panduranga Mahamatyam. which is considered as one of the Pancha Maha Kavyas (the Five Great Kavyas) of Telugu literature.
    • Pingali Suranna, author of Garuda Puranam.
  • He himself authored Amukthamalyadha (Telugu), Jambavati Kalyanam and Ushaparinayam (Sanskrit).
  • Many important monuments were either completed or commissioned during the time of King Krishnadevaraya. He built some great stone temples such as famous the Vittalaswamy temple and restored the Hazara Ramaswamy temple at Vijayanagar.
  • The famous temple of Tirupati greatly developed during his period.

Achyuta Deva Raya (1529 –1542 AD)

  • Was the younger brother of Krishna Deva Raya.
  • He patronized the Kannada poet Chatu Vittalanatha, the great singer and composer Purandaradasa (father of Carnatic music), and the Sanskrit scholar Rajanatha Dindima II.
  • The Tiruvengalanatha temple, which is now popularly known by his name as the Achyutaraya temple, was built in Vijayanagar during his reign.

Sada Siva Raya (1542–1570 AD)

  • Krishna Deva Raya’s son. He was the last ruler of Tuluva dynasty.

Battle of Talikota (in Karnataka) (AD 1565): Between the alliance of Ahmednagar, Bijapur, Golkonda and Bidar at one side and Vijayanagara on the other side. Vijayanagara was defeated.

The Aravidus (1570-1650 AD)

  • The dynasty was founded by Thirumala Deva Raya. He shifted the capital to Penukonda (Ananthapur) and and later to Chandragiri (near Tirupati) for about another century.

Note: The Nayankara System

  • Under this system, military chiefs were assigned certain pieces of land. These chiefs, known as nayaks, had revenue and administrative rights on their lands.
  • They were required to maintain elephants, horses and soldiers in certain numbers, which were included in the royal army during wars. They also had to pay a sum of money to the central exchequer.
  • In course of time, the nayaks began to assert their military, administrative and economic powers, which later became a major cause of the decline of the Vijayanagara empire.

The Bahmani Kingdom

  • The Bahmani kingdom of Deccan was founded by Hasan Gangu (or Bahaman Shah). The capital was Gulbarga in Karnataka.
  • Firoz Shah (1397-1422) was the most remarkable figure in the Bahmani kingdom. He was determined to make Deccan the cultural center of India. He inducted Hindus in his administration to large extent. He built an observatory at Daulatabad. He founded the city of Firozabad on the bank of the river Bhima. Firoz defeated Devaraya I.
  • Firoz Shah was succeeded by his brother Ahmed Shah I (AD 1422-36). He shifted his capital from Gulbarga to Bidar, also in Karnataka.
  • After the break-up of Bahmani kingdom, separate Muslim states were formed as follow:
    • Nizam Shahis of Ahmednagar (Maharashtra): founder-Ahmed Nizam Shah, later annexed by Shahjahan.
    • Adilshahis of Bijapur (1490-1686) founded by Yusuf Adil Shah. It was annexed by Aurangazeb. A ruler Muhammad Adil Shah built the Gol Gumbaz.

      • Gol Gumbaz, Karnataka

    • Imad Shahis of Berar (1490-1574) founded by Fateullah Khan Immad-ul-Mulk with Daulatabad as capital. Later, it was conquered and annexed by one of the Nizam Shahi rulers of Ahmednagar.
    • Qutub Shahis of Golconda (1518-1687) founded by Quli Qutub Shah. He built the famous Golconda fort and made it his capital. He founded the city of Hyderabad . He built the famous Charminar. Most important port of Qutub Shahi kingdom was Masulipatnam. The kingdom was annexed by Aurangzeb (1687).
