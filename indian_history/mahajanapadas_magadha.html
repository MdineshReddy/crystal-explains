THE MAHAJANAPADAS AND THE MAGHADA

The Mahajanapadas

  • The sixth century BCE is known as an era of ‘Second Urbanisation’ in the Indian Subcontinent. It also marked the beginning of NBPW (Northern Black Polished Ware) culture.
  • After the decline of the Harappan towns and nearly after a gap of more than a thousand years, urban centres emerged again but not in the Indus plain. The centre of economic and political activity shifted from the North-West, Punjab, Haryana, and Western UP, to Eastern UP and Bihar (Ganga Basin).
  • This region was not only fertile on account of better rainfall and river systems, but was also closer to the iron production centres.
  • The use of better iron tools and weapons enabled some territorial states to become very large, and they came to be called Mahajanapadas.
  • According to Buddhist texts, Anguttara Nikaya, the land between Himalayas and Narmada was divided into 16 independent states (Mahajanapadas):

Mahajanapada States

  • The most powerful states of them were Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa, and Avanti. Two of the Mahajanapadas, the Vajji and Malla were Gana-Sanghas. Unlike kingdoms, the Gana-Sanghas (literally meaning 'assembly') had no king, instead they are governed by a small group of people (notably the rich, also known as oligarchy).

Rise of the Magadha

  • The political conflict among the Mahajanapadas ultimately led to the emergence of Magadha as the most powerful state and the centre of a vast empire.
  • Magadha’s rise to political supremacy began with Bimbisara, who belonged to the Haryanka dynasty.

Cause of the Rise of Magadha

  • Advantageous geographical location of the capital cities.
  • Abundance of natural resources such as iron, helped in preparing effective weapons.
  • The alluvial soil of the Gangetic plains and sufficient rainfall aided for surplus agriculture produce.
  • Unorthodox character of Magadhan society and ambitious rulers.

Haryanka Dynasty

Bimbisara

  • He was contemporary of Buddha and Mahavira.
  • He had his capital at Rajgriha (Girivraja), which was an impressive city and almost impregnable as surrounded by five hills.
  • Earlier, he had rivalry with Avanti’s king Pradyota, but later became friends and Bimbisara even sent his royal physician Jivaka to Ujjain (along Dakshinapatha to Avanti) when Pradyota had jaundice.
  • He conquered Anga (East Bihar). Anga, and especially its capital, Champa, were important for inland and maritime trade.
  • He strengthened his position by three matrimonial alliances:
    • His first wife was from Kosala named Mahakoshala (sister of King Prasenjit), who brought in dowry the territory of Kashi.
    • He married lichchhavi Princess Chellana (sister of Vajji King Chetaka) from Vaishali, who gave birth to Ajatashatru.
    • He married the daughter of the chief of the Madra clan (Punjab).
  • He was the first Indian King to have a regular and standing army.

Ajatashatru

  • He was the son of Bimbisara. It is believed that he seized the throne by killing his father.
  • He annexed Kosala (ruled by his maternal uncle Prasenjit) and Vajji (ruled by Chetak, his maternal grandfather).
  • He used two innovative military weapons: War engine, which was used to throw stones like catapults (Mahashilakantaka). Chariot with mace for mass-killing (Rathamusala).
  • Buddha died during his reign. 
  • He arranged the first Buddhist council.

Udayin

  • He was the son and successor of Ajatashatru.
  • He founded the new capital at Pataliputra (modern Patna), situated at the confluence of Ganga and Son rivers.
  • He was succeeded by weak rulers and most of them were parricides (i.e., killed their parent(s)).

Shishunaga Dynasty

Shishunaga

  • Shishunaga was earlier a Viceroy/Amatya (high ranking official) at Varanasi (Banaras), and it is believed that the people of Magadha, outraged by the successive parricides of the successors of Udayin, appointed Shishunaga as king.
  • He temporarily shifted capital to Vaishali.
  • The most important achievement of Shishunaga was to defeat Avanti (Pradyota Dynasty) and make it a part of Magadha.

Kalashoka

  • He was the son and successor of Shishunaga.
  • During his reign, second Buddhist Council was held at Vaishali.

Nanda Dynasty (First non-Kshatriya dynasty) (344 BC-321 BC)

Mahapadmananda

  • The Shishunaga dynasty was overthrown by Mahapadma who established a new line of kings known as the Nandas. It is considered to be the first non-Kshatriya dynasty.
  • The first empire builder of India, and claimed to be the ekarat, the sole sovereign who destroyed all other ruling princes.
  • He was also called Ugrasena in Pali texts, because of his large army.
  • He was believed to be belonged to a non-Kshatriya caste. The Nandas were considered adharmika (those who do not follow the norms of Dharma). He was also called Sarvashatrantak, i.e., uprooter of all the Kshatriyas (Puranas)
  • He added Kalinga to Magadha and brought an image of Jina as a victory trophy.
  • He made methodical collection of taxes by regularly appointing officials. He also built canals and did a lot of irrigation work

Dhanananda (The Last Nanda Ruler)

  • He was called Agrammes or Xandrames by the Greek historians who credited him with having a huge army.
  • It is believed that Alexander attacked Punjab during his reign but his army checked them from advancing towards Magadha.
  • He is believed to be an arrogant and oppressive ruler who imposed heavy taxes on the common man. His greed and exploitation made him quite unpopular among the masses.
  • Ultimately Chandragupta Maurya under the able guidance of Kautilya, took the advantage of this public resentment and uprooted the Nanda rule and set up the Maurya empire in 321 BCE.

Foreign Invasions

Iranian Invasions

  • Cyrus of Persia was the first foreign conqueror, who penetrated well into Indian Subcontinent. He enrolled Indian soldiers in the Persian Army.
  • Darius–I, grandson of Cyrus invaded North-West India in 516 BC and annexed Punjab, West of Indus and Sind.
  • Xerxes, the successor of Darius, also employed Indians in the long war against the Greeks.
  • Impact of Iranian Invasions:
    • Iranian contact gave an impetus to Indo-Iranian trade.
    • There was cultural exchange in the form of Kharoshthi script from Iran to India. Some of Ashoka’s inscriptions in North-West India were written in this script.
    • Iranian influence is perceptible on Mauryan sculpture, e.g., the bell-shaped capitals.

Alexander’s Invasion

Alexander The Great

  • Alexander, the king of Macedonia brought the mighty Persian (Iran) empire to its knee. From Iran, he marched towards India, attracted by its great wealth and divided polity.
  • Alexander conquered Kabul in 328 BC. He moved to India through the unguarded Khyber Pass in 326 BC.
  • Ambhi, the ruler of Taxila, readily submitted to Alexander. He got the first and strongest resistance from Porus.
  • Alexander defeated Porus in the Battle of Hydaspes on the banks of the river Hydaspes (Jhelum), but was impressed by his bravery, so he restored to Porus, his kingdom and made him his ally.
  • Alexander proceeded up to Beas with a view to conquer the East, but his fatigued army refused to cross the river.
  • So, he was forced to retreat. He placed the North-Western India under the Greek Governor Selucus Nikator.
  • He remained in India for 19 months (326-325 BC) and died in Babylon (323 BC) at the age of 33 years.
  • Impact of Alexander’s Invasion:
    • Opening up of new trade routes between North-West India and Western Asia.
    • Indians learnt from the Greeks in the fields of coinage, astronomy, architecture and sculpture (Gandhara school).
    • The vacuum left by him, helped the Mauryans, to incorporate North-West India into the Mauryan empire with ease.
    • His historians have left valuable accounts related to Indian geography, social and economic conditions, which enable us to build the Indian chronology of the times.
