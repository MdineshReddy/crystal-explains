The Extremist Phase (1905-1920)

The period from 1905 to 1920 in the Indian National movement was known as extremist phase as this period was dominated by the extremists or the aggressive nationalists who believed that success could be achieved through any means.

Cause for the Rise of Extremists

  • The failure of Moderates to achieve any substantial outcome and dissatisfaction with their methods.
  • Growing consciousness about the exploitative character of the British rule.
  • Impact of growth of educationâ€”increase in awareness and unemployment.
  • Reaction to Increasing Westernisation
  • Reactionary policies of Lord Curzon such as the
    • Calcutta Corporation Act (1899), which reduced the strength of elected members from India, thereby giving British a majority.
    • the Official Secrets Act (1904), to muzzle the voice of nationalist publications.
    • partition of Bengal (1905).

Ideology and Methods of the Extremists

Ideology

  • Hatred for foreign rule
  • They believed in the capacity of the masses
  • Swarajya as goal. Unlike the Moderates, the Extremists had no faith in the British. They wanted complete independence and not just self-government.
  • They advocated direct political action and self-sacrifice.

Methods

  • Promotion of Swadeshi goods and Boycott of foreign goods.
  • Non Co-operation with Britishers (Passive Resistance).

Extremist Leaders

Lala Lajpat Rai (1865-1928)

  • referred to as the Punjab Kesari (The Lion of Punjab)
  • He was a veteran leader of the Indian Nationalist Movement, Indian independence movement led by the Indian National Congress, Hindu reform movements and Arya Samaj.
  • He cofounded the Punjab National Bank (1894) as a part of the Swadeshi movement.
  • He led a peaceful protest march against the all-British Simon Commission in 1928. The police superintendent in Lahore, James A. Scott, ordered the police to lathi charge the protesters and personally assaulted Rai.
  • Despite being severely injured, Rai subsequently addressed the crowd and said "I declare that the blows struck at me today will be the last nails in the coffin of British rule in India". He did not fully recover from his injuries and died on 17 November 1928.
  • Bhagat Singh who was a witness to the event, swore to avenge the death of Rai, who was a significant leader of the Indian independence movement.

Bal Gangadhar Tilak (1856-1920)

  • referred to as Lokmanya Tilak, he was from Bombay. Lokmanya means "accepted by the people (as their leader)",
  • Tilak was one of the strongest advocates of Swaraj ("self-rule") and a strong radical in Indian consciousness.
  • He is known for his quote in Marathi: "Swarajya is my birthright and I shall have it!" .

Bipin Chandra Pal (1858 - 1932)

  • Bipin Chandra Pal was an Indian nationalist from the Bengal Presidency.
  • He was one of the main architects of the Swadeshi movement.

Note: Lala Lajpat Rai, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, and Bipin Chandra Pal were collectively referred to as a Lal Bal Pal triumvirate.

Other Prominent Leaders included Sir Aurobindo Ghosh, , Tanguturi Prakasham Pantulu (Andhra Kesari) etc.

Bengal Partition and the Swadeshi Movement

  • Through a royal proclamation, Lord Curzon ordered Partition of Bengal on 16th October, 1905. The idea was to have two provinces:
    • Hindu-majority province of Western Bengal including the provinces of Bihar and Orissa
    • Muslim-majority province of Eastern Bengal and Assam.
  • The British said that the existing province of Bengal was too big to be efficiently administered by a single Provincial Government.
  • However, the real objective was to set up a communal gulf between the Hindus and Muslims.
  • The Indian National Congress and the nationalists of Bengal firmly opposed the Partition. Within Bengal, different sections of populationâ€” zamindars, merchants, lawyers, students and even women rose up in spontaneous opposition to the partition of their province.
  • Divide and Rule: The nationalists could see that it was a deliberate attempt to divide the Bengalâ€™s territory on religious grounds as for Eastern part, Muslims will be in majority and for the Western part, the Hindus.

Anti-Partition Campaign

  • The Anti-Partition movement was initiated on 7th August, 1905. On that day, a massive demonstration against the partition, was organised in the Town Hall in Calcutta.
  • Rabindranath Tagore composed the National Song Amar Sonar Bangla for the occasion. This song was adopted as National Anthem by Bangladesh in 1971, after its liberation from Pakistan.
  • Calcutta Session of INC (1906) Dadabhai Naoroji, the President of the session, declared that Self Government or Swaraj, like that of United Kingdom was the goal of Indian people.

The Swadeshi and Boycott (1905-1908)

  • It had its origin in the Anti-Partition movement of Bengal. Mass meetings were held all over Bengal, where Swadeshi or the use of Indian goods and the boycott of British goods and also the social boycott of any Indian who used foreign goods were proclaimed and pledged.
  • Public burning of foreign cloth were organised and shops selling foreign cloths were picketed.
  • An important aspect of the Swadeshi Movement was the emphasis placed on self reliance or Atmashakti.
  • The Swadeshi Movement had several consequences like flowering of nationalist poetry, journalism, self-reliant and opening many national educational institutions (e.g., Bengal Technical Institute, Bengal National College.).
  • BC Pal and Chidambram Pillai led Vandemataram Movement in Madras.
  • Extent of mass participationâ€”students, women, certain sections of zamindari, labour, some lower middle and middle classes in towns and cities participated for the first time while the Muslims generally kept away.

Why Swadeshi Movement Failed?

  • Severe government repression.
  • Split in nationalists at Surat.
  • Lack of effective organisation.
  • With arrest/deportation of all leaders, the movement left leaderless.

Formation of Muslim League

  • All-India Muslim League (popularised as the Muslim League) was established in 1906 in Dhaka.
  • The League supported Partition of Bengal, opposed the Swadeshi Movement, demanded special safeguards for its community and separate electorate for Muslims.

Surat Split (1907)

  • The INC splited into two groups during the session at Surat in 1907. Extremists were led by Lal, Bal, Pal, while Moderates were led by GK Gokhale.
  • Moderates demanded mild constitutional reforms, economic relief, administrative reorganisation and protection of civil rights. They wanted to restrict the Boycott Movement to Bengal and to a boycott of foreign cloth and liquor.
  • Extremists
    • They were dissatisfied with the achievement of the moderates.
    • They wanted to take the movement to all parts of the country and include within its ambit all forms of association with the government through a boycott of schools, colleges, law courts, legislative councils, government service, municipalities, etc.
    • There were 3 groups of extremists The Maharashtrian group (headed by Bal Gangadhar Tilak), the Bengal group (represented by BC Pal and Aurobindo) and the Punjab group (led by Lala Lajpat Rai).

Indian Councils Act of 1909 or Morley-Minto Reforms

(John Morley was the then secretary of state for India, and Minto was the then viceroy)

  • Number of elected members in the central and provincial legislative councils increased.
  • Introduced a system of communal representation for Muslims by accepting the concept of â€˜separate electorateâ€™. Lord Minto came to be known as the Father of Communal Electorate.
  • Non-official members to be elected indirectly. Thus, election was introduced for the first time.
  • Legislatures could pass resolutions, ask questions and supplementaries and vote for separate items on the budget.
  • One Indian to be taken in Viceroyâ€™s executive council. Satyendra Sinha was first Indian member to the executive council (as a law member).

Annulment of Partition

  • The partition of Bengal was annuled/revoked in 1911 and announced at the Delhi Durbar at which King George V came in person and was crowned Emperor of India.
  • The reason given was to curb the menace of revolutionary terrorism. The annulment came as a rude shock to the Muslim political elite.
  • George V also announced the capital would be moved from Calcutta to Delhi as a way to please the Muslims, as it was associated with Muslim glory, but the Muslims were not pleased.
  • Bihar and Orissa were taken out of Bengal and Assam was made a separate province.

First World War and Nationalist Response

In the First World War (1914-1919), Britain allied with France, Russia, USA, Italy and Japan against Germany, Austria-Hungary and Turkey. This period saw the maturing of Indian nationalism. The nationalist response to British participation in the First World War was three-fold:

  • the Moderates supported the empire in the war as a matter of duty.
  • the Extremists, including Tilak supported the war efforts in the mistaken belief that Britain would repay Indiaâ€™s loyalty with gratitude in the form of self-government and
  • the revolutionaries decided to utilise the opportunity to wage a war on British rule and liberate the country.

Home Rule League Movement

  • It was the manifestation of a trend of aggressive politics in national movement.
  • Pioneered by Tilak and Annie Besant on lines of a similar movement in Ireland.
  • Aim of the Movement: To convey to the common man the concept of Home Rule as self-government.
  • Tilakâ€™s Leagueâ€”Started in April 1916 and operated in Maharashtra, Karnataka, Central Provinces and Berar; had six branches. He gave the slogan â€œSwaraj is my birth right and I shall have it." Tilakâ€™s newspapers Maratha and Kesari were organs for home rule.
  • Besantâ€™s Leagueâ€”Started in September 1916 and operated in rest of India; had 200 branches. Many moderate nationalists, who were dissatisfied with the Congress inactivity, joined home rule agitation.
  • Methods used: Organising discussions, reading rooms, propaganda through public meetings, newspapers, pamphlets, posters, etc.
  • Positive Gains: Emphasis shifted to the masses permanently; organisational link established between town and country; prepared a generation of ardent nationalists, influenced Moderate-Extremist reunion at Lucknow (1916)

Lucknow Session of the Congress (1916)

  • Extremists were readmitted to Congress.
  • Muslim League and Congress put up joint demands under Lucknow Pact . Through the pact, the two parties agreed to allow representation to religious minorities in the provincial legislatures.
  • Congress accepted the Leagueâ€™s position on separate electorates.

Montague Declaration (1917)

  • A British policy was announced after the Lucknow pact, which came to be known as the August Declaration.
  • It aimed at increasing association of Indians in every branch of the administration for progressive realisation of responsible government in India.
  • The declaration formed the basis of Montague-Chelmsford Reforms, of 1919.

Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (1919)

  • This Act is also known as Government of India Act of 1919
  • The Central subjects were demarcated and separated from those of the Provincial subjects.
  • Provincial subjects were further divided into two partsâ€“ transferred and reserved. The transferred subjects were to be administered by the Governor with the aid of Ministers responsible to the legislative council. The reserved subjects, on the other hand, were to be administered by the Governor and his executive council without being responsible to the legislative council. This dual scheme of governance was known as â€˜dyarchyâ€™.
  • It introduced, for the first time, bicameralism in centre and direct elections in the country. Thus, the Indian legislative council was replaced by a bicameral legislature consisting of an Upper House (Council of State) and a Lower House (Legislative Assembly). The majority of members of both the Houses were chosen by direct election.
  • It required that the three of the six members of the Viceroyâ€™s Executive Council (other than Commander-in-Chief) were to be Indians.
  • It extended the principle of communal representation by providing separate electorates for Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians and Europeans.
  • It provided for the appointment of a statutory commission to inquire into and report on its working after ten years of its coming into force.

Drawbacks

  • Dyarchy arrangement too complex and irrational to be functional.
  • Central executive not responsible to legislature.
  • Limited franchise.
