PRE-HISTORY

The term History is derived from the Greek word, historia, meaning "inquiry". Herodotus, a 5th-century BC Greek historian known for writing The Histories- a detailed account of the Greco-Persian Wars, is considered (atleast within the Western tradition) to be the "father of history", as he was the first writer to treat historical subjects using a method of systematic investigation.

Historical Divisions

History is divided into three categories pre-history, proto-history, and history.

Pre-History

Pre-History refers to the period before writing was known. Hence, Pre-History can also called Pre-Literary History. Since there are no written records, this period is constructed from the material remains like tools etc. In India, Pre-Historic period comprises of the three stone ages.

Proto-History

Proto-History is a period between prehistory and history, during which a culture or society has not yet developed writing but are mentioned in the written records of other literate civilizations. For Example:

  • There are traces of a script (found on different seals) discovered at different sites of Harappan civilization. But the script has not yet been deciphered so far. However Mesopotamian writings mentions trade with the Harappan civilisation. Therefore, Harappan civilization has been considered a part of proto-history.
  • There was no consensus among historians whether vedic period is a part of proto-history or not. Although there are definite written records belonging to this period, some historians argue that those were written long after they had been orally transmitted for ages.

History

History refers to the study of socities, past the invention of writing. Their history is constructed using both the written and archaeological sources.

The Stone Ages

The early man in India used tools made of crude stone. This period is therefore, known as the Stone Age, which has been divided into three phases:

  • The Palaeolithic or Old Stone Age
  • The Mesolithic or Middle Stone Age
  • The Neolithic or New Stone Age

The Palaeolithic Age (5,00,000 BCE - 10,000 BCE)

  • The Palaeolithic Age is the earliest period of the Stone Age, which developed in the Pleistocene period or the Ice Age (a geological period where the earth was covered with ice and weather was cold).
  • The Palaeolithic man of India are believed to have belonged to the Negrito race and lived in caves and rock shelters. They did not have knowledge of houses, pottery, agriculture. It was only in later stages they discovered fire.
  • The human beings living in the Paleolithic Age were essentially hunters and gatherers and depended on nature for food.

The Mesolithic Age (10,000 BCE âˆ’ 6000 BCE)

  • It was a transitional phase between the Palaeolithic Age and the Neolithic Age. The climate became warmer during this period.
  • Both Mesolithic and Neolithic Cultures belong to the Holocene era (which succeeded the Pleistocene era around 10,000 years ago).
  • The Mesolithic man lived on hunting, fishing, food gathering, and in later stages, also domesticated animals.
  • The characteristic tools of the Mesolithic Age are microliths. Microliths were small tools made of stone.
  • Important Sites include:
    • Adamgarh in Madhya Pradesh and Bagor in Rajasthan provide the earliest evidence for the domestication of animals.
    • Though pottery is absent at most Mesolithic sites, they have been found in Langhnaj (Gujarat) and in the Kaimur region of Mirzapur (U.P).
    • Bhimbetka in Madhya Pradesh is a striking site of Pre-historic painting of Mesolithic age.

The Neolithic Age (6,000 BCEâˆ’ 1800 BCE)

  • The people of this age are characterised by the use of polished stone tools. They particularly used stone axes.
  • The Neolithic settlers were the earliest farming communities. They produced ragi and horse-gram (kulathi). Neolithic sites in Allahabad district (Uttar Pradesh) are noted for the cultivation of rice in the sixth millenium BC.
  • The people of Neolithic age lived in rectangular or circular houses which were made of mud and reed (grass).
  • Hand made pottery and use of potter wheel first appeared during the Neolithic age.
  • Due to such innovations, Neolithic phase was termed as Neolithic Revolution by V. Gordon Childe, an eminent Australian archaeologist.
  • Important Sites include:
    • Burzahom in Jammu and Kashmir famous for pit dwelling, stone tools, and domestic dogs buried with their masters in graves.
    • Maski and Brahmagiri in Karnataka.
    • Paiyampalli in Tamil Nadu and Utnur in Andhra Pradesh.
    • Garo Hills in Meghalaya, Chirand in Bihar.
    • Mehrgarh is the earliest Neolithic site known as the Breadbasket of Baluchistan, a province of Pakistan.
    • Belan Valley, on the northern spurs of the Vindhyas in Uttar Pradesh, and the middle part of the Narmada valley shows evidence of all the three phases of Paleolithic settlement, followed by Mesolithic and Neolithic settlements.
    • Koldihwa, in Belan valley shows presence of three phases of Neolithic, Chalcolithic and Iron Age settlements.

Chalcolithic Age / Copper-Stone Age

  • The end of the Neolithic period saw the use of metals along with stone tools. Copper was the first metal to be used, though they also occasionally used bronze.
  • The chalcolithic age largely applied to the pre-Harappan phase, but in many parts of the country, it appears after the end of the bronze Harappan culture.
  • Chalcolithic people were primarily rural communities. They domesticated animals and practised agriculture. They were not acquainted with burnt bricks and lived in thatched (roofs made of dry grass) houses.
  • They used different types of pottery, of which black and red pottery was the most popular.
  • The Chalcolithic people were expert coppersmiths.
  • Important sites include: Brahmgiri (near Mysore) and Navada Toli on the Narmada.
