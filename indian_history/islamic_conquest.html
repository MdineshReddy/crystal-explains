The Islamic Conquest of India

The Arabs

Muhammad bin Qasim

  • In 712 AD, Arabs invaded India from the north-west region (Sindh) under Muhammad bin Qasim, who was a general of the Umayyad caliphate (of Damascus, Syria).
  • He was the first Muslim to have successfully captured Hindu territories and initiate the early Islamic India in 712 AD.
  • Though his rule lasted only for two years, yet it greatly disturbed the economic life of Sindh and a large number of people and merchants fled from Sindh. However, many Arabs settled down in Sindh and the Arab influence continued for a long period.

The Turks

  • The Turks had entered the Abbasid empire (of Baghdad, Iraq) as palace guards and mercenary soldiers but they soon emerged as king-makers.
  • Towards the close of the 9th century AD, the vast empire of Arab broke up and the Turks who got the upperhand over the Caliphate of Baghdad, established many independant principalities. One of such Turk state was Ghazni (in Afghanistan).
  • Subuktigin, the ruler of Ghazni of that period, fought with Jaipala (of Hindu Shahi dynasty), the ruler of western Punjab and defeated him.

Mahmud of Ghazni

  • Mahmud Ghazni, the eldest son of Subuktigin, ascended the throne in AD 998. He was the first ruler to hold the title Sultan ("authority").
  • For India, the only memory of Mahmud is that of a plunderer and destroyer of temples. Mahmud is said to have made 17 raids into India. The initial raids were directed against the Hindu Shahi rulers. In 1001 AD, the Hindu Shani ruler Jaipala was again defeated. This time by Mahmud.
  • Jaipala's son Anandapala succeeded the throne. A decisive battle between Mahmud and Anandapala was fought in 1008-09 AD at Waihind during his sixth expedition. Anandapala lost the war.
  • The objective of Mahmud’s expeditions was to plunder and loot. He was not interested in expanding his empire to India. He plundered Somnath temple (in Gujarat) in 1025 AD (his sixteenth raid).
  • He patronised:
    • Al Biruni – the scholar from central Asia, and the composer of the Kitab Tarikh Ul-Hind (History of India) or simply Kitab-ul-Hind in which he talks about India, expecially about religions in India.
    • Firdausi – The Persian poet called the Homer of the East, writer of the Shahnama("Book of Kings"), which is regarded as the national epic of Iran. It tells mainly the mythical and to some extent the historical past of the Persian Empire from the creation of the world until the Muslim conquest in the seventh century.
  • Utbi was his court historian, who wrote Kitab-i-Yamini, a book on history of the reigns of Subuktigin and Mahmud.
  • In c.1030 CE, Mahmud died, and following his death a powerful empire called the Seljuk empire came into being.
  • Though the 17 invasions of India undertaken by Ghazni did not show any systematic effort to conquer the sub-continent, yet they proved very costly to Indian sub-continent. His conquest opened the gates of India to be conquered from the north-west and this made it easier for other Afghan and Turkish rulers to enter India into the Gangetic valley at any time. One such ruler was Muhammad Ghori.

Muhammad Ghori

  • Towards the middle of the 12th century, another group of Turkish tribe man shattered the power of the Seljuk Turks called the Ghurids.
  • The Ghurids or Ghorids were a dynasty from the Ghor region of present-day central Afghanistan.
  • Many historians assert that the actual founder of the Islamic Empire in India was Muhammad Ghori, who was the Sultan of the Ghurid empire.
  • In 1173,Muhammad Ghori ascended the throne at Ghazni. He conquered Multan and upper sindh.
  • In 1178, he attempted to penetrate into Gujarat by marching across the Rajputana desert, but was completely rooted out by the Solanki (Gujarat) ruler.
  • He realised the necessity of creating a suitable base in Punjab before venturing upon the conquest of India. He conquered Peshawar, Lahore and Sialkot.
  • At that period, the Chauhan power under Prithviraj Chauhan had been steadily growing. Chauhans had captured Delhi from the Tomars around the middle of the century.
  • Both Prithviraj and Muhammad Ghori’s attention towards the Punjab and Ganga valley brought the two ambitious rulers into conflict.
    • First Battle of Tarain (Haryana) (1191): The Ghori forces were completely rooted out by Prithviraj.
    • Second Battle of Tarain (c.1192 CE): considered turning point in Indian history, as Prithviraj Chauhan was defeated and Ghori emerged successful. Prithviraj was taken prisoner and was later executed.
  • The defeat laid the foundation of Muslim rule in India.
  • Bakhtiyar Khilji one of Ghori’s commanders, annexed Bihar and Bengal and destroyed Nalanda and Vikramshila Universities.
  • Muhammad Ghori died in AD 1206, leaving the charge with Qutub-ud-din Aibak, a turkish slave who laid the foundation of the Slave Dynasty.
