THE BRITISH CONQUEST OF INDIA

The Conquest of Bengal

  • Bengal was the richest province of the Mughal Empire which included present day Bangladesh. Its Nawab had authority over the region constituting present day states of Bihar and Odisha as well.
  • In 1700, Murshid Quli Khan became the Governor of Bengal. He was succeeded by his son-in-law, Shujauddin who ruled till 1739. After that, for a year (1739-40), Sarfaraz Khan, an incapable son of Murshid Quli Khan, became the ruler; he was killed by Alivardi Khan.
  • Alivardi Khan ruled till 1756 and also stopped paying tributes to the Mughal emperior. He was succeeded by his grandson, Siraj-ud-daula, the son of his youngest daughter.
  • Almost all the governors of Bengal strongly resented the special privileges enjoyed by the English company as it meant a huge loss to the State.
  • The Battle of Plassey
    • Cause of the Battle: The officials of the Company made rampant misuse of its trade privileges that adversely affected the nawab’s finances. The English fortified Calcutta without the nawab’s permission. Mention may be made here of the much propagated ‘Black Hole Tragedy’. In June of 1756 the British governor of Calcutta, Roger Drake made the mistake of insulting the emissary sent by the Nawab Siraj ud-Daulah who duly besieged and captured the British garrison. Drake escaped to nearby ships with the town's women and children. But the towns defenders remained and the survivors were imprisoned in a small windowless room that came to be known as the Black Hole and 40 of 63 prisoners suffocated overnight. This story is mostly famous because the British press exaggerated the numbers in order to build support for the war in India.
    • The Battle: The arrival of a strong force under the command of Robert Clive at Calcutta from Madras strengthened the English position in Bengal. Clive forged a secret alliance with Mir Jafar, husband of Alivardi Khan’s sister. Under the deal, Mir Jafar was to be made the nawab who in turn would reward the Company for its services. Due to this conspiracy , the 50,000-strong force of Siraj was defeated by a handful of Clive’s forces.
    • Significance of the Battle: The Battle of Plassey placed at the disposal of the English vast resources of Bengal. After Plassey, the English virtually monopolised the trade and commerce of Bengal. It laid the foundation of the British empire in India. Their main rivals, the French, were ousted.
  • Mir Jafar was increasingly irritated by the interference of Clive. He entered into a conspiracy with the Dutch at Chinsura (Hooghly). But the Dutch were defeated and humbled by the English forces at Bedara/Chinsura/Hooghly.
  • The treachery of Mir Jafar and his failure to make the payments due to the Company, annoyed the English. Under the pressure of the Company, Mir Jafar decided to resign in favour of Mir Kasim/Qasim, the son-in-law of Mir Jafar.
  • After assuming power, Mir Kasim shifted the capital from Murshidabad (West Bengal) to Munger in Bihar.
  • The Battle of Buxar
    • Cause of the battle: The misuse of the Company’s dastak or trade permit (a permit which exempted the goods specified from payment of duties) by Company officials resulted in loss of tax revenue to the nawab. By an imperial farman, the English company had obtained the right to trade in Bengal without paying transit dues or tolls. However, the servants of the Company also claimed the same privileges for their private trade. The Company’s servants also sold dastak to Indian merchants for a commission.
    • The Battle: The combined armies of Mir Kasim, Nawab of Bengal; the Nawab of Awadh Shuja-ud-Daula; and the Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II, were defeated by the English forces under Major Hector Munro at Buxar in 1764.
    • The war had been brought to an end by the Treaty of Allahabad in 1765. Under this treaty, Shah Alam II granted the East India Company Diwani rights, or the right to collect taxes on behalf of the Emperor from the eastern province of Bengal-Bihar-Orissa.
  • After the battle of Buxar, the East India Company became the real masters of Bengal. Robert Clive introduced the dual system of government, i.e., the rule of the two—the Company and the Nawab—in Bengal in which both the diwani, i.e., collecting revenues, and nizamat, i.e., police and judicial functions, came under the control of the Company. The system held a great advantage for the Company. It left the appearance of authority to the puppet Indian ruler, while keeping the sovereign power in the hands of the Company.
  • The dual system led to an administrative breakdown and proved disastrous for the people of Bengal. Neither the Company nor the Nawab cared for administration and public welfare. In 1770, Bengal suffered from a famine which is regarded as one of the most terrible famines in human history. Warren Hastings did away with the dual system in 1772.

The Conquest of Mysore

  • After the battle of Talikota (1565) gave a deadly blow to the great kingdom of Vijayanagara, many small kingdoms emerged from its remnants. In 1612 a Hindu kingdom under the Wodeyars emerged in the region of Mysore.
  • During the second half of the 18th century, Mysore emerged as a formidable power under the leadership of Haidar Ali and his son Tipu Sultan.
  • The English felt their political and commercial interests in south India was threatened because of Mysore’s proximity with the French and Haidar Ali and Tipu’s control over the rich trade of the Malabar coast.
  • First Anglo-Mysore War (1767-69)
    • Cause of the war: The British convinced the Nizam of Hyderabad to attack Hyder Ali.
    • The War: The Nizam, the Marathas, and the English allied together against Haidar Ali. Haidar acted with considerable tact and diplomatic skill. He paid the Marathas to turn them neutral and, promising to share conquered territories with the Nizam, converted the Nizam into his ally. Hyder's attack towards Madras resulted in the Madras government suing for peace, and the resultant Treaty of Madras.
    • Treaty of Madras: Haidar Ali was promised the help of the English in case he was attacked by any other power.
  • Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780-84)
    • Cause of the war: Hyder felt Treaty of Madras was broken when he didn't receive any help when Mysore went to war with the Marathas in 1771.
    • The War: Haidar forged an anti-English alliance with the Marathas and the Nizam. Sir Eyre Coote, the British commander defeated Hyder Ali at the Battle of Porto Novo. Haidar Ali died of cancer on December 7, 1782. Now his son, Tipu Sultan, carried on the war for one year without any positive outcome. Fed up with an inconclusive war, both sides opted for peace, negotiating the Treaty of Mangalore (March, 1784) under which each party gave back the territories it had taken from the other.
  • Third Anglo-Mysore War (1790-92)
    • Cause of the war: Tipu Sultan, the ruler of Mysore and an ally of the french, invaded the nearby state of Travancore in 1789 which was a British ally.
    • British forces were commanded by Governor-General Cornwallis himself. The resultant war lasted three years and was a resounding defeat for Mysore.
    • Treaty of Seringapatam: The war ended after the 1792 Siege of Seringapatam (Srirangapatnam) and the signing of the Treaty of Seringapatam, according to which Tipu had to surrender half of his kingdom to the British East India Company and its allies. Besides, a war damage of three crore rupees was also taken from Tipu. Half of which was to be paid immediately while the rest was to be given in installments, for which Tipu’s two sons were taken as hostages by the English.
  • Fourth Anglo Mysore War (1799)
    • Cause of the war: Tipu fulfilled all the terms of the Treaty of Seringapatam and got his sons released. In 1796, when the Hindu ruler of Wodeyar dynasty died, Tipu refused to place Wodeyar’s minor son on the throne and declared himself sultan. He also decided to avenge his humilitating defeat and the terms put by the Treaty of Seringapatam. Lord Wellesley, the new Governor General, was concerned about Tipu’s growing friendship with the French and aimed at annihilating Tipu’s independent existence or force him to submission through the system of Subsidiary Alliance.
    • The War: The war began on April 17, 1799 and ended on May 4, 1799 with the fall of Seringapatam. The English were again helped by the Marathas and the Nizam. The Marathas had been promised half of the territory of Tipu and the Nizam had already signed the Subsidiary Alliance. Tipu laid down his life fighting bravely. The English chose a boy from the earlier Hindu royal family of Mysore as the maharaja and also imposed on him the subsidiary alliance system.

Anglo-Maratha Wars

This part continues from where we left of in The Marathas Article.

First Anglo-Maratha War (1775-82)

Cause of the War

  • After the death of Peshwa Madhavrao I in 1772, his brother Narayanrao became peshwa of the Maratha Empire.
  • Narayanrao was murdered by his palace guards in August 1773, and his uncle Raghunathrao became Peshwa.
  • However, Narayanrao's wife, Gangabai, gave birth to a son, who was the legal heir to the throne. The newborn infant was named 'Sawai' Madhavrao .
  • Twelve Maratha chiefs, known as the Baarbhai led by Nana Phadnavis, directed an effort to install the infant as the new Peshwa and to rule in his name as regents.
  • Treaties of Surat: Raghunathrao, unwilling to give up his position in power, sought help from the English at Bombay and signed the Treaty of Surat in 1775. Under the treaty, Raghunathrao ceded the territories of Salsette and Bassein to the English along with a portion of the revenues from Surat and Bharuch districts. In return, the British promised to provide Raghunathrao with 2,500 soldiers.
  • Treaty of Purandhar: The British Calcutta Council, on the other side of India, condemned the Treaty of Surat (1775) and made a new treaty (Treaty of Purandhar, 1776) with the regency renouncing Raghunath and promising him a pension. The Bombay government rejected this and gave refuge to Raghunath. In 1777, Nana Phadnavis violated his treaty with the Calcutta Council by granting the French a port on the west coast. The English retaliated by sending a force towards Pune.

The War

  • Though the Maratha army had a larger number of soldiers than the English, the latter had highly superior ammunition and cannons.
  • However, the Maratha army was commanded by a brilliant general named Mahadji Sindhia.
  • The Marathas utilised a scorched earth policy, burning farmland and poisoning wells to cut off the English from food and water supplies.
  • The English surrendered by mid-January 1779 and signed the Treaty of Wadgaon that forced the Bombay government to relinquish all territories acquired by the English since 1775.
  • Treaty of Salbai (1782): Warren Hastings, the Governor-General in Bengal, rejected the Treaty of Wadgaon and sent a large force who captured Ahmedabad, Bassein, Gwalior and defeated Sindhia. Sindhia proposed a new treaty between the Peshwa and the English, and the Treaty of Salbai was signed in May 1782; it was ratified by Hastings and Phadnavis. The treaty guaranteed peace between the two sides for twenty years.

Second Anglo Maratha War (1803-1805)

Cause of the War:

  • After Peshwa Madhavrao Narayan committed suicide in 1795, Bajirao II, son of Raghunathrao, became the Peshwa.
  • On April 1, 1801 he brutally murdered the brother of Yaswantrao Holkar (of Holkar dynasty of the Maratha Empire).

The War

  • A furious Yaswant Rao defeated the combined armies of Sindhia and Bajirao II. Terrified Bajirao II fled to Bassein where, on December 31, 1802, he signed a treaty with the English.
  • Treaty of Bassein It provisioned the English keeping troops permanently in Maratha territory. The treaty would become the "death knell of the Maratha Empire".

Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817-1819)

Cause of the War

  • The Pindaris, were a group of mercenaries attached to Maratha armies. When the Marathas became weak, the Pindaris could not get regular employment. As a consequence, they started plundering neighbouring territories, including those of the Company.
  • Lord Hasting's actions taken against the Pindaris were seen as a transgression of the sovereignty of the Marathas; they served to once again unite the Maratha confederacy.
  • Bajirao II made a last bid in 1817 by rallying together the Maratha chiefs against the English in course of the Third Anglo-Maratha War.

The War

  • The political and administrative conditions of all the Maratha states were confused and inefficient. Though they won some initial battles, the English won the war.
  • Maratha confederacy was dissolved. The peshwaship was abolished.
  • In the end, it can be concluded that the English attacked a ‘divided house’ which started crumbling after a few pushes.

Anglo-Sikh Wars

First Anglo-Sikh War (1845-46)

Context

  • The Sikh kingdom of Punjab was expanded and consolidated by Maharajah Ranjit Singh during the early years of the nineteenth century.
  • Ranjit Singh maintained a policy of wary friendship with the British, ceding some territory south of the Sutlej River, while at the same time building up his military forces both to deter aggression by the British and to wage war against the Afghans.
  • Sultej River acted as a line of demarcation between the Sikhs and the English.
  • Ranjit Singh died in 1839. Almost immediately, his kingdom began to fall into disorder.
  • Immediately after the death of Ranjit Singh, the British East India Company had begun increasing its military strength in the regions adjacent to the Punjab. Their main concern was that the Sikh army, without strong leadership to restrain them, was a serious threat to British territories along the border. In 1843, the English conquered and annexed Sindh, to the south of the Punjab.
  • An East India Company army began marching towards Ferozepur (on the banks of the Sutlej River in Punjab). In response to the British move, the Sikh army began crossing the Sutlej on 11 December 1845.
  • The Sikhs claimed they were only moving into Sikh possessions on the east side of the river, but the move was regarded by the British as clearly hostile and they declared war.

Outcome of the war

  • In the end, sikhs were defeated by the British and the Treaty of Lahore was signed between them.
  • Treaty of Lahore: The valuable region (the Jullundur Doab) between the Beas River and Sutlej River was surrendered to the British. Gulab Singh, the ruler of Jammu, was allowed to acquire entire Jammu and Kashmir from the Sikh Empire by a large cash payment to the East India Company.
  • Treaty of Bhairowal: The Sikhs were not satisfied with the Treaty of Lahore over the issue of Kashmir, so they rebelled. In December, 1846, the Treaty of Bhairowal was signed.

Second Anglo-Sikh War (1848-49)

  • Although the Sikh Army was weakened by the first anglo-sikh war, resentment at British interference in the government led to the Second Anglo-Sikh War within three years.
  • Lord Dalhousie himself proceeded to Punjab. Dalhousie annexed Punjab. Sir John Lawrence became the first Commissioner of Punjab.

Extension of British Paramountcy Through Administrative Policy

  • The process of imperial expansion and consolidation of British paramountcy was carried on by the Company during the 1757-1857 period through a two-fold method:
    • policy of annexation by conquest or war and
    • policy of annexation by diplomacy and administrative mechanisms.
  • Major Indian powers like Bengal, Mysore, the Marathas and the Sikhs were defeated and subjugated by the Company mainly by waging wars against them.
  • In the case of many other powers, the British applied diplomatic and administrative policies. In this context, we may list following examples:
    • Warren Hastings’ ‘ring-fence’ policy
    • Wellesley’s system of ‘subsidiary alliance’
    • Dalhousie’s ‘doctrine of lapse’

The Policy of Ring-Fence by Warren Hasting

  • Lord Warren Hastings was the first ‘Governor General of Bengal’.
  • He followed a policy of ring-fence which aimed at creating buffer zones to defend the Company’s frontiers.
  • It was the policy of defence of their neighbours’ frontiers for safeguarding their own territories.
  • Thus the states brought under the ring-fence system were assured of military assistance against external aggression—but at their own expense. In other words, these allies were required to maintain subsidiary forces which were to be organised, equipped and commanded by the officers of the Company who, in turn, were to be paid by the rulers of these states.

Subsidiary alliance system by Lord Wellesley

  • Lord Wellesley was governor-general from 1798-1805.
  • Wellesley’s policy of subsidiary alliance was an extension of the ring-fence system which sought to reduce the Indian states into a position of dependence on the British government.
  • Under the system, the allying Indian state’s ruler was compelled to:
    • accept the permanent stationing of a British force within his territory and to pay a subsidy for its maintenance.
    • The Indian ruler had to agree to the posting of a British resident in his court.
    • The Indian ruler could not employ any European in his service without the prior consultation with the Company. Nor could he go to war or negotiate with any other Indian ruler without consulting the governor-general.
  • In return for all this, the British would defend the ruler from his enemies and adopt a policy of non-interference in the internal matters of the allied state.
  • The Indian rulers lost their independence by buying security. They were not free of interference from the British Resident. They lost much of their revenue, paying for the British troops. Also, the alliance made the Indian rulers weak and irresponsible; the subjects were exploited and it was practically impossible to depose the oppressive rulers as they were protected by the British.
  • States which Accepted Alliance were: the Nizam of Hyderabad (1798), the ruler of Mysore (1799), the ruler of Tanjore (1799), the Nawab of Awadh (1801), the Peshwa (December 1801), the Bhonsle Raja of Berar (1803), the Sindhia (1804), the Rajput states (1818). The Holkars were the last Maratha confederation to accept the Subsidiary Alliance in 1818.

Doctrine of Lapse by Dalhousie

  • The doctrine stated that the adopted son could be the heir to his foster father’s private property, but not the state; it was for the paramount power (the British) to decide whether to bestow the state on the adopted son or to annex it.
  • Though this policy is attributed to Lord Dalhousie (1848-56), he was not its originator. But he was the one who showed too much passion in enforcing the policy.
  • Annexed Lapsed States: Satara (1848), Jhansi, Awadh and Nagpur (1854). The other small states included Jaitpur (Bundelkhand), Sambhalpur (Orissa), and Baghat (Madhya Pradesh).
  • Dalhousie reign almost completed the process of expansion of British power in India, which began with the victory over Siraj-ud-daula at Plassey in 1757.
