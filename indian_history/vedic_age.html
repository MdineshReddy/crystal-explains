THE VEDIC AGE

The Vedic period or Vedic age is the period in the late Bronze Age and early Iron Age of the history of India.

Origins of the Aryans

  • The location of the original homeland of the Aryans is still controversial, but the most accepted theory is that they migrated from Central Asia in several groups between 2000-1500 BCE and settled in Eastern Pakistan and India.
  • This region is popularly known as the land of seven rivers or ‘Sapta Sindhu’ which include the Indus, its five tributaries i.e., Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas and Sutlej and the Saraswati (identified as the modern-day Ghaggar-Hakra river).

Rigvedic or Early Vedic Period (1500-1000 BC)

  • Early Vedic people lived in the region of Sapta Sindhu.
  • Aryans came into conflict with the indigenous inhabitants called Dasas and Dasyus (earlier branch of Aryans). Dasyuhatya or slaughter of Dasyus is repeatedly mentioned in the Rig Veda.
Accounts of military conflicts inbetween the various tribes of Vedic Aryans are described in the Rigveda. Most notable of such conflicts was the Battle of Ten Kings (Dasrajan war), which took place on the banks of the river Parushni (modern day Ravi).

Polity and Society

  • Rig Vedic society was a tribal society and its people lived a seminomadic life. Aryan tribes were called Janas. Their chief (rajan) was known as the Gopati or Gopa (protector of cows), and the chief queen was called the Mahisi.
  • The society’s political structure was somewhat similar to the monarchial form, but the Gopati’s office was not hereditary and he was selected among the clan’s men.
  • Occupation was not based on birth. Members of a family could adopt different occupations.
  • Varna or color was the basis of initial differentiation between the Vedic and non-Vedic people. The only mention of the four varnas was found in the Purusashukta of the tenth Mandal of the Rig Veda, which makes one conclude that the varna system was probably introduced at the end of the Rig Vedic age. However, the Rig Vedic people were familiar with slavery.
  • Kula (Family) was the basic social unit and Kulapa was the head of the family. They followed the patrilineal system. The family was part of a larger grouping, called vis or clan. One or more than one clan made jana or tribe. The jana was the largest social unit. Kula → Vis → Jana.
  • There was no regular or standing army. Military technique was much advanced as they possessed chariots driven by horses.
  • Some important tribal assemblies of this period were Sabha, Samithi, Vidatha and Gana:
    • The two most important Assemblies were the Sabha and Samiti. Samiti was general in nature and less exclusive than Sabha.
    • Women attended Sabha and Vidhata in Rigvedic times

Economy

  • Rigvedic Aryans followed a mixed economy consisting of both agriculture and pastoralism.
  • Pastoralism was their primary occupation while agriculture was secondary.
  • Cow was the most important form of wealth. The term for war in the Rig Veda is Gavishthi or search for cows. The horse was almost as important as cow.
  • There was no regular system of taxation. People gave voluntary offerings to the king, called Bali.

Religion

  • There was neither temple nor idol worship in the Early Vedic age.
  • Important deities included:
God Assosciated Field
Indra/Purandara Breaker of Forts
Agni Fire God
Varuna Water God and upholder of natural order or Rta.
Soma God of plants. The ninth mandala of the Rig Veda i.e., ‘Soma Mandala’ is attributed to Soma.
Pushan The god of jungle paths, herdsmen, and cattle.
Usha Goddess of dawn
Aditi Goddess of eternity and the mother of the gods

Later Vedic Age (1000-500 BC) (Painted Grey Ware Phase)

Polity

  • Tribal assemblies lost their importance and royal power increased at their cost. Sabha and Samities continued to hold ground, though not like the Rig Vedic Age, but the vidatha completely disappeared. Women were no longer permitted to attend assemblies.
  • Formation of large kingdoms, Kingship became completely hereditary.
  • The term Rashtra indicating territory, first appeared in this period. The wars were no longer fought for cows, but for territories. It should be noted that even in Later Vedic times, the king did not possess a standing army and tribal units were mustered in times of war.
  • The king’s influence was further strengthened by ritual enactments such as the Rajasuya (which was royal consecration and conferred supreme power on the king), Vajapeya(a chariot race in which royal chariot was made to win against all kinsmen), and Aswamedha (unquestioned control over an area in which the royal horse ran uninterrupted).

Society

  • The fourfold division of society became clearer. Initially based on occupation, it later became hereditary.
    • Brahmana: The growing cult of sacrifice enormously added to the power of Brahmins.
    • Kshatriyas: They constituted the warrior class.
    • Vaisyas: The working class. They were the agriculturalists, cattle rearers, traders, artisans and metal workers.
    • Shudras: Lowest in the social hierarchy and considered to be born to serve the upper three varnas.
  • As compared to the Rig Vedic age, women lost importance in the society and there have been references to rare instances of sati and child marriage.

Economy

  • The Later Vedic culture is also called as PGW–Iron Phase culture, as a particular kind of pottery (painted grey ware) was used during that phase.
  • Agriculture became the chief economic activity.
  • The main factor in the expansion of the Aryan culture during the Later Vedic period was the beginning of the use of iron, which was introduced around 1000 BCE.
  • Unlike the Rig Vedic age where only voluntary offerings were taken and per se no revenue collection was done, in the Later Vedic age, collection of taxes and tributes was made mandatory and were done by Sangrihitri.

Religion

  • The Later Vedic culture was focused on the centrality of the cult of sacrifice. Sacrifice became more important and elaborate, and assumed both public and domestic character.
  • The two most important Rig Vedic Gods, Indra and Agni, lost their importance and instead of them, Prajapati (the creator) became supreme. Some of the other minor gods became prominent, such as Vishnu (conceived as the preserver and protector of people).
  • Idolatry appears in late Vedic phase and just as the society was divided in the later Vedic phase in terms of social classes, similarly some of the social orders too adopted their own deities. For example, Pushan (who was supposed to look after cattle) came to be regarded as the god of the Shudras.
  • However, towards the end of Later Vedic age, a strong reaction began to emerge against priestly domination, against cults and sacrifices. Around 600 BCE, the Upanishads were compiled.

Vedic Literature

The Vedic Literature is generally divided into Early Vedic and Later Vedic texts.

  • Early Vedic Literature/Rig Vedic Culture (c. 1500–1000 BCE): Includes the Rig Veda Samhita.
  • Later Vedic Literature/Later Vedic Culture (c. 1000–500 BCE): Includes Books 1, 8, 9, and 10 of the Rig Veda Samhita, the Samhitas of the Sama Veda, the Yajur and the Atharva Vedas and the Brahmanas, Aranyakas, and Upanishads attached to all the four Vedas.

Rig Veda

  • The Rig Veda is a collection of 1,028 hymns, divided into 10 Mandals (books). They are the earliest compositions and hence, they depict the life of the Early Vedic people in India.
    • Earliest Mandalas, i.e., II to VII are called family books as they are ascribed to particular families of seers/rishis.
    • The III mandala contains the Gayatri Mantra. which was compiled in the praise of Goddess Savitri.
    • Mandal IX – Compilation of Soma hymns.
    • Mandala X contains the Purusashukta which explains the origins of four varnas.

Sama Veda

  • The Sama Veda is the collection of verses mostly taken from the Rig Veda, but arranged in a poetic form to facilitate singing. It is a collection of 1,810 melodies, and also contains the famous Dhrupada raga, later sung by Tansen (Akbar's Court Musician).

Yajur Veda

  • The Yajur Veda deals with the procedure for the performance of sacrifices.

Atharva Veda

  • The Atharva Veda is a collection of magic spells and charms to ward off the evil spirits and diseases.

Upvedas

Each Veda is further assosciated with an Upveda:

  • Ayur Veda (Upaveda of Rig Veda): Deals with medicine.
  • Gandharvaveda (Upaveda of Sama Veda): Deals with art and music
  • Dhanurveda (Upaveda of Yajur Veda): Deals with art of warfare.
  • Shilpaveda/Arthaveda (Upaveda of Atharva Veda): Deals with craft and wealth.

Brahmanas

  • The Brahmanas describe the rules for the performance of sacrificial ceremonies. They explain the hymns of the Vedas in an orthodox (i.e., traditional) manner.

Aranyakas

  • They are called the ‘forest books’ as they were written mainly by hermits living in the jungles for their pupils. They deal with mysticism and philosophy and oppose sacrifice. They emphasize meditation and are considered to be the concluding portion of the Brahmanas, and interpret rituals in a philosophical way.

Upanishads

  • The literal meaning of ‘Upanishad’ is ‘to sit near someone’. There are 108 Upanishads. The Upanishads dwell on the ‘Atman’ and ‘Brahman’, and focus a lot on the philosophy about life, universe, self, body, sacrifice, etc. They emphasize that the knowledge of the self or atman should be acquired and that the relation of the atman with the Brahman should be properly understood.
  • The Mandukyopanishad is the largest of all Upanishads from which the phrase “Satyamev jayate ” (Truth Alone Triumphs) is taken.

Vedantas

  • Vedanta literally signifies the ‘end of the Vedas’, as they reveal the final aim of the Vedas. They condemn sacrifices and ceremonies and denote the last phase of the Vedic period.

Vedangas

The word ‘Vedanga’ means the ‘limbs of the Vedas’. They are the supplementary texts that aid in the proper recitation and understanding of the Vedas. There are six of them. They are:

  • Shiksha (Phonetics)
  • Kalpa (Ritualistic science)
  • Jyotisha (Astronomy)
  • Vyakarana (Grammar)
  • Nirukta (Etymology)
  • Chhanda (Metrics)

Puranas

  • The word ‘puranas’ literally means ‘old’. They were the religious sectarian literature of later times (around 4th 6th centuries), but have a well-defined perception of the past traditions. They reflect the Brahmanical social and religious values and also shed light on the emergence and development of Hindu religious practices. The contents of Puranas generally span of four ages/yugas (Satya, Treta, Dvapara, and Kali).
  • The Puranas are further divided into 18 Mahapuranas and numerous Upapuranas (secondary Puranas). Matsya Purana is the oldest.

Dharamshastra

  • They are the Sanskrit texts dealing specifically with Dharma (code of conduct that conceptually signifies ‘a righteous moral law’ which is in consonance with the universal natural law).
  • Manav Dharma Shastra or Manusmriti is the oldest and most famous. Manu is supposed to be the first king and law maker.

Epics

The two Sanskrit epics, the Mahabharata and the Ramayana, are part of both oral and written traditional history. The existing texts of these epics are believed to belong to the post-Vedic age, between c. 400 BCE and 400 CE.

Mahabharata

  • The Mahabharata is said to be composed by Ved Vyasa and consists of 18 Parvas (chapter) and has around 1 lakh verses. It is considerably longer than Ramayana. It is believed to be older than Ramayana.
  • Bhagavad Gita is extracted from Bhishma Parva of Mahabharata.

Ramayana

  • Ramayana is considered as the Adi-Kavya (first conscious poetry) composed by Valmiki and it consists of seven Kandas (books) having around 24,000 verses. It is much shorter than the Mahabharata, despite later additions.
