REFORM MOVEMENTS

  • The period from 19th century saw the emergence of socio-religious reforms. The adverse impact of british rule, religious superstitions among the masses, depressing Position of Women etc made the newly emerging educated middle class intellectuals to spread awareness and fight against the various socio-religious ills plaguing the Indian society.

Raja Rammohan Roy and the Brahmo Samaj

  • Raja Rammohan Roy (1772-1833), often regarded as the father of Indian Renaissance and the 'maker of Modern India' is a pioneer of socio-religious reform movements in modern India.
  • In 1814, he set up the Atmiya Sabha (or Society of Friends) in Calcutta to propagate the monotheistic ideals of the Vedanta and to campaign against idolatry, caste rigidities, meaningless rituals and other social ills.
  • He founded Brahmo Sabha in 1828 which was later renamed Brahmo Samaj. Through the Sabha he wanted to purify Hinduism and to preach monotheism based on the twin pillars of reason and the Vedas and Upanishads.
  • He protested againt the child marriage, polygamy, purdah system and female infacticide. He favored the remarriage of widows, female education and women’s right of Inheritance and property.
  • He started a Bengali weekly newspaper Samvad Kaumudi and edited a Persian weekly Mirat- ul- akbhar .
  • Rammohan was a determined crusader against the inhuman practice of sati. His efforts were rewarded by the Government Regulation in 1829 which declared the practice of sati a crime.
  • In 1817, he collaborated with David Hare to found the Hindu college (now Presidency college, Calcutta).

Young Bengal Movement and Henry Vivian Derozio

  • The Young Bengal was a group of Bengali free thinkers emerging from Hindu College, Calcutta.
  • They were also known as Derozians, after their teacher at Hindu College, Henry Louis Vivian Derozio. The Young Bengals were inspired and excited by the spirit of free thought and revolt against the existing social and religious structure of Hindu society. The Derozians also supported women’s rights and education.

Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar

  • The great scholar and reformer, Vidyasagar’s ideas were a happy blend of Indian and Western thought.
  • Vidyasagar started a movement in support of widow remarriage which resulted in legalisation of widow remarriage ( Widow Remarriage Act 1856). He was also a crusader against child marriage and polygamy.
  • As secretary of Bethune School (established in 1849), he was one of the pioneers of higher education for women in India.

Swami Dayanand Saraswathi and the Arya Samaj

  • Dayananda Saraswati founded the Arya Samaj, a reform movement of the Vedic dharma.
  • His society strove against idolatry, polytheism, rituals, priesthood, animal sacrifice, child marriage and the caste system. He worked towards reviving Vedic ideologies.
  • He was the first to give the call for Swaraj as "India for Indians" in 1876, a call later taken up by Lokmanya Tilak.
  • His vision of India included a classless and casteless society, a united India. He gave the slogan “Back to the Vedas”.

Jyotiba Phule and the Satyashodhak Samaj

  • Jyotirao was social reformer from Maharashtra.
  • He strived for eradication of untouchability, educating women and exploited caste people.
  • He and his wife, Savitribai Phule started their first school for girls in 1848 in Pune.
  • He, along with his followers, formed the Satyashodhak Samaj (Society of Truth Seekers) to attain equal rights for people from exploited castes.
  • Phule was awarded the title ‘Mahatma’ for his social reform work.

Ramakrishna Movement and Swami Vivekananda

  • The teachings of Ramakrishna Paramahamsa (1836-1886), a poor priest at the Kali temple in Dakshineshwar, on the outskirts of Calcutta found many followers.
  • He did not write books, but his conversations with people formed the basis of what were considered his teachings. But what he said was of universal relevance.
  • Two objectives of the Ramakrishna movement were—
    • to bring into existence a band of monks dedicated to a life of renunciation and practical spirituality, from among whom teachers and workers would be sent out to spread the universal message of Vedanta.
    • lay disciples were to carry on preaching, philanthropic and charitable works, looking upon all men, women and children, irrespective of caste, creed or colour.
  • Paramahamsa himself laid the foundations of the Ramakrishna Math with his young monastic disciples as a nucleus to fulfil the first objective.
  • The second objective was taken up by Swami Vivekananda after Ramakrishna’s death when he founded the Ramakrishna Mission in 1897.
  • Narendranath Datta (1862-1902), who later came to be known as Swami Vivekananda spread Ramakrishna’s message.
  • At the Parliament of Religions held at Chicago in 1893, Swami Vivekananda made a great impression on people by his learned interpretations.
  • In 1897 he founded the Ramakrishna Mission dedicated for humanitarian relief and social work.
  • The headquarters of the Ramakrishna Math and Mission are at Belur near Calcutta.

Theosophical Society

  • The Theosophical society was founded in New York (later shifted to Madras) in 1875 by Madam H.P. Blavatsky (Russian) , and Colonel Henry Steel Olcott (American).
  • In India, the movement became somewhat popular with the election of Annie Besant (1847-1933) as its president after the death of Olcott in 1907.
  • The society believed that a special relationship could be established between a person’s soul and God by contemplation, prayer, revelation, etc.
  • It accepted the Hindu beliefs in reincarnation and karma, and drew inspiration from the philosophy of the Upanishads and samkhya, yoga and Vedanta schools of thought.
  • It aimed to work for universal brotherhood of humanity without distinction of race, creed, sex, caste or colour.

Sir Sayyid Ahmed Khan and the Aligarh Movement

  • The Aligarh movement was started by Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (1817-98).
  • The movement aimed at spreading (i) modern education among Indian Muslims without weakening their allegiance to Islam; (ii) social reforms among Muslims relating to purdah, polygamy, widow remarriage, women’s education, slavery, divorce, etc.
  • He wanted to reconcile Western scientific education with the teachings of the Quran which were to be interpreted in the light of contemporary rationalism and science.
  • In 1875, he founded a modern school at Aligarh to promote English education among the Muslim. This had later grown into the Mohammadan Anglo Oriental college and then into the Aligarh Muslim University.

Deoband Movement

  • This was started in 1867 in Deoband in UP by theologians, Muhammad Qasim Nanotavi and Rashid Ahmad Gangohi.
  • In contrast to the Aligarh Movement, which aimed at the welfare of Muslims through Western education, the aim of the Deoband Movement was moral and religious regeneration of the Muslim community.

Self-Respect Movement by E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker

  • This movement was started by E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker (or Periyar Ramaswamy) in the mid-1920s in Tamil Nadu. He is known as the 'Father of the Dravidian movement'.
  • He rebelled against Brahminical dominance and gender and caste inequality in Tamil Nadu. He deleted the caste title Naicker from his name.
  • His movement aimed at rejection of the brahminical religion and culture which he felt was the prime instrument of exploitation of the lower castes.
  • E.V. Ramasamy promoted the principles of rationalism, self-respect, women’s rights and eradication of caste. He opposed the exploitation and marginalisation of the non-Brahmin Dravidian people of South India and the imposition of what he considered Indo-Aryan India.
  • A number of political parties in Tamil Nadu, such as Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) and All India Anna Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (AIADMK) owe their ideological origins to the Self-respect movement.

Temple Entry Movement by S Narayana Guru

  • Narayana Guru was a social reformer from Kerala whose family belonged to Ezhava caste.
  • He led a reform movement against the injustice in the then caste-ridden society of Kerala in order to promote spiritual enlightenment and social equality.
  • In 1888, he visited Aruvippuram (in Thiruvananthapuram) where he meditated for a while and during his stay there, he consecrated a piece of rock taken from the river, as the idol of Shiva, which has since become the Aruvippuram Shiva Temple. The upper caste Brahmins questioned Guru's right to consecrate the idol. He replied to them "This is not a Brahmin Shiva but an Ezhava Shiva" . This became a famous quote, used against casteism.
