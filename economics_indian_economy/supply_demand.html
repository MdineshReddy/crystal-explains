Supply and Demand

Probably the most important concept of microeconomics, supply and demand is a theory that explains the interaction between the sellers of a resource and the buyers for that resource.

Let's use the market for strawberries to help us understand this concept. On the Y axis, we have the price of strawberries, On the X axis we have the quantity of boxes of strawberries.

Let's start by looking at buyers and how they respond to a change in price. If the price goes up for strawberries, then some buyers will go buy some other fruit. And if the price goes down for strawberries, then people are going to buy more. This is called the law of demand: when the price goes up, people buy less, when the price goes down, people buy more. On the graph it's shown by a downward sloping demand curve.

Now let's think about sellers like the farmer in the farmer's market. If the price of strawberries goes up, then that farmer will make more profit, so he will have an incentive to produce more strawberries. If the price goes down then he's not going to produce strawberries. That's called the law of supply, and on the graph, it's shown by an upward sloping supply curve.

Surplus, Shortage and Equilibrium

If the price is really high then producers would like to produce a lot of strawberries, but consumers won't want to buy them. This mismatch is called a surplus. If the price goes down for strawberries, then buyers want to buy a whole lot, but producers won't have incentive and they'll produce very little. At the end you have mismatch, but this one's called a shortage. There's only one price where the quantity that buyers want to buy is exactly equal to the quantity that sellers want to sell, and it's when supply equals demand. The price is called the equilibrium price, and the quantity is called the equilibrium quantity.

Effect of External Factors

The above graph makes sense in normal times, but the price of strawberries isn't always constant; sometimes it goes up and sometimes it goes down. In fact, the prices for all sort of stuff change all the time. External forces can shift both the supply and demand curves, changing the equilibrium price and quantity.

For example, let's assume that the above graphs show the demand and supply of strawberries in the summer. What happens in the winter? Colder temperatures make it harder to grow strawberries. The result is the entire supply curve is going to shift to the left. This is because at all possible prices, there'd be fewer strawberries produced.

Supply and demand model apply to analyzing all sorts of stuff. In fact, let's look at a market for a commodity known for its volatility because of its fluctuating prices: gasoline. When you see gas prices are moving all over the board, that's just demand and supply. For example, in 2014, the retail gas price fell dramatically. Why? Well, it was demand and supply. The economies of both Europe and China weakened, which decreased the demand for gasoline, shifting the demand curve to the left. At the same time, new fracking technology and restored production of oil in Iraq and Libya caused the supply of gasoline to increase, or shift to the right. The combination drove gas prices down by more than 40% per gallon.

Conclusion

That's it. This graph is just a tool that economists use to show the results of a change in a market. Even though supply and demand behave in a predictable way that we've seen in the models, we can't lose sight of the fact that both of them are reliant on humans acting as buyers and sellers. Our actions influence supply and demand in a way that is more often unpredictable.
