Growth and Development

Economic Growth

Economic growth is a quantitative concept. In simple terms, economic growth is the increase in the monetary value of the goods and services produced by an economy over time. A common economic index for measurement of growth of a nationâ€™s economy is the Gross Domestic Product (GDP).

Growth is a value neutral term i.e.; it might be positive or negative for an economy over a period of time. But generally, we use it in positive sense. Increasing growth of an economy signifies wellbeing of that economy.

When the output of economy is increasing, but the rate of increase is decreasing over previous quarter/half year/year, then it is referred to as 'growth rate is slowing down'. Output declining over same reference period is referred to as contraction whereas continuous periods of contraction over two quarters (six months) is known as recession and still longer periods of continuous recession is known as Depression.

Economic growth is useful not only in national level economic analysis and policy-making, but also highly useful in the study of comparative economics. International financial and commercial institutions go for policy-making and future financial planning on the basis of growth rate data available for the economies of world.

Increased growth means increased output and increased income for factors of production which sets off a virtuous cycle of growth in an economy.

Economic Development

Economic development is a much broader term which indicates how the output of an economy is distributed among its people. India's GDP is over six times larger than the GDP of Singapore, but that doesn't mean the average Indian is richer than the average Singaporean. That's because India has 240 times more people than Singapore.

So, economists look at something called GDP per capita, to determine how wealthy a country is. GDP per capita is the GDP of the country divided by its population. It represents output per person, and a country with a high GDP per capita is considered rich. Even GDP per capita is not an exhaustive measurement, since it does not account for concentration of wealth in few individuals. But it is the best measurement we have got for now.

Of course, some of you may say being rich has nothing to do with GDP or money. It has to do with whether or not you're happy. Fine, money may not buy happiness, but it can prevent a lot of misery. The United Nations' Human Development Index, or HDI, measures life expectancy, literacy, education, quality of life, and it ranks countries according to their findings. The data shows the country that have a high GDP per capita have far less infant mortality, poverty and preventable diseases.

So, economists often used GDP per capita to measure a country's standard of living. Countries with the lowest standard of living are the ones that are conventionally considered poor.

Gross National Happiness

Bhutan, our neighbouring Himalayan kingdom and an economic non-entity, developed a new concept of assessing development in the early 1970sâ€”the Gross National Happiness (GNH). Without rejecting the idea of human development propounded by UNDP, the kingdom has been officially following the targets set by the GNH. Bhutan has been following the GNH since 1972 which has the following parameters to attain happiness/development:

  • Higher real per capita income
  • Good governance
  • Environmental protection
  • Cultural promotion (i.e.., inculcation of ethical and spiritual values in life without which, it says, progress may become a curse rather than a blessing)

Growth vs Development

Earlier, it was believed that increased growth would automatically lead to betterment of lives of people. But in case of India, it has not worked due to various reasons.

In the absence of development, the masses are to be guaranteed with a basic minimum level of quality life based upon the entitlement or welfare approach. For this, a minimum level of income has to be generated from productive activities by expanding the industries and services sectors. Thus, while there can be growth without development, development without growth is unconceivable.

Amartya Sen believes that India should invest more in its social infrastructure to boost the productivity of its people and thereby, raise growth. He said that without investing in health and education to improve human capabilities, inequality will widen and the growth process itself will falter. Senâ€™s approach has also been referred to as the capability approach. Sen had upheld what he calls the Kerala Experience where high social spending has resulted in growth, as a role model for other states to follow.

Jagdish Bhagwati on the other hand, argues that only increased growth can yield adequate resources for investing in human welfare. Growth may initially raise inequality but sustained growth will eventually raise enough resources for the state to redistribute and mitigate the effects of the initial inequality. He endorses the Gujarat Model as a metaphor for a primarily growth and private entrepreneurship driven development and the Kerala Model for a primarily redistribution and state-driven development.
