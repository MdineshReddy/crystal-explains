International Trade and Exchange Rates

International trade is the lifeblood of the global economy. Basically, when a good or service is produced in, let's say South Korea, and sold to a person or business, say in India, that counts as an export for South Korea and an import for India.

The annual difference between a country's exports and imports is called net exports. So, if South Korea exports 250 billion dollars' worth of goods and imports 200 billion, then its net exports are 50 billion. That means South Korea has a trade surplus. In case, if the net exports were negative, then its called a trade deficit.

Trade Deficit and Impact of International Trade

Some people assume that having a trade deficit is inherently bad. Like for example, U.S. import nearly all of its clothing. U.S. producers could easily make more than enough clothing, but they don't because they focus on other things that they're better at producing. The U.S. buys its clothes from other countries because it's cheaper. This is the value of international trade. It doesn't make sense to make everything on your own if you can trade with other countries that have a comparative advantage.

It can seem like exporting would make a country wealthy, while importing would make it poor. After all, if we buy products produced in other countries, then we're shipping jobs overseas, right? Well, only to an extent. Imagine you had the choice of buying a TV made in India or a TV made in China. Because of lower labor costs in China, the imported TV costs ₹ 10000 less than the Indian made one. So, you buy the imported TV (not considering the patriotism factor). That might cost jobs at a TV factory in the India., but you saved ₹ 10000 by buying the imported TV. You will be spending that ₹ 10000 on something you couldn't have afforded if you bought the Indian TV. Like taking your family to a nice restaurant. That creates jobs in those industries that wouldn't have existed if you would've bought the more expensive TV.

Economic theory suggests that international trade reshuffles jobs from one sector of the economy to another. Like from the TV factory to the restaurant. But the quality of these jobs can be markedly different. The guy assembling TVs at the Indian factory was probably making a lot more at his manufacturing job before he got reshuffled to a restaurant job. Which is just to say that all this is really complicated and what is good in the aggregate is not necessarily good for individuals.

The debate over the value of specific trade agreements continues, but it's unlikely that the world's largest economies will return to strict protectionism. Protectionist policy, like placing high tariffs on imports and limiting the number of foreign goods, usually hurts an economy more than it helps.

There are now several organizations designed to eradicate protectionism, most notably the World Trade Organization, or WTO. The WTO has been effective in getting countries to agree to specific rules and help settle disputes. But it's also been accused of favoring rich countries and not doing enough to help the environment or workers.

Trade between countries depends on the demand for a country's goods, political stability and interest rates. But one of the most important factors is exchange rates. Basically, this is how much your currency is worth when you trade it for another country's currency.

Currency Exchange

Suppose the U.S./India exchange rate is 70 rupees to the dollar. If an American is on vacation in India and wants to buy some sunscreen that costs 280 rupees, they'll have to trade 4 dollars for rupees. Likewise, if someone from India is on vacation in the U.S. and wants to buy a 20-dollar t-shirt, she will have to exchange 1400 rupees for dollars.

Now, let's think about what happens if the exchange rate goes up to 140 rupees per dollar. Now to buy that 280 rupees sunscreen in India, it'll cost that American two dollars instead of four. We say that the dollar has appreciated. At the same time, the Indian tourist who wants to buy the 20-dollar t-shirt will need 2,800 rupees instead of 1400. In this case the rupee depreciated.

It works the same way with imports and exports. When the rupees depreciates, foreign imports get more expensive, which makes them fall and Indian exports to other countries gets cheaper, which means they rise.

On the other hand, what if the exchange rate fell to 35 rupees per dollar? Now to buy that sunscreen, the American tourist needs 8 dollars. Each dollar has gotten less powerful i.e., depreciated. At the same time, the Indian tourist who wants to buy the 20-dollar t-shirt needs only 700 rupees. In this case rupee appreciated.

So, when the rupee appreciates, it gets cheaper for Indian consumers to import foreign goods and Indian exports to other countries get more expensive. Indian imports rise and exports fall.

Most currencies like the rupee and the dollar have floating exchange rates that change based on supply and demand. When the U.S. imports more products from India, they exchange dollars for rupees. This will increase the demand for rupees, and the rupee will appreciate. At the same time, the dollar will depreciate.

Some countries have elected to peg their currency to another currency. This is when a country's central bank wants to keep the exchange rate in a certain range. And they buy or sell currencies to keep it in that range. The Chinese government was well known for buying U.S. dollars to keep the Chinese currency artificially depreciated. When the U.S. imported goods from China, the yuan would appreciate. Then the Chinese government would turn around and buy dollars, which kept the exchange rate about the same. This kept Chinese exports cheap for Americans.

Balance of Payments

Up to this point, we focused on importing and exporting goods and services, but there's a whole other side of international trade that involves financial assets. Let's look at something called the balance of payments.

Every country keeps an accounting statement called the balance of payments that records all international transaction. It's made up of two sub accounts:

  • the current account and
  • the financial account, sometimes called the capital account .

The current account records the sale and purchase of goods and services, investment income earned abroad and other transfers like donations and foreign aid. So, when the U.S. buys 50 billion dollars of computers from China, that's recorded in the U.S. current account. This is a simplification, but when Americans spend money on Chinese goods, the people in China, in theory, have only two things they can do with that money. They can buy U.S. goods or they can buy U.S. financial assets like stocks and bonds. These transactions are recorded in the other sub account: the financial account.

Conclusion

International trade, like everything else in economics, is about trade-offs and choices and winners and losers. In purely economic terms, trade deficits and surpluses are the result of people and nations seeking their own self-interest. But while everyone is acting in a self-interested way, international trade doesn't always meet our individual interests. What may be good for the wider global economy might be really bad for you or your hometown. But in the aggregate, trade does improve the global standard of living. It's just sometimes hard to see up close.
